1. Scope & intent

This supplement explains how to treat the same diagnostics differently in silver. It does not redefine the core signals. It adds silver-specific priors so an interpreter can: (a) appropriately weight volatility and mechanical squeeze dynamics, and (b) handle the more frequent overshoot/mean‑reversion behaviour observed in silver.

  • Core remains unchanged: Net %OI, z‑score stretch, 4‑week flow, reversal score, regime flags.
  • Silver supplement adds: volatility priors, squeeze/exhaustion emphasis, and liquidity/noise handling.

2. Silver market structure: what makes it different

2.1 Silver is smaller and more volatile than gold

  • Silver tends to display higher beta to risk-on/off and larger percentage swings.
  • Positioning extremes more frequently translate into fat‑tail outcomes (sharp reversals and air pockets).

2.2 Futures positioning can be “tighter” and more episodic

  • Liquidity is lower than gold; weekly positioning shifts can be more discontinuous.
  • Large traders can materially shift net positioning over short windows; treat one-week moves cautiously.

2.3 Silver has a stronger squeeze narrative component

  • Short-squeeze risk is more salient in silver than in most macro commodities.
  • Squeeze regimes should be treated as convexity flags (asymmetric upside), not as timing triggers.

3. Mapping CoT categories to silver participants

Silver’s CoT categories often reflect a mix of physical hedging and bullion-bank intermediation. Interpretation should be similar to gold, but with greater emphasis on the mechanics of short-covering.

3.1 Practical mapping (interpretive)

  • Speculators (managed money / non-commercials): often momentum + macro expression; can become crowded quickly.
  • Commercials: producers/merchants and bullion-bank hedging; may be structurally net short.
  • Swap dealers (if used): treat as intermediation/OTC hedging; may amplify positioning swings.

Interpretation bias

In silver, extreme speculative shorts can be particularly important because the unwind can be fast. Use spec stretch + flow to identify when squeeze risk becomes dominant.

4. Structural baselines and “normal” bias

Silver has persistent structural tendencies similar to other metals, but with higher variability. These are priors to prevent misclassification.

4.1 Commercial net short is often normal

  • Commercials are frequently net short due to hedging and intermediation.
  • Interpret commercial shorts via Hedger_zscore and historical percentiles, not absolute sign.

4.2 Spec net long is often normal — but extremes matter more

  • Speculators are often net long; focus on stretch and flow.
  • Because volatility is higher, the penalty for chasing crowded longs is larger.

5. Silver-specific interpretation adjustments

5.1 Upgrade the role of squeeze/exhaustion regimes

  • In silver, treat crowding flags as stronger warnings because unwind dynamics can be violent.
  • When |Spec_zscore| ≥ 2, assume tail-risk is elevated and outcomes are more non-linear than in gold.

5.2 Use flow for timing more aggressively (within limits)

  • Because silver moves fast, flow turns can precede price turns by only a short window.
  • Interpret one-week flow changes cautiously, but when a multi-week flow trend flips, upgrade reversal setup quality.

5.3 Elevate “convexity” language at extremes

  • At extreme speculative shorts, emphasize upside skew and short‑covering convexity.
  • At extreme speculative longs, emphasize air‑pocket risk and liquidation cascades.

5.4 Noise and liquidity handling

  • Silver’s lower liquidity can make z-scores and flows noisier; prefer confirmation from:
  • — multi-week persistence (not single-week prints)
  • — divergence patterns (if price overlay is available)
  • — alignment of multiple flags (crowding + reversal score + hedger extreme)

6. Regime flag nuances in silver

6.1 Crowded Long (silver)

  • Interpret as: elevated fragility + higher probability of sharp drawdowns.
  • Upgrade to “exhaustion setup” when flows fade or reverse.

6.2 Crowded Short / Squeeze Risk (silver)

  • Interpret as: strong convex upside risk; squeezes can be fast.
  • Upgrade squeeze risk when: spec shorts are extreme and flows show short-covering (less short) or stabilisation.

6.3 Exhaustion Risk (silver)

  • Most credible when: crowded long + flow fade + price momentum stalls (if available).
  • Treat as a strong risk-management cue: reduce confidence in linear upside paths.

6.4 Reversal Score / Positioning Tension

  • In silver, reversal-score spikes tend to map more directly to tactical opportunities, but timing remains uncertain.
  • Highest-quality setups: extreme stretch + flow turn + aligned crowding/squeeze/exhaustion flags.

7. Output language templates for silver

7.1 Neutral / trend-supported

“Silver positioning is directionally aligned without extreme stretch; recent flows are supportive. Volatility is normal, and trend persistence remains plausible absent crowding flags.”

7.2 Crowded long (fragile continuation)

“Silver is stretched to the long side, implying elevated fragility. In silver, crowded regimes tend to amplify drawdown sensitivity; continuation is possible but path risk is high.”

7.3 Squeeze‑skewed setup

“Silver is stretched to the short side, creating convex upside risk via short‑covering. If flows confirm shorts are reducing, squeeze‑skew dominates near‑term outcomes with elevated volatility.”

7.4 High reversal setup

“Composite positioning tension is elevated and flows are turning, consistent with a higher‑quality reversal setup. Given silver’s volatility, expected paths are non‑linear and tail‑risk is elevated.”

8. Quick checklist (LLM‑friendly)

  1. Baseline: commercials net short and specs net long can be normal in silver.
  2. Stretch: if |Spec_zscore| ≥ 2, assume fragility and tail-risk are elevated.
  3. Flow: prioritize multi-week flow turns; treat one-week prints as noisy.
  4. Squeeze/exhaustion: treat these flags as higher-impact in silver than in gold.
  5. Divergence: if available, use price/positioning divergence to confirm setups.
  6. Language: emphasise skew, convexity, and non-linear paths at extremes.

Note: this supplement is intentionally concise and interpretive. Market-specific thresholds, instrument choices, and tactical decision rules should be layered separately.
