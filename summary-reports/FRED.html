M2 Money Supply Signal Methodology

This signal analyzes the M2 Money Supply (M2SL), a broad measure of money in circulation, which serves as a critical indicator of monetary policy and its potential impact on asset prices. An expanding money supply is typically indicative of a looser monetary policy, which can be a tailwind for various asset classes.

The signal is based on two primary calculations to capture both the long-term trend and short-term momentum:

  1. Year-over-Year (YoY) Percentage Change: This measures the annual growth rate of the M2 money supply.
  2. 3-Month Percentage Change: This serves as a momentum indicator, highlighting any recent acceleration or deceleration in money supply growth.

A signal is then generated based on the following criteria:

  • Bullish: High or accelerating M2 growth suggests a supportive environment for equities due to lower borrowing costs, and for precious metals as a hedge against potential currency debasement.
  • Bearish: Low or negative M2 growth indicates a tightening of monetary conditions, which can be a headwind for equities and precious metals.
  • Neutral: Moderate and stable M2 growth that does not meet the bullish or bearish thresholds.

US Treasury Yield Curve Slope Signal Methodology

The Treasury Yield Curve Slope Signal is a contrarian indicator that provides insight into economic expectations and their potential impact on various asset classes. It is based on the spread between the 10-Year Treasury Constant Maturity Rate (GS10) and the Federal Funds Effective Rate (FEDFUNDS).

The shape of the yield curve is a powerful predictor of economic activity:

  • A steepening curve (widening spread) often signals economic recovery and potential inflation, which can be bullish for equities and industrial metals.
  • An inverting or flat curve (narrowing or negative spread) typically precedes an economic slowdown, which is generally bearish for equities and industrial metals but can be bullish for precious metals as investors seek safe-haven assets.

The signal is generated based on both the level of the spread and its trend, calculated using a 3-month moving average:

  • Bullish: An inverted yield curve ( 0%) signals a potential economic downturn, which is bullish for precious metals.
  • Bearish: A wide spread (> 1.5%) or a steepening curve suggests economic expansion, which can be bearish for precious metals but positive for equities and industrial metals.
  • Neutral: A moderate and stable spread.

US CPI vs PPI Divergence Inflation Signal Methodology

This signal measures inflation pressure by combining headline CPI (CPIAUCSL) with Producer Prices (PPIACO) to detect leading/lagging divergence. It is calculated monthly and smoothed for robustness.

Data & processing:

  • Monthly values resampled to monthend and converted to YoY % changes: CPI_YoY and PPI_YoY.
  • Divergence = PPI_YoY - CPI_YoY. Shortterm momentum is measured as the 3month change of the divergence.
  • Robust rolling zscore (median/MAD) of the divergence over a 60month window yields divergence_z to reduce sensitivity to outliers.

Signal rules (composite level + divergence z):

  • HOT (inflation pressure): CPI elevated ( 3.0% YoY) AND PPI leading CPI (divergence_z 0.75), OR divergence_z 1.0.
  • COOL (disinflation): CPI low ( 1.5% YoY) AND PPI lagging CPI (divergence_z 0.5), OR divergence_z 1.0.
  • NEUTRAL: All other states (insufficient data also mapped to Neutral).

Output columns: CPI_YoY, PPI_YoY, PPI_minus_CPI_YoY, divergence_z, CPI_PPI_Inflation_Signal.

Practical notes:

  • Use the 60month robust z for regime conviction; treat single high z values as warnings until confirmed by level (CPI) or momentum.
  • Watch the 3month divergence change for early turn signals; large positive divergence often precedes higher consumer inflation.
  • Implications: HOT tends to be supportive for inflationsensitive assets (precious metals) and can be a headwind for real incomes and some equities; COOL favours disinflationary asset regimes.

Federal Reserve Liquidity Composite Methodology

This composite captures the Fed's liquidity stance by blending balance-sheet and money-aggregate channels. It combines level (YoY) and short-term momentum (3month) from multiple series to produce a single, interpretable liquidity indicator.

Data & processing (stepbystep):

  • Series: M2 Money Supply (M2SL), Fed Total Assets (WALCL), Reserve Balances (WRESBAL), ON RRP usage (RRPONTSYD).
  • Frequency: Align all series to monthend (last observation per month) before calculating transforms.
  • Transforms:
    • YoY = 12month % change (value.pct_change(12) * 100) captures the level/trend.
    • 3M = 3month % change (value.pct_change(3) * 100) captures recent impulse/acceleration.
  • RRP inversion: ON RRP is a liquiditysoaking instrument higher RRP = tighter liquidity. We therefore invert RRP signs (multiply YoY and 3M by 1) before classification and zscoring so falling RRP easing (positive) and rising RRP tightening (negative).

Individual signal (per-series) rules:

  • M2_Signal2: classifies M2 using symmetric thresholds on YoY and 3M impulse:
    • Bullish (easing) if M2_YoY > +6% OR M2_3M > +1% (level high or accelerating).
    • Bearish (tightening) if M2_YoY < +2% OR M2_3M < 1% (level weak or decelerating).
    • Neutral otherwise or if insufficient data.
  • FED_Signal (WALCL): similar classification but with thresholds tuned for balancesheet magnitudes (e.g. YoY high +5%, low +0%, 3M accel +0.8%).
  • RES_Signal (WRESBAL): reserve balances can be more volatile so thresholds are wider (example: YoY high +8%, low +1%).
  • RRP_Signal (RRPONTSYD): use inverted YoY/3M (see above) and classify with RRPspecific thresholds (note: a negative YoY on raw RRP becomes positive after inversion and is treated as easing).
  • All classifiers return one of {'Bullish','Neutral','Bearish'} for that channel and are implemented to return 'Neutral' when either component is NaN.

Liquidity_Composite (numeric):

  • Standardization: For each month, compute zscores for the four channel components. By default the implementation uses a simple sample z: z = (s mean) / std (ddof=0). The code forms two z parts per channel (level = YoY, impulse = 3M).
  • Channel contribution: channel_score = z(level) + z(impulse) for RRP the inputs are inverted before z (z(RRP_YoY) + z(RRP_3M)).
  • Composite: Liquidity_Composite = mean(channel_scores across M2, FED, RES, RRP). Each channel is equally weighted in the current implementation (you can supply weights if desired).
  • Notes: zscores are sample standardized across the full available history; rows with many NaNs will produce NaN composite. Consider robust median/MAD z or rolling windows for outlier resistance.

Liquidity_Regime (discrete):

  • Expansion: Liquidity_Composite +0.75 (system liquidity easing).
  • Neutral: 0.75 < Liquidity_Composite < +0.75.
  • Contraction: Liquidity_Composite 0.75 (system liquidity tightening).

Output columns: Date, M2_YoY, M2_3M, FED_YoY, FED_3M, RES_YoY, RES_3M, RRP_YoY, RRP_3M, M2_Signal2, FED_Signal, RES_Signal, RRP_Signal, Liquidity_Composite, Liquidity_Regime.

Practical notes & tuning:

  • Thresholds shown are examples tune via backtests; consider different thresholds for recent regimes (postQE, QT, etc.).
  • RRP inversion is critical for correct sign interpretation of ON RRP moves.
  • Use rolling/robust zscores or outlier winsorization if single large balancesheet moves (ex: QE/Taper events) overly dominate the composite.
  • Consider freshness/ffill penalties and perchannel weights if you need to downgrade the composite when inputs are proxied or stale.
