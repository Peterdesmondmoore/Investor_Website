US Labour Market

The US Labour Market framework synthesises employment composition, labour tightness, wage dynamics, and worker flows to assess labour regime conditions. By combining hard employment data with sentiment and flow indicators, the model distinguishes resilience from cooling inflection dynamics. In this briefing, we evaluate labour's contribution to growth momentum and inflation persistence while surfacing deterioration thresholds.

As of early February, the U.S. labor market and consumer sentiment present a complex picture. We assess a Transitional / Fragile Balance regime emerging, characterized by increasing slack and cautious households. This dynamic reflects a rebalancing that warrants close monitoring.

The context

We observe the U.S. labor market in a state of fragile balance, carrying medium confidence in its stability. This environment is currently marked by a clear divergence in signals. While several key labor market indicators, including Employment Composition, Job Flows, Weekly Hours, Labor Market Tightness, and Sectoral Employment Diffusion, remain in a neutral regime, dominant drivers point to an underlying softening.

The current tensions arise from the persistence of these neutral readings against a backdrop of increasing stress in other areas. We note that the relatively stable aggregate employment metrics are being offset by more granular signs of weakening, suggesting that the broader picture may be masking emergent shifts.

The shift

This transitional phase is primarily driven by a noticeable increase in Underemployment Risk and a significant deterioration in Consumer Sentiment. We observe the Underemployment Risk signal has moved into a HOT regime, indicating a rise in broad labor market slack and longer job-finding durations. Concurrently, Consumer Sentiment has turned Bearish, reflecting a clear contraction in household optimism and, by extension, future demand.

These two signals carry high aggregation weights and strong conviction, overriding the stability seen in other indicators. We further note that while the Explicit Slack Gap signal remains neutral, its momentum is clearly widening, suggesting a trend toward greater slack. This shift is occurring despite the downweighting of signals like Labor Market Turnover Differential and Labour Market Momentum, which present mixed signals or lower conviction in the current environment.

The implications

The emerging fragile balance suggests potential for further rebalancing within the labor market, with risks skewed towards additional softening. We anticipate sustained pressure on household consumption as bearish consumer sentiment interacts with the observed increase in underemployment risk, potentially translating into reduced spending and investment.

The primary upside risk for this regime would be a sustained improvement in consumer confidence, alongside a notable reversal in underemployment trends, which could shift underlying momentum. Conversely, the downside risk involves a further acceleration in underemployment metrics and a clear shift of currently neutral signals towards "Cool" or "Slackening" regimes, indicating a more pronounced downturn.

We maintain a posture of careful monitoring, anticipating a continued fragile balance with emerging slack in the near term. Key signposts to watch for potential regime change include the Underemployment Risk signal consistently falling below its hot threshold, a rebound in Consumer Sentiment's level and positive momentum, and a reversal in the widening trend of the Explicit Slack Gap signal.
