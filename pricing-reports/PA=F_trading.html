Palladium Futures, often tracked by the continuous contract ticker PA=F, are standardized, legally binding financial agreements to buy or sell a specific quantity of palladium at a predetermined price on a future date. They are the primary tool used by large-scale producers, industrial consumers, and institutional speculators to manage price risk (hedging) or to profit from anticipated price movements.

The main purpose of the futures market is for price discovery and risk transfer. A car manufacturer might buy palladium futures to lock in the cost of a key component for their catalytic converters, while a mining company might sell futures to lock in a profitable price for its future production. Speculators provide the necessary liquidity by taking on the risk that these hedgers wish to offload.

Unlike a physically-backed instrument like an ETC or ETF, a futures contract is a derivative that does not represent ownership of the metal stored in a vault. It is an obligation. While contracts can be settled by the physical delivery of palladium, the vast majority are closed out (bought or sold back) before expiration, with the profit or loss settled in cash. The price reflects the market's expectation of palladium's value at a future point in time.
