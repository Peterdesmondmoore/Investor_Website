Core Investment Thesis

Structural deficit setup

The core investment thesis is that a powerful long-term structural bull market in copper is currently being obscured by near-term macroeconomic and geopolitical volatility. This dynamic creates a compelling entry point for investors targeting a 3-7 year horizon.

High-Level Strategy & Rationale

  • Structural demand supercycle: Copper demand is anchored by the green energy transition (EVs, renewables, grid buildout) and amplified by the rapid expansion of AI and data centre infrastructure.
  • Deepening supply deficit: Years of underinvestment, falling ore grades and rising disruption rates have left the market facing a chronic supply shortfall that tightens into the late 2020s.
  • Geopolitical asymmetry: Tariff policy and COMEX/LME pricing dynamics introduce a regional premium, especially supportive for US-linked production.

Near-term volatility driven by tariffs and China uncertainty masks this structural backdrop. The strategy is designed to lean into that volatility via staged deployment, while ensuring the portfolio remains robust across macro regimes.

Timelines & Execution

  • Deployment (2025-2026): Capital is deployed gradually through dollar-cost averaging, using pullbacks linked to macro headlines (China, tariffs, growth scares) as opportunities to build core and tactical positions.
  • Super-cycle realisation (2027-2029): As the structural deficit tightens, higher incentive prices support sector-wide re-rating; quality producers capture strong cash flow growth and operating leverage.
  • Drawdown (2029-2032): Positions are progressively reduced as evidence emerges of a meaningful new supply wave funded and constructed, signalling late-cycle conditions.

Current phase (Jan 2026): Maintain core exposure while actively managing volatility; tactical adds are reserved for dislocations tied to tariff headlines or China macro surprises.

Scenario Framework & Market Logic

Regime-sensitive

The strategy uses three explicit scenarios - Bear, Neutral and Bull - each with defined assumptions, probabilities and price paths rebased to the current spot level.

Bear scenario - 10% probability (tail risk)

Assumptions: A severe global recession, a "hard landing" in China, or both, trigger a significant contraction in industrial and construction activity. This demand shock temporarily overwhelms the structural deficit, pushing copper prices down toward cautious forecasts (around US$9,000-10,000/t) before a slow, prolonged recovery.

Probability rationale: A tail risk driven not by a change in the long-term copper story but by an extreme macro event. A synchronized global recession or sharp Chinese slowdown would cause broad-based demand destruction and temporarily suppress prices despite the underlying deficit. Such a multi-year bear market is considered low probability.

Neutral scenario - 50% probability (base case)

Assumptions: Represents the consensus view. The structural supply/demand imbalance is the dominant force, moderated by periods of moderate growth and fluctuating Chinese demand. The market remains tight, supporting elevated prices, with copper holding in the US$11,000-13,000/t range and gradually trending higher as deficits deepen late in the decade.

Probability rationale: Anchored in the powerful, non-negotiable fundamentals of a supply deficit colliding with multi-decade demand from the energy transition and digitisation. Macro headwinds and higher price elasticity create volatility but do not derail the primary upward trend.

Bull scenario - 40% probability (significant upside)

Assumptions: The structural deficit becomes acute sooner than expected. Demand from energy transition and AI data centres accelerates exponentially, while supply disruptions become more frequent and few new large-scale projects reach production. The market experiences a severe squeeze, pushing prices toward US$14,000-15,500+/t as the incentive price threshold is exceeded.

Probability rationale: Reflects the potential for known structural drivers to overshoot. Faster-than-expected AI and EV adoption and/or persistent disruptions at major mines would rapidly transform a tight market into an outright shortage, creating conditions for a sharp price spike.

Key Risks & Intended Use by a Model

Macro & mining risk

The copper thesis is powerful but exposed to cyclical, geopolitical and project-level risks. This section preserves the original risk framing and adds a brief note on how a model should use the strategy page.

Key risks

Risk Channel
Global recession A severe downturn remains the most significant threat to industrial demand. While policy-driven "green" demand provides a more resilient floor than in past cycles, prices can still experience large drawdowns in a hard landing.
Mine-specific disruptions Labour strikes, political instability or operational failures can affect individual miners. The core ETF sleeve mitigates single-asset and single-country shocks by diversifying across many producers.
Chinese demand uncertainty As the largest consumer, a sharp slowdown or "hard landing" in China is a key risk. The thesis leans on the increasingly global nature of demand from the energy transition and AI, which progressively diversifies the demand base away from single-country dependence.
US tariff policy reversal An unexpected reversal of US tariffs would collapse the LME/COMEX price arbitrage, causing sharp short-term volatility. Over the full horizon, the structural deficit remains the primary driver, but path dependency matters for timing tactical adds and trims.

Intended use by a model

  • Map macro inputs (global growth, China indicators, AI/data centre capex, supply disruption rates) into the three scenarios and compute probability-weighted outcomes at the sleeve and asset level.
  • Use the scenario framework and return expectations as illustrative inputs, not forecasts, to stress-test the "Core + Tactical" structure and assess whether bands remain appropriate under different regimes.
  • Analyse relative risk/return between the diversified ETF and higher-beta producers, highlighting concentration and correlation risks.
  • Integrate the copper sleeve into a broader multi-asset portfolio framework, focusing on correlations with other commodities, equities and macro regimes.
  • Track key newsflow (tariffs, mine disruptions, China data) as triggers for scenario probability shifts and for adjusting tactical exposure within the bands.
