Federal Reserve Beige Book — Executive Summary

Publication Date - January 2026

Descriptive synthesis of current US economic conditions based on district-level reporting.

National Backdrop

Overall economic activity increased at a slight to modest pace in eight of the twelve Federal Reserve Districts, marking an improvement over the previous three reporting cycles (1). Three Districts reported no change in activity, while one observed a modest decline (2). Consumer spending was buoyed by the holiday season, though a notable bifurcation emerged as higher-income consumers increased spending on luxury goods and travel, while lower-income households became increasingly price-sensitive (3). Manufacturing activity remained varied, with nearly half of the Districts reporting growth and the other half reporting contraction (4). Banking conditions were generally stable, with some Districts reporting increased demand for credit cards and commercial lending (5).

Labour & Wages

  • Labour Demand: Stable; eight Districts reported no change in hiring levels, with many firms focusing on backfilling vacancies rather than creating new positions (6).
  • Hiring & Retention: Balanced; turnover has eased across several Districts, though shortages of skilled labour persist in specialized fields such as engineering, health care, and the trades (e.g., Cleveland, San Francisco) (1).
  • Wage Pressure: Moderating; wage growth has largely returned to "normal" or pre-pandemic levels, growing at a moderate pace across the majority of the country (7).

Prices & Input Costs

  • Consumer Prices: Moderating to moderate; prices rose at a moderate rate in most Districts, though retail and restaurant contacts noted difficulty passing costs to price-sensitive customers (8).
  • Input Costs: Increasing; cost pressures from tariffs were cited as a consistent theme across all Districts, particularly for metals, glass, and general merchandise (e.g., Cleveland, Richmond) (1).
  • Pass-Through: Mixed; firms are increasingly passing on tariff-related costs as pre-tariff inventories are depleted, though some manufacturers continue to absorb costs to preserve market share (2).

Sectoral Activity Snapshot

  • Consumer Spending: Increased slightly to modestly, driven by high-end experiential spending and luxury goods, while lower-end retail struggled with price fatigue (e.g., New York, Philadelphia) (3).
  • Manufacturing: Varied; some Districts saw gains in data center-related equipment and light vehicles, while others reported declines due to trade uncertainty and higher input costs (4).
  • Real Estate & Construction: Softened; residential sales and construction declined in the majority of Districts due to affordability and interest rate sensitivity, though data center construction remained a bright spot (9).
  • Banking & Credit: Stable or improving; loan volumes increased in some regions, particularly for credit cards and commercial real estate, despite tighter lending standards for business loans (5).
  • Energy / Resources: Flat to down; lower oil prices constrained drilling and capital investment in the Kansas City and Dallas Districts, though utility demand for data centers grew (1).
  • Transportation / Logistics: Flat to slightly down; a "freight recession" continued for trucking firms, though air cargo tonnage saw double-digit increases in some areas like Atlanta (1).

Regional Divergence

The Philadelphia District saw a rebound to slight growth following a previous decline, while the New York District reported a continued modest decline in activity, specifically in the service sector (1). The Dallas District reported steady activity buoyed by a "bright spot" in banking, contrasting with the Minneapolis District where employment decreased slightly and manufacturing contracted (2). San Francisco expanded modestly, supported by robust high-income holiday spending that offset softening manufacturing activity (3).

Forward-Looking Signals (Descriptive Only)

  • Firms express "cautious optimism" for the first half of 2026, citing reduced uncertainty regarding tariff policies and potential interest rate declines (2).
  • Investment plans are focused on efficiency and capacity, with increased interest in AI implementation for productivity rather than immediate workforce expansion (4).
  • Hiring plans are expected to increase slightly in the coming months, particularly in the manufacturing sector (e.g., Kansas City) (6).

Key Observations Summary

  • Overall US economic activity is expanding at a slight to modest pace, representing an improvement in broad momentum (2).
  • Labour markets are stable and balanced, with moderate wage growth and a focus on staff retention over expansion (6).
  • Price pressures remain moderate, with tariff-related input costs increasingly being passed through to consumers as inventories normalize (8).
  • Regional dispersion persists, with gains in the Mid-Atlantic and Southwest offsetting continued softness in the Northeast and Upper Midwest (1).
