Federal Reserve Beige Book — Executive Summary

Publication Date — November 2025

Descriptive synthesis of current US economic conditions based on district-level reporting.

National Backdrop

Economic activity was largely unchanged since the prior report, with most of the twelve Federal Reserve Districts indicating flat activity; two noted modest declines and one reported modest growth (1). Consumer spending declined further overall, though higher-end retail remained resilient (1). Manufacturing activity increased somewhat across most Districts, but tariffs and related uncertainty continued to be a headwind (1). Nonfinancial services revenues were mostly flat to down, and loan demand presented a mixed picture (1). Outlooks were generally unchanged, with some contacts anticipating slower activity while manufacturers expressed optimism (1).

Labour Market Conditions

Employment declined slightly, with about half of the Districts reporting weaker labor demand (1). Employers predominantly managed headcounts through hiring freezes, replacement-only hiring, and attrition rather than widespread layoffs (1). Several firms adjusted hours worked to align with business volume, and some noted that artificial intelligence was replacing entry-level positions or enhancing productivity, thereby reducing new hiring needs (1). Worker availability generally improved across most Districts, making it easier to find staff, though shortages persisted for certain skilled roles and immigrant workers (1). Wage growth remained modest overall, with some sectors like manufacturing and construction experiencing more moderate pressure due to tighter labor supply, while rising health insurance premiums continued to push up labor costs (1).

Prices & Cost Pressures

Prices rose moderately during the reporting period, with widespread input cost pressures in manufacturing and retail, largely attributed to tariffs (1). Beyond tariffs, several Districts, including Philadelphia and Cleveland, noted rising costs for insurance, utilities, technology, and health care (1). The ability to pass through these higher costs to customers varied significantly, influenced by demand strength, competitive pressures, and consumer price sensitivity, leading to reports of margin compression (1). Prices for certain materials declined due to sluggish demand or reduced tariffs, but firms generally anticipate ongoing upward cost pressures (1). Plans for near-term price increases were mixed, with some businesses holding steady due to competitive pressure and softer demand, while others intended to raise prices (1).

Sector Themes

  • Consumer activity: Overall consumer spending declined further, with resilience in higher-end retail but softening for low- and middle-income households who exhibited increased price sensitivity and caution with discretionary purchases (1). For instance, Boston reported flat consumer spending and New York noted slight declines, while Chicago observed a slight increase, particularly among higher-earning consumers (1). Auto sales saw declines in EV sales due to tax credit expiration and affordability concerns, as reported by Cleveland and St. Louis (1).
  • Manufacturing: Manufacturing activity increased somewhat across most Districts (1). New York reported moderate growth, and Chicago saw slight increases (1). However, tariffs and tariff-related uncertainty were consistent headwinds for manufacturers across multiple Districts, including Philadelphia, Dallas, and Richmond, leading to reduced new orders and planning difficulties (1). Demand related to AI data centers provided a boost for some manufacturers in Cleveland (1).
  • Services: Revenues in the nonfinancial services sector were mostly flat to down (1). Boston noted slight revenue growth, while New York and Philadelphia reported moderate declines (1). Professional and business services saw moderate growth in Cleveland and Chicago, partly driven by tax law changes and AI adoption (1). Travel and tourism activity was mixed, with some areas flat to slightly down, but with resilient higher-end segments (New York) and anticipated boosts from major events (Atlanta) (1).
  • Housing & construction: Residential construction activity declined in some Districts like Philadelphia and Atlanta, was unchanged in others (St. Louis), and saw renewed strength in home sales in Boston due to lower mortgage rates (1). Commercial real estate conditions improved somewhat overall, with ongoing recovery in office markets in Boston and New York, and increased demand for data center construction in Cleveland and Chicago (1). High construction costs, including labor shortages and tariffs, continued to deter development in Boston and Richmond (1).
  • Banking & credit: Loan demand was mixed overall (1). New York reported a decline in overall loan demand but an increase in residential mortgages, while Cleveland noted a moderate increase in loan demand driven by declining interest rates (1). Credit standards were mostly unchanged in New York and Kansas City, but some Districts like Philadelphia, Atlanta, and St. Louis reported tightening standards (1). Delinquencies improved for some loan types in New York but were reported as rising in Philadelphia, Cleveland, Richmond, and St. Louis, particularly among households and small businesses (1).
  • Agriculture & energy: Conditions in these sectors were largely stable, though some contacts cited challenges (1). Atlanta reported moderate growth in energy, with strong demand for primary energy sources tied to data center activity (1). Chicago noted a slight increase in 2025 farm income prospects, but Kansas City and Dallas reported subdued agricultural economies due to low crop prices, drought, and increased export uncertainty (1). Oil and gas activity declined slightly in Kansas City due to falling oil prices (1).

Regional Dispersion

  • Stronger districts: Chicago experienced slight increases in overall economic activity, consumer and business spending, construction, and manufacturing (1). Cleveland saw slight increases in business activity and professional and business services, with some manufacturers benefiting from AI data centers (1). Boston reported slight economic expansion, buoyed by renewed strength in home sales (1). San Francisco's financial sector strengthened marginally, with expanded loan demand from commercial borrowers (1).
  • Softer districts: New York reported a modest decline in economic activity and employment (1). Philadelphia was already trending down, with government shutdown impacts exacerbating declines across most sectors (1). St. Louis and Kansas City noted an unchanged or slightly slowed pace, characterized by a demand slowdown and cooling consumer activity (1). Dallas experienced slight weakening across nonfinancial services, retail, and banking (1).
  • Common constraints: Tariffs and tariff-related uncertainty were frequently cited headwinds impacting manufacturing costs, planning, and profitability across Districts including New York, Philadelphia, Cleveland, Richmond, Atlanta, Chicago, and Dallas (1). Consumer price sensitivity and reduced discretionary spending were widely reported (Boston, Cleveland, Atlanta, Minneapolis, Kansas City, San Francisco) (1). The government shutdown caused disruptions and added to economic uncertainty in Philadelphia, Kansas City, and Dallas (1). Rising insurance and healthcare costs were a notable labor cost pressure across several Districts (1).
  • Outliers: AI adoption was noted as impacting hiring for entry-level positions or boosting productivity in Boston, Cleveland, and San Francisco (1). Data center construction provided a unique boost to manufacturing (Cleveland) and energy demand (Atlanta, Kansas City) (1). Specific regional issues like a gubernatorial election affecting auto sales in New Jersey (Philadelphia) or the lingering effects of hurricanes on travel (Atlanta) highlighted unique conditions (1).

Signals to Watch

  • Growth risks: Contacts noted an increased risk of slower activity in coming months (National Summary), alongside persistent demand slowdowns in various sectors (St. Louis, Kansas City, Dallas) (1). Tariff concerns and general economic uncertainty were frequently cited as factors clouding outlooks (Dallas, Philadelphia) (1).
  • Labour inflections: The widespread adoption of hiring freezes, replacement-only hiring, and reliance on attrition rather than layoffs signals a shift in hiring behavior (National, Cleveland, Atlanta) (1). The increasing use of AI to replace entry-level roles or improve worker productivity (National, Boston, Cleveland, San Francisco) suggests a structural change in labor demand (1).
  • Inflation persistence: Persistent input cost pressures from tariffs, rising insurance premiums, utilities, and healthcare costs continue to drive moderate price increases (National, Philadelphia, Cleveland, Chicago, San Francisco) (1). The mixed ability and willingness of firms to pass these costs onto consumers, alongside consumer price sensitivity, highlights ongoing tension in pricing dynamics (1).
  • Capital investment: Despite a cautious tone, some manufacturers indicated optimism and plans to increase future capital expenditures, particularly in automation and equipment upgrades (Philadelphia, Chicago) (1). However, other commercial clients remained cautious about investing (Cleveland), or were delaying capex due to tight margins (Chicago agriculture) (1).
  • Financial stress: Increased demand for food and housing assistance by community organizations (New York, Atlanta, Dallas, San Francisco) signals rising household financial stress (1). Concurrently, reports of increasing delinquencies and defaults, especially among households and small businesses (Philadelphia, Cleveland, St. Louis, Dallas), along with tightening credit standards by lenders, point to early stress indicators in the credit environment (1).
