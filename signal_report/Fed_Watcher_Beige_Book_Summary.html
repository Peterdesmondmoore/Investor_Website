Federal Reserve Beige Book — Executive Summary

Publication Date — November 2025

Descriptive synthesis of current US economic conditions based on district-level reporting.

National Backdrop

Economic activity across most Federal Reserve Districts was little changed since the previous report, with two Districts noting a modest decline and one reporting modest growth (1). Overall consumer spending continued to decline, although higher-end retail spending remained resilient (1). Manufacturing activity generally increased somewhat, despite ongoing headwinds from tariffs and associated uncertainty (1). Revenues in the nonfinancial services sector were mostly flat to down, and residential construction saw declines in some areas while remaining unchanged in others (1). Conditions in agriculture and energy were largely stable, but low-price environments posed challenges for oil and certain crops (1).

Labour Market Conditions

Employment declined slightly across the nation, with approximately half of the Districts observing weaker labor demand (1). While there was an uptick in layoff announcements, most Districts reported employers limiting headcounts through hiring freezes, replacement-only hiring, and attrition rather than widespread layoffs (1). Several firms adjusted hours worked to match business volume, and some noted that artificial intelligence was replacing entry-level positions or improving worker productivity, thus curbing new hiring (1). Worker availability improved in most Districts, though specialized skills and fewer immigrant workers still presented hiring challenges (1). Wage growth remained modest overall, with some moderate pressure noted in manufacturing, construction, and health care due to tighter labor supply and rising health insurance premiums (1).

Prices & Cost Pressures

Prices rose moderately during the reporting period, with widespread input cost pressures in manufacturing and retail, primarily driven by tariffs (1). Several Districts, including Boston and Philadelphia, reported rising costs for insurance, utilities, technology, and health care (1). The ability to pass through these higher costs to customers varied, influenced by demand, competitive pressures, and consumer price sensitivity, leading to multiple reports of margin compression (1). Some firms faced financial strain from tariffs, while prices for certain materials declined due to sluggish demand or reduced tariff rates (1). Looking ahead, contacts largely anticipate persistent upward cost pressures, although plans for near-term price increases were mixed (1).

Sector Themes

  • Consumer activity: Consumer spending declined further overall (1). Higher-end retail spending remained resilient (New York, Cleveland, Atlanta), but lower- and middle-income consumers showed increased price sensitivity and caution with discretionary purchases (1). EV sales declined following the expiration of federal tax credits, notably in New York and St. Louis (1). Government shutdowns impacted consumer purchases, especially SNAP benefits (Philadelphia, Kansas City, San Francisco) (1).
  • Manufacturing: Activity increased somewhat in most Districts, such as Boston and Chicago (1). However, some areas like Richmond and Philadelphia experienced modest contractions (1). Tariffs and related uncertainty remained a significant headwind, affecting planning and competitiveness (Philadelphia, Dallas) (1). Demand related to AI infrastructure investments boosted some manufacturers (Cleveland, Boston) (1).
  • Services: Revenues in the nonfinancial services sector were mostly flat to down (1). Business services saw mixed activity, with some firms benefiting from M&A (Boston) or tax law changes and AI adoption (Cleveland) (1). Leisure and hospitality activity was mixed, with some noting cautious discretionary spending (Cleveland, Kansas City) and others seeing slight increases (Richmond, Chicago) (1).
  • Housing & construction: Residential construction declined in some Districts (Dallas, Philadelphia) and was unchanged in others (Atlanta), with varying home sales activity influenced by mortgage rates and inventory levels (1). Commercial real estate improved somewhat in certain areas, particularly for office properties in New York and Hartford (Boston), and data centers construction (Cleveland, San Francisco) (1).
  • Banking & credit: Loan demand was mixed, with some Districts reporting overall declines (New York, Dallas, St. Louis) but slight increases in residential mortgages (New York) or commercial loans (Cleveland) (1). Credit standards were mostly unchanged but some lenders reported tightening (New York, Philadelphia, Dallas) (1). Delinquencies generally remained elevated or increased in consumer loans and for small businesses (Philadelphia, Atlanta, St. Louis) (1).
  • Agriculture & energy: Conditions were largely stable in agriculture and energy (1). However, some contacts cited challenges from low oil prices and crop prices (Dallas, Kansas City) (1). Strong crop harvests were noted (Minneapolis, Dallas), but supply often exceeded demand, impacting farmer profits (1). Demand for primary energy sources, including LNG, solar, and wind, grew moderately due to data center activity (Atlanta, Kansas City) (1).

Regional Dispersion

  • Stronger districts:
    • Chicago: Economic activity rose slightly, with increases in employment, consumer spending, business spending, construction and real estate, and manufacturing (1). Financial conditions loosened somewhat (1).
    • Boston: Economic activity expanded slightly, driven by renewed strength in home sales, despite flat consumer spending (1). Manufacturing and commercial real estate saw modest growth (1).
  • Softer districts:
    • New York: Economic activity declined modestly (1). Employment fell slightly, consumer spending declined slightly, and services activity continued to decline moderately (1).
    • Philadelphia: Economic activity was trending down before the government shutdown, resulting in modest declines across most sectors and falling employment levels (1).
    • Dallas: Economic activity weakened slightly, with declines in nonfinancial services, retail, and banking (1). Employment fell on net, and outlooks generally worsened (1).
    • St. Louis: Activity remained unchanged, but with continued slowdown in demand amplified by the government shutdown (1). Consumer spending declined modestly (1).
    • Kansas City: Economic growth slowed slightly, reflecting softer labor conditions and cooling consumer activity (1). Oil and gas activity declined slightly due to falling oil prices (1).
  • Common constraints: Tariffs and tariff-related uncertainty were a recurring headwind for manufacturing and retail, impacting input costs and planning across multiple Districts (Boston, New York, Philadelphia, Cleveland, Richmond, Atlanta, Chicago, Dallas) (1). The federal government shutdown disrupted economic activity, consumer spending, and social services, particularly impacting low- and middle-income households (Philadelphia, Cleveland, Kansas City, Dallas, San Francisco) (1). Labor availability remained a constraint for specific skilled positions and due to fewer immigrant workers, despite overall easing in labor markets (Philadelphia, Cleveland, Richmond, San Francisco) (1).
  • Outliers:
    • Artificial Intelligence impact: Several firms noted AI replacing entry-level positions or increasing worker productivity, curbing new hiring (National, Boston, Cleveland, San Francisco) (1).
    • Data Center Demand: Strong demand related to AI data center construction was a notable boost for some manufacturers (Cleveland, Boston) and energy providers (Atlanta, Kansas City), offsetting softness in other industrial segments (1).
    • Community Distress: Community organizations across multiple Districts reported increased demand for food assistance and social services, often exacerbated by the government shutdown and rising food costs (New York, Atlanta, St. Louis, San Francisco) (1).

Signals to Watch

  • Growth risks: Ongoing consumer price sensitivity, particularly among lower- and middle-income households, and weakening discretionary spending (Cleveland, Minneapolis, Kansas City) (1). Persistence of tariff-related uncertainty impacting manufacturing investment and trade policy resolution (National, Atlanta, Dallas) (1). Slowdowns in residential construction and commercial real estate due to high mortgage rates and financing uncertainty (Cleveland, Dallas, San Francisco) (1).
  • Labour inflections: Increased reliance on hiring freezes, attrition, and replacement-only hiring as primary headcount management strategies (National, Boston, Cleveland, Atlanta) (1). Use of AI to replace entry-level roles and improve worker productivity, potentially curbing future hiring (National, Boston, Cleveland, San Francisco) (1). Reported easing in overall labor availability while pockets of difficulty persist for skilled positions and immigrant workers (National, New York, San Francisco) (1).
  • Inflation persistence: Widespread input cost pressures, particularly from tariffs, insurance, utilities, and health care (National, Philadelphia, Cleveland) (1). Firms reporting challenges in passing through higher costs due to competitive pressures and demand softness, leading to margin compression (National, Cleveland, St. Louis) (1). Contacts anticipating upward cost pressures to persist, with mixed plans for near-term price increases (National, New York, Philadelphia) (1).
  • Capital investment: Manufacturers showing optimism about future capital expenditures (Philadelphia, Kansas City) and increased demand for automation (Chicago) (1). Project delays in energy and construction sectors due to low oil prices and uncertain policy environments (Dallas) or funding constraints (San Francisco) (1).
  • Financial stress: Rising delinquencies and defaults, especially among households and small businesses, alongside tightening credit standards (Philadelphia, Atlanta, St. Louis, Dallas) (1). Small businesses struggling to obtain credit and survive amidst policy uncertainty and funding challenges (Philadelphia, Dallas) (1). Increased demand for food and housing assistance from community organizations reflecting financial strain on vulnerable households (New York, Atlanta, San Francisco) (1).
