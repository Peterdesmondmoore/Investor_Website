Federal Reserve Beige Book — Executive Summary

Publication Date — November 2025

Descriptive synthesis of current US economic conditions based on district-level reporting.

National Backdrop

Economic activity across the United States was largely unchanged since the previous report, with most Federal Reserve Districts noting little change, though two Districts observed a modest decline and one reported modest growth (1). Overall consumer spending continued to decline, while higher-end retail activity remained resilient (1). The recent government shutdown was cited by some retailers as negatively impacting consumer purchases, and a decline in EV sales followed the expiration of a federal tax credit (1). Manufacturing activity increased somewhat across most Districts, despite ongoing headwinds from tariffs and associated uncertainty (1). Revenues in the nonfinancial services sector were mostly flat to down, and conditions in agriculture and energy were largely stable, though low prices posed challenges for some commodities (1). Business outlooks were largely unchanged, with some contacts expressing optimism, particularly in manufacturing, while others noted an increased risk of slower activity in the coming months (1).

Labour Market Conditions

Employment declined slightly over the reporting period, with approximately half of the Districts indicating weaker labor demand (1). Despite an uptick in layoff announcements, many Districts observed that employers were more inclined to manage headcounts through hiring freezes, attrition, and replacement-only hiring rather than outright layoffs (1). Several firms adjusted hours worked to align with business volume, and a few noted that artificial intelligence was replacing entry-level positions or boosting worker productivity, thereby reducing the need for new hires (1). Across most Districts, employers reported an easier time finding workers; however, specific skilled positions and a reduced availability of immigrant workers continued to present hiring difficulties (1). Wage growth generally proceeded at a modest pace, though some sectors like manufacturing, construction, and healthcare experienced more moderate wage pressure due to tighter labor supply and rising health insurance premiums (1).

Prices & Cost Pressures

Prices rose moderately across the reporting period (1). Input cost pressures were widespread in manufacturing and retail, primarily attributed to tariff-induced increases (1). Several Districts also highlighted rising costs for insurance, utilities, technology, and health care (1). The ability to pass through these higher input costs to customers varied, influenced by demand, competitive dynamics, and consumer price sensitivity, leading to multiple reports of margin compression or financial strain for firms (1). Conversely, prices for certain materials declined, which firms linked to sluggish demand, deferred tariff implementation, or reduced tariff rates (1). Looking ahead, contacts largely anticipate that upward cost pressures will persist, but plans for near-term price increases were mixed, with some firms holding prices steady due to competitive pressures (1).

Sector Themes

  • Consumer activity: Consumer spending declined further overall, with resilience in higher-end retail but increased cautiousness and tightening belts among lower- and middle-income households across Districts like Cleveland, Atlanta, and San Francisco (1). EV sales saw declines after tax credit expiration, as reported by auto dealers in New York, Cleveland, St. Louis, and Atlanta (1). Restaurant spending was mixed, with some noting fewer customers and smaller average order sizes, particularly in St. Louis (1).
  • Manufacturing: Manufacturing activity generally increased somewhat, though the sector continued to face headwinds from tariffs and trade policy uncertainty in Districts like New York, Philadelphia, Richmond, Atlanta, and Dallas (1). Some manufacturers in Cleveland reported a boost from AI data center construction, while others in St. Louis experienced supply chain disruptions from an aluminum plant fire (1).
  • Services: Nonfinancial services revenues were mostly flat to down, with business services in New York and Dallas experiencing declines (1). Professional and business services demand increased moderately in Cleveland (1). Tourism activity was mixed or softened in Boston, New York, Atlanta, and Kansas City, with government shutdown impacts noted (1).
  • Housing & construction: Residential construction declined in some Districts (Philadelphia, Cleveland, Atlanta) and was unchanged in others (1). Home sales varied, with Boston seeing renewed strength due to lower mortgage rates, while Atlanta and Dallas reported slight declines (1). Commercial real estate improved in some Districts, like Boston and New York, particularly in the office market, but large construction projects remained subdued in Cleveland and St. Louis (1).
  • Banking & credit: Loan demand was mixed overall, declining in New York and Dallas, but with residential mortgage demand increasing slightly in New York (1). Credit standards were largely unchanged but saw some tightening for business loans in New York and for consumer loans in Philadelphia (1). Delinquencies reportedly rose across multiple loan types, particularly for households and small businesses in Philadelphia, St. Louis, and San Francisco (1).
  • Agriculture & energy: Conditions in both sectors were largely stable (1). Energy industry segments grew moderately in Atlanta, driven by strong demand for primary energy sources tied to data center activity (1). However, oil and gas activity declined slightly in Kansas City and Dallas due to low oil prices (1). Agricultural conditions remained weak despite strong harvests in Minneapolis, with low crop prices challenging farm finances in Dallas (1).

Regional Dispersion

  • Stronger districts: The Chicago District reported slight increases in economic activity, employment, consumer spending, business spending, and manufacturing (1). Boston observed a slight expansion in economic activity, bolstered by strong home sales (1). Cleveland saw slight increases in business activity and moderate growth in professional and business services (1).
  • Softer districts: New York experienced a modest decline in economic activity, with employment falling slightly and consumer spending easing (1). Philadelphia's economic activity trended down, exacerbated by the government shutdown (1). Dallas reported slightly weakened economic activity, with declines in nonfinancial services, retail, and banking (1). St. Louis economic activity remained unchanged, facing a slowdown in demand and a pessimistic outlook (1). Kansas City noted slightly slower economic growth due to softer labor conditions and cooling consumer activity (1).
  • Common constraints: Tariffs and associated uncertainty continued to be a pervasive headwind for manufacturing and pricing across multiple Districts including New York, Philadelphia, Richmond, Atlanta, and Dallas (1). The federal government shutdown impacted demand and operations in Philadelphia, St. Louis, Kansas City, and Dallas, and disrupted SNAP benefits nationally (1). Elevated input costs, particularly for insurance, utilities, and health care, were widely reported across nearly all Districts (1). Softer consumer demand and cautious discretionary spending, especially from lower- and middle-income households, were common themes (1).
  • Outliers: Cleveland manufacturing saw a boost from AI data center construction, offsetting softness in other areas (1). Kansas City firms reported passing through only about 20 percent of their input cost increases (1). In San Francisco, agriculture contacts highlighted increased difficulties in hiring workers due to immigration visa revocations (1).

Signals to Watch

  • Growth risks: Worsening outlooks were noted in Dallas and St. Louis, citing general economic slowdown, tariff concerns, interest rates, and heightened uncertainty (1). Cautious discretionary spending among consumers and the impact of the government shutdown on various sectors pose risks to demand (1).
  • Labour inflections: The widespread adoption of hiring freezes, replacement-only hiring, and attrition over layoffs suggests a shift in employment strategies (1). The use of AI to replace entry-level positions and enhance worker productivity (e.g., Boston, Cleveland, Dallas, San Francisco) indicates a structural change in hiring needs (1).
  • Inflation persistence: Persistent upward pressure on input costs from tariffs, insurance, health care, and energy is a key concern (1). The mixed ability and willingness of firms to pass through these costs, coupled with reports of margin compression, will influence future pricing behavior (1).
  • Capital investment: Capital expenditure plans picked up in New York and Chicago, with some manufacturers expecting increases (1). However, commercial clients in Cleveland remained cautious, and many farmers in Minneapolis were delaying capital expenditures due to tight margins (1).
  • Financial stress: Rising delinquencies among households and small businesses in Philadelphia, St. Louis, and San Francisco indicate increasing financial strain (1). Increased reliance on debt for household expenses (Atlanta) and difficulties for small businesses in securing funding (Philadelphia, Dallas, San Francisco) are early stress indicators (1).
