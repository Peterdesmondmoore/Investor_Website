US Labour Market: employment composition, tightness, wages, hours, flows and sentiment.

As of 2025-11-30

Gemini Summary

Employment composition, tightness, wages, hours, flows and sentiment.

Executive Summary

The US labor market currently presents a mixed and generally neutral picture, with underlying indicators suggesting an easing of previously tighter conditions. While most composite regimes remain in the "NEUTRAL" classification, several components indicate rising slack and softening momentum, nearing or in "COOL" or "Bearish" states.

Labor market momentum is subdued, hidden slack is persistent, and consumer sentiment is notably weak. This points to a market that is not yet in an outright slowdown regime but is consistently exhibiting characteristics of reduced dynamism and increased downside pressure.

  • Employment Composition: Neutral regime, but with increasing unemployment and elevated slack.
  • Underemployment: Neutral regime, but composite score indicates stress near historical highs.
  • Tightness / Turnover: Labor Market Tightness is Neutral with easing momentum; Turnover Differential is Bearish, signaling sustained loosening.
  • Hours and Momentum: Weekly Hours are Neutral, approaching a "COOL" threshold; Labor Momentum is Neutral, but payroll momentum is below its historical median.
  • Overall Labour-Market Bias: Stabilising, but with underlying softening trends and persistent slack.

1. Employment Composition & Headline Slack

The Employment Composition signal currently stands in a NEUTRAL regime, with a composite z-score of -0.657 as of 2025-09-01. This reading is just above the "COOL" threshold, reflecting an observed trend towards increasing slack.

The unemployment rate has risen to 4.4% with a negative z-score of -0.843, indicating significant deviation below its historical median (after inversion). The participation rate is 62.4%, and the employment-population ratio is 59.7%. Momentum diagnostics show upward pressure on the unemployment rate (+0.3 over three months), posing continued downside risk to headline slack measures.

2. Underemployment & Hidden Slack

Underemployment Risk remains in a NEUTRAL regime as of 2025-09-01, with a composite z-score of 0.519540. The U-6 underutilization rate is 8.0%, and median weeks unemployed stands at 10.0 weeks.

Despite the "NEUTRAL" z-score classification, the Underemployment 0-100 score is 99.999999, suggesting that labor market stress, when viewed on its historical range, is near extreme highs. Both U-6 and median weeks unemployed are above their historical medians, and U-6 underutilization has experienced an increase of 0.3 over three months. This persistence of elevated hidden slack could dampen future consumption growth and wage pressure.

3. Tightness, Turnover & Job Flows

Labor Market Tightness, as indicated by JOLTS data, is in a NEUTRAL regime with a composite z-score of -0.37 as of 2025-08-01. This reflects an easing of demand-side pressures, with the quits rate at 1.9%, layoffs at 1.1%, and job openings at 4.3%.

The Labor Market Turnover Differential (quits minus layoffs) has been in a Bearish regime since September 2024, with its latest reading at 0.8 percentage points and a z-score of -0.843 as of 2025-08-01. This signals sustained loosening in worker confidence and labor market flexibility. Job Flows, based on Q4 2024 BDS data, are in a NEUTRAL regime with a z-score of -0.21. While reduced job losses contribute to this neutrality, gross job gains remain below their historical median, indicating a lack of strong hiring dynamism.

4. Hours Worked & Labour Momentum

The Weekly Hours signal remains in a NEUTRAL regime as of 2025-09-01, with its composite level z-score at -0.674. This reading is nearing the "COOL" regime threshold, suggesting softening labor demand.

Total Private hours are 34.2, and Manufacturing hours are 39.9, with both individual z-scores at -0.67. Manufacturing hours also show negative short-run momentum, declining by 0.2 over three months. The broader Labour Market Momentum signal, combining jobless claims and payrolls, is in a Neutral regime as of 2025-09-30, with a composite z-score of -0.11. While initial claims declined 1.32% YoY (latest 2025-11-30), continuing claims rose 3.82% YoY, and payroll momentum (PAYEMS 3-month difference) has been consistently negative since April 2025, suggesting below-median hiring strength.

5. Sectoral Breadth & Diffusion

The Sectoral Employment Diffusion signal registers a NEUTRAL regime as of 2025-09-01, with a diffusion index of 50.0 and a z-score of 0.0. This indicates that half of the four tracked sectors exhibit positive year-over-year employment growth.

Leisure & Hospitality shows the strongest growth at +1.39% YoY, while Manufacturing recorded the largest decline at -0.73% YoY. The median sector YoY growth shows negative three-month momentum, suggesting a weakening underlying trend in employment breadth despite the neutral headline diffusion index.

6. Synthesised US Labour Market Regime

The synthesised US labor market regime is best characterised as one of Neutrality with Underlying Softening and Elevated Slack. While many headline composites reside in a "NEUTRAL" state, several key indicators show sustained movements towards "COOL" or "Bearish" conditions, pointing to a persistent, albeit gradual, unwinding of labor market tightness. The market demonstrates resilience but lacks broad-based dynamism, with signs of rising hidden slack and weakening momentum in critical forward-looking components.

7. Forward Risks

Upside / Resilience Risks

  • Stabilisation in underemployment could alleviate hidden slack and support consumption.
  • An improvement in the Turnover Differential and Gross Job Gains would signal renewed firm-level dynamism.
  • Sectoral re-acceleration beyond Leisure & Hospitality, particularly in cyclically sensitive areas, could broaden employment gains.

Downside / Slowdown Risks

  • Underemployment composite turning definitively "HOT" would signal accelerating broad slack and job-finding frictions.
  • Further deterioration in job flows (declining gains, rising losses) would imply reduced firm creation and increasing employment vulnerability.
  • Sectoral diffusion index tipping into "COOL" would indicate significant narrowing of job growth breadth, often preceding broader slowdowns.

8. Investment Interpretation (Informational)

The observed labor market dynamics suggest a backdrop for moderated wage pressure. Indicators of slack, such as rising unemployment and elevated underemployment stress, imply a reduced inflationary impulse from labor costs. This environment is typically associated with a reduced need for restrictive monetary policy. Consumer spending could face headwinds from persistent weak sentiment and elevated underemployment, impacting aggregate demand.

Corporate earnings may experience sensitivity to both reduced pricing power from easing wage pressures and potential softness in consumer demand. Policymakers are likely to monitor these signals closely for signs of a more significant economic deceleration, which could influence future monetary policy adjustments towards a more accommodative stance.
