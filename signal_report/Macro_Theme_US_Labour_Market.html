US Labour Market: employment composition, tightness, wages, hours, flows and sentiment.

As of 2025-11-30

Gemini Summary

Employment composition, tightness, wages, hours, flows and sentiment.

Executive Summary

The US labor market currently presents a nuanced picture, largely interpreted as a neutral but softening environment as of October 2025. While several aggregate indicators remain in a "Neutral" regime, underlying metrics and momentum signals suggest an easing of previously tight conditions and a potential for increased slack. The market is not exhibiting widespread overheating, nor is it in a deep contraction, but the bias is clearly towards cooling dynamics.

Hidden slack and declining consumer sentiment are emerging as notable concerns, alongside signs of flattening payroll momentum and weakening job dynamism at the firm level. Wage pressure signals have moved into a cooling phase, reflecting this broader moderation. This state points to a reduction in inflationary pressures from the labor market and suggests a cautious outlook for household consumption, with potential implications for forward policy decisions.

  • Employment Composition: Currently NEUTRAL, with a z-score nearing the "COOL" threshold, indicating underlying weakness.
  • Underemployment Trend: NEUTRAL in regime, but with elevated stress levels and rising U-6 momentum, suggesting increasing hidden slack.
  • Tightness / Turnover Conditions: JOLTS tightness is NEUTRAL with easing momentum, contrasting with a "Bearish" regime in worker-initiated turnover.
  • Hours and Momentum: Weekly hours are NEUTRAL but nearing a "COOL" regime. Overall labor momentum is NEUTRAL, characterized by flattening payroll growth.
  • Overall Labour-Market Bias: Neutral, but with several components demonstrating a pronounced shift towards cooling and softening conditions, implying a disinflationary impulse.

1. Employment Composition & Headline Slack

The Employment Composition signal is currently in a NEUTRAL regime as of September 2025, with a composite z-score of -0.657. This positioning is close to the -0.75 threshold for a "COOL" regime, reflecting a trend towards increasing slack in headline employment metrics. The unemployment rate stands at 4.4%, with participation at 62.4% and the employment-population ratio at 59.7%.

Analysis of individual components reveals upward momentum in the unemployment rate (+0.3 over three months) and persistently low employment-population ratios (-0.771 z-score). While not yet definitively "COOL," the underlying trends point to a decelerating employment environment and a modest but consistent unwinding of prior tightness.

2. Underemployment & Hidden Slack

Underemployment risk is in a NEUTRAL regime as of September 2025, with a composite z-score of 0.519. This z-score indicates above-median labor market stress compared to historical norms. The U-6 underutilization rate is 8.0%, and median weeks unemployed stands at 10.0.

Notably, the signal's 0-100 score is at 99.999999, suggesting that within its observed range, stress levels are near historical highs, despite the "NEUTRAL" z-score classification. The 3-month momentum for the U-6 rate is positive (+0.3), indicating a rising trend in broader labor underutilization and a growing pool of hidden slack. This elevated stress in underemployment points to a potential constraint on future consumption and wage pressure, even if headline unemployment remains contained.

3. Tightness, Turnover & Job Flows

Labor market tightness, as measured by the JOLTS composite, is in a NEUTRAL regime as of August 2025, with a composite z-score of -0.371. The quits rate is 1.9%, layoffs rate is 1.1%, and job openings rate is 4.3%. Three-month momentum for both labor tightness (quits minus layoffs) and job openings shows a slight easing (-0.2 and -0.3 respectively).

Conversely, the separate Labor Market Turnover Differential signal is in a "Bearish" regime as of August 2025, with a z-score of -0.84. This reflects a significant decline in worker-initiated turnover relative to employer-initiated separations, signaling decreasing worker confidence and a loosening labor market. Quarterly Job Flows (latest data December 2024) are NEUTRAL with a composite z-score of -0.21, showing improvement from recent "COOL" regimes primarily due to reduced job losses (positive z-score for negated losses), but still characterized by weak gross job gains (negative z-score). This combination suggests a reduction in labor market dynamism and a shift away from a sellers' market for labor.

4. Hours Worked & Labour Momentum

The Weekly Hours signal is currently in a NEUTRAL regime as of September 2025, with a composite z-score of -0.674. This position is notably close to the "COOL" regime threshold of -0.75. Average weekly hours for Total Private are 34.2, and for Manufacturing are 39.9. Manufacturing hours exhibit negative 3-month momentum (-0.2), signaling a softening in industrial labor demand.

Overall Labour Market Momentum, integrating jobless claims and payroll growth, is in a Neutral regime as of September 2025, with a composite value of -0.113. While initial and continuing claims show near-neutral z-scores, payroll momentum (3-month difference) has a negative z-score (-0.495). This indicates a flattening trend in short-term employment growth, reinforcing the narrative of a decelerating labor market.

5. Sectoral Breadth & Diffusion

The Sectoral Employment Diffusion signal is in a NEUTRAL regime as of September 2025, with a diffusion z-score of 0.0. The diffusion index is 50.0, indicating that two out of the four tracked sectors (Manufacturing, Construction, Professional & Business Services, Leisure & Hospitality) exhibited positive year-over-year employment growth. Leisure & Hospitality shows the strongest growth (+1.39% YoY), while Manufacturing recorded a decline (-0.73% YoY).

Despite the neutral aggregate reading, the 3-month momentum of the median sector year-over-year growth is negative (-0.003), suggesting a weakening in the underlying breadth of job gains. This indicates that while the market is not contracting broadly, the resilience of growth across sectors is diminishing, with pockets of weakness emerging.

6. Synthesised US Labour Market Regime

The synthesised US labor market regime presents a picture of increasing moderation and loosening conditions. While many headline indicators remain within "Neutral" classifications, several underlying components and forward-looking measures exhibit a distinct bias towards "Cooling" or "Bearish" states, particularly related to worker confidence, hidden slack, and demand indicators. This suggests a systemic shift away from the post-pandemic tightness towards a more balanced, albeit softening, labor environment.

7. Forward Risks

Upside / Resilience Risks

  • Stabilisation in underemployment rates and median weeks unemployed could signal renewed labor market health.
  • Improving turnover dynamics (quits increasing relative to layoffs) and strengthening gross job gains would indicate greater firm dynamism.
  • Sectoral re-acceleration beyond defensive areas, with an increasing diffusion index, would reflect broadening economic strength.

Downside / Slowdown Risks

  • Underemployment composite turning definitively into a “HOT” regime would confirm significant and worsening hidden slack.
  • Further deterioration in gross job gains and a sustained negative net job creation from job flows would signal deeper firm-level contraction.
  • Sectoral employment diffusion tipping into a “COOL” regime would indicate broad-based employment contraction and narrowing growth.

8. Investment Interpretation (Informational)

The prevailing neutral-to-cooling labor market regime implies diminishing upward pressure on wages. The Wage Pressure signal, a component of the broader Wage Growth and Inflation Composite, has already entered a 'COOL' regime, and the overall Inflation Composite remains 'NEUTRAL' despite periods of underlying wage pressure. This suggests that the labor market's contribution to inflationary concerns is abating, potentially affording monetary policy greater flexibility.

A softening labor market, characterized by rising hidden slack (Underemployment Risk) and declining consumer sentiment (UMCSENT "Bearish"), carries implications for consumer spending and corporate earnings. Reduced wage growth and increased job market uncertainty could dampen household consumption, impacting revenue growth for consumer-facing sectors. Companies may face lower labor costs but also potentially weaker demand, affecting earnings outlooks. This environment highlights the importance of monitoring consumption and policy responses for their impact on asset classes.
