US Labour Market
Synthesises employment composition, labour-market tightness, wage and hours signals, and worker flows/sentiment to assess the prevailing US labour-market regime.

Regime Assessment:

  • Regime: Defensive / Risk-Negative
  • Regime Confidence Index: Medium Confidence
  • The regime is fragile.

Why This Regime:

  • The dominant drivers are pronounced weakness in consumer sentiment and deteriorating labor market dynamics. The University of Michigan Consumer Sentiment signal is Bearish with extreme conviction (4). Labor Market Turnover is also Bearish, signaling sustained weakness in worker confidence (6).
  • Dynamic weighting prioritised these signals due to their high interpretation confidence and extreme or moderately extreme conviction, despite their medium aggregation weight. These early-to-mid and mid-to-late cycle indicators collectively highlight increasing economic headwinds.
  • The Labour Market Momentum signal (10) was downweighted. Its neutral call is less influential due to mixed signals arising from missing data.

Alignment & Tensions:

  • Signals reinforce each other in the direction of increasing slack. The Underemployment Risk signal, while Neutral, leans towards rising labor market stress (2). Similarly, Employment Composition, also Neutral, indicates a lean towards increasing slack (3). Job Flows, while Neutral, notes underlying softening in dynamism (9).
  • Tensions exist with other neutral signals such as the Explicit Slack Gap (1), Sectoral Employment Diffusion (5), and Weekly Hours (11).
  • These tensions do not overturn the regime call. The strong conviction of the negative signals, combined with the discernible lean towards softening within many "neutral" readings, indicates a fragile balance tilting towards deterioration.

Scenario Balance:

  • The dominant scenario is increasing labor market slack, accompanied by weakening consumer demand and confidence.
  • Primary upside risk is a significant and sustained rebound in consumer sentiment, triggering increased aggregate demand and spending.
  • Primary downside risk is further deterioration in consumer spending intentions and a more pronounced rise in underemployment.

What Would Change the Regime:

  • A material reversal from the extreme bearishness in consumer sentiment (4).
  • A shift in labor market turnover dynamics from Bearish to Neutral or Bullish (6).
  • Underemployment risk receding significantly, moving away from the threshold of rising stress (2).
