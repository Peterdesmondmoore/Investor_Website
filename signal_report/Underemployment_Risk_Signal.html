Gemini Summary

Signal Summary:

  • The Underemployment Risk signal currently stands in a NEUTRAL regime, but its composite z-score (0.701) is approaching the +0.75 threshold, indicating rising labour market stress.
  • This reflects a trend towards increasing underemployment, positioning the signal on the cusp of a potential transition to a HOT (stress rising) regime.

Key Dynamics:

  • The U-6 underutilization rate is 8.7% and median weeks unemployed is 9.5 as of 2025-11-01 (1).
  • Momentum in U-6 underutilization is positive (+0.6 over 3 months), suggesting increasing broad slack (1).
  • Conversely, median weeks unemployed shows slightly negative momentum (-0.3 over 3 months), implying a minor easing in job-finding frictions (1).
  • Conditional Invalidation: A sustained decline in both the U-6 rate and median weeks unemployed, pushing the composite z-score consistently below -0.75.

Scenario Balance:

  • Base Case dominant: The NEUTRAL regime persists, with the composite z-score hovering near the HOT threshold, reflecting ongoing, elevated underemployment risk.
  • Downside secondary: The signal transitions to a HOT regime, with the composite z-score exceeding +0.75, triggered by further increases in the U-6 rate.
  • Upside residual: Significant reductions in both U-6 and median weeks unemployed lead to a COOL regime, signaling easing labour market stress.

Time Horizon & Aggregation:

  • Time Horizon: Cyclical (months), given its sensitivity to broader economic cycles and their impact on labour market conditions.
  • Aggregation Weight Hint: High, as underemployment is a key indicator of labour market health, influencing wage growth and consumer demand.

Macro Relevance:

  • This signal primarily informs the "slack" dimension of the labour market and wider economic conditions (1).
  • It is relevant across mid to late-cycle phases, signaling either persistent slack or emerging weakness as the economy slows.
  • Interacts with inflation signals (2) by indicating disinflationary pressure and with growth indicators through consumer spending implications (3).
