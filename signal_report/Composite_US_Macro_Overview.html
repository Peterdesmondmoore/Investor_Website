Macro Theme : US Macro Overview

US Macro Overview: synthesising growth, labour, credit, liquidity, inflation and global FX/inflation macro themes into a consolidated view.

As of 2025-11-29

Gemini Summary

Executive Summary

The US macro backdrop is characterized by a nuanced rebalancing of forces. While underlying industrial activity shows resilience and monetary conditions are easing due to systemic liquidity injections, elements of market caution persist, and the labor market exhibits signs of softening. Inflationary pressures have largely moderated, with expectations remaining anchored.

Current conditions are most consistent with a transition phase from a mature expansion towards a more moderated growth environment, with potential for further softening in specific areas. The easing of financial conditions is a key supportive factor, but demand-side signals are becoming more cautious.

Globally, a 'Weakening (USD)' regime contributes to easing financial conditions and supports commodity prices, implying a potential for renewed FX-driven inflationary pressure. This global dynamic introduces a counteracting force to domestic disinflationary trends and necessitates monitoring of imported inflation alongside domestic price dynamics.

Growth & Real Activity (Global + US)

Global Growth Signals

  • Industrial demand indicators, specifically copper, registered a 'Demand_Tailwind' from April to June 2025, signaling robust industrial activity and capital expenditure.
  • WTI crude oil prices declined significantly from July to November 2025, suggesting a moderation in energy demand or increased supply.
  • Precious metals (Gold, Silver) show extreme speculative net short positioning as of October 2025, indicative of speculative caution or bearish sentiment, despite rising gold prices. Hedger activity in silver suggests underlying physical demand.
  • Forward economic cycle proxies for the "Other" sector indicate balanced flows with minor positive momentum, suggesting stabilization rather than strong acceleration or deceleration.

US Growth-Related Content

  • University of Michigan Consumer Sentiment is 'Bearish' as of October 2025, indicating significant deterioration in consumer confidence, implying potential demand headwinds for the US economy.

Real Activity Momentum and Leading vs Coincident Signals

  • Real activity momentum is mixed, with strengthening in cyclical industrial sectors (copper) contrasting with moderating energy-related activity (WTI) and softening consumer sentiment.
  • **Coincident signals** such as robust industrial metals and easing labor market tightness suggest an ongoing, albeit possibly slower, expansion.
  • **Leading signals** including declining WTI crude, extreme speculative short positioning in precious metals, and 'Bearish' US consumer sentiment and labor market turnover differential point to potential future headwinds and increasing risk aversion.

Signal Alignment/Disalignment

  • A disalignment exists between the resilient industrial demand suggested by copper and the cautious/bearish signals from WTI crude and precious metals speculative positioning.
  • Within the US, 'Bearish' consumer sentiment contradicts the 'NEUTRAL' readings from many aggregate labor market indicators, suggesting a forward-looking deterioration not yet fully reflected in concurrent aggregate data.

Labour Market & Wage Dynamics

  • The US labour market is undergoing a period of normalization and gradual loosening from previous tightness. Employment Composition, Labor Market Tightness (JOLTS), and Underemployment Risk are all classified as 'NEUTRAL' as of August/September 2025. The unemployment rate has risen to 4.4% in September 2025.
  • The Labor Market Turnover Differential is 'Bearish' as of August 2025, reflecting declining worker confidence and a shift towards more stressed reallocation dynamics. Labour Market Momentum is 'Neutral' as of November 2025, with mixed claims data and negative payrolls momentum.
  • The Wage Growth & Inflation Composite is 'NEUTRAL' as of August 2025, with moderate wage growth (private industry 3.5% YoY, civilian 3.6% YoY as of April 2025). Wage pressure indicators are consistent with moderating inflation risk, and labour cost pressures are contained within a neutral range.
  • The 'Bearish' University of Michigan Consumer Sentiment (October 2025) suggests softening domestic demand, which could foreshadow future softening in labor outcomes and broader economic activity. Moderating wage growth, combined with neutral labour market tightness, implies reduced risk of a reinforcing wage-price spiral, supporting the broader disinflationary narrative.

Credit Conditions & Financial Stress

  • High Yield (HY) and Investment Grade (IG) option-adjusted spreads are at low levels, maintaining a 'NORMAL' regime as of November 30, 2025 (HY_OAS: 3.00%, IG_OAS: 0.82%). Spreads have generally declined since April 2025, with 3-month momentum indicating a subtle upward drift.
  • The Private-Credit Impulse is in a 'Stable' regime as of November 30, 2025, indicating a recovery in credit growth momentum from a previous 'Decelerating' phase. This suggests moderate credit creation that is not currently amplifying market stress, potentially acting as a moderating factor.
  • The Financial Stress Composite is 'Neutral' (0.045) as of November 30, 2025, indicating overall stable market functioning. However, the St. Louis Fed Financial Stress Index (STLFSI4) registered a 'Bearish' signal (z-score 1.700081) as of November 21, 2025, reflecting a recent surge and indicating elevated systemic stress.
  • Credit spreads show a cyclical transition to 'NORMAL'. The 'Bearish' signal from STLFSI4 appears to be a short-cycle perturbation. The 'NORMAL' credit spread regime and 'Stable' private credit impulse suggest that the structural underpinnings of credit availability and pricing remain contained, with contained HY spreads and a positive yield curve offsetting the elevated STLFSI4 signal within the broader composite.

Liquidity, Policy Stance & Real Rates

  • The overall monetary policy stance is exhibiting a nuanced easing bias, despite ongoing quantitative tightening, driven by significant liquidity injections effectively counteracting other tightening measures.
  • The Federal Reserve Liquidity Composite is in an "Expansion" regime (1.30 as of November 30, 2025) since July 2025, primarily due to the unwinding of the Overnight Reverse Repurchase Agreement (ON RRP) facility (inverted RRP YoY -96.17%).
  • This expansion occurs concurrently with "Bearish" signals from Federal Reserve Total Assets (YoY -5.11%) and Reserve Balances (YoY -10.26%), indicating ongoing balance sheet contraction (quantitative tightening). The ON RRP unwinding's strength is currently overriding these contractionary forces.
  • M2 money supply dynamics have recently moderated to "Neutral" (November 30, 2025), following a "Bullish" expansion.
  • The US 10-year real interest rate has transitioned from a "Bearish" (restrictive) to a "Neutral" regime (1.77% as of November 1, 2025), stabilizing from previously elevated levels. This shift to a less restrictive real rate environment aligns directionally with the "Expansion" in the Federal Reserve Liquidity Composite, suggesting a broad reduction in monetary restrictiveness and proving broadly supportive for the real economy.

Inflation, Price Dynamics & FX Interactions

  • **Domestic Inflation Pressures:** US inflation is characterized by disinflationary momentum or stable, moderate price pressures. The Fed Inflation Signal is "COOL" (CPI YoY 2.79%), and the Policy-Relevant Core PCE Composite is "Neutral" (Core PCE YoY 2.91%). Market-implied inflation expectations are "STABLE" and term structure is "Neutral" (slightly inverted). Wage growth is also "NEUTRAL," indicating contained labour cost pressures. Upstream PPI (3.66% YoY) exceeds CPI, but the PPI-CPI divergence is "NEUTRAL."
  • **Global Price and FX Conditions:** The U.S. Dollar is in a 'Weakening (USD)' regime since May 2025. This generally implies easing global financial conditions and is supportive of commodity prices. A weakening USD is typically associated with increased US import costs and potential upward pressure on global tradable inflation.
  • **FX Behavior Reinforcing or Offsetting:** The 'Weakening (USD)' regime is offsetting domestic disinflationary trends. While domestic signals point to contained and moderating inflation, the weaker dollar implies that external factors (higher import costs, supported commodity prices) are exerting upward pressure on overall price dynamics, preventing further disinflation or contributing to sustained inflationary dynamics.
  • **Shifts in Inflation Regime:** US inflation signals (Fed Inflation, PCE Composite) have shifted from previously "HOT" regimes to current "COOL" or "NEUTRAL" classifications, reflecting a broad disinflationary regime shift. Market-Implied Inflation Expectations have transitioned from "RISING" to the current "STABLE" regime. The 'Weakening (USD)' regime in FX represents a shift from a 'Strengthening (USD)' regime (Oct 2023-Apr 2025).

Cross-Theme Consistency and Tensions

Areas of Strong Cross-Theme Alignment

  • Disinflationary Trend: US Inflation & Price Dynamics ('COOL' Fed Inflation, 'Neutral' PCE, 'STABLE' expectations, 'NEUTRAL' wage growth) aligns with the US Labour Market's 'NEUTRAL' Wage Growth & Inflation Composite and moderating wage pressures.
  • Easing Monetary Conditions: US Liquidity & Monetary Conditions shows an "Expansion" in the Federal Reserve Liquidity Composite and a shift to "Neutral" for US 10-year real yields. This aligns with a 'Weakening (USD)' regime in Global Inflation & FX Dynamics, associated with easing global financial conditions.
  • Economic Moderation: 'Bearish' US Consumer Sentiment (US Labour Market) and decelerating WTI crude oil prices (Global Growth) are consistent with a moderation in overall economic momentum and demand.

Areas Where Messages Diverge

  • Growth vs. Sentiment: Robust industrial activity (copper 'Demand_Tailwind' in Global Growth) contrasts with 'Bearish' US Consumer Sentiment (US Labour Market) indicating softening demand-side impulses.
  • Systemic Stress vs. Market Spreads: US Credit Conditions highlights a 'Bearish' St. Louis Fed Financial Stress Index (STLFSI4), diverging from 'NORMAL' corporate credit spreads and a 'Neutral' broader Financial Stress Composite. The broader composites appear to be buffering the specific stress signal.
  • Broad Money vs. Specific Liquidity: M2 money supply is "Neutral" (US Liquidity & Monetary Conditions), while the Federal Reserve Liquidity Composite is in "Expansion" due to ON RRP unwinding, indicating specific, targeted liquidity injections are strong, but broad money aggregates are not expanding at the same pace.
  • Domestic vs. Global Inflationary Forces: Domestic US inflation metrics indicate moderation/disinflation. However, the 'Weakening (USD)' regime (Global Inflation & FX Dynamics) implies renewed FX-driven inflationary pressure, acting as a counter-force to domestic disinflation.

Leading-Lagging Relationships That Explain Divergences

  • 'Bearish' consumer sentiment and labour market turnover (leading indicators from US Labour Market) may precede a broader slowdown in real activity and demand, which is currently supported by robust industrial signals (coincident/lagging, Global Growth).
  • The 'Bearish' STLFSI4 (more real-time systemic stress indicator) may lead to wider credit spreads or a shift in broader credit conditions composites if the stress persists. Currently, other components within the composites are **lagging** in reflecting this stress or are buffering it.
  • The 'Weakening (USD)' (Global Inflation & FX Dynamics) can be seen as a leading indicator for imported inflation, potentially impacting US Inflation & Price Dynamics with a lag as pass-through occurs.

Risk Configuration and Scenario Space

Risk Configuration

  • Growth Risk: Risk appears **mixed to downside-biased**. While industrial activity remains robust, softening consumer sentiment and labour market turnover indicate emerging demand headwinds.
  • Inflation Risk: Risk is assessed as **moderate to potentially re-emerging from external channels**. Domestic inflation trends are disinflationary/neutral. However, a 'Weakening (USD)' carries elevated global inflation expectations and potential for FX-driven price pressure.
  • Financial Stability and Liquidity Risk: Financial stability risk is **elevated in specific systemic indicators but contained in broader market-based measures**. Liquidity conditions are "Expansionary" due to ON RRP unwinding, mitigating immediate liquidity stress.

Plausible Macro Configurations Implied by the Reports

  • Soft Landing Scenario: The gradual easing of labour market tightness, moderating wage growth, contained credit spreads, and expansive systemic liquidity (driven by ON RRP unwinding) are consistent with an orderly deceleration of inflation and growth towards sustainable levels without a severe downturn.
  • Divergent Cyclical Slowdown: Resilient industrial activity and easing financial conditions exist alongside persistent weakness in consumer sentiment and some short-term systemic stress. This implies an uneven slowdown, with specific sectors and aspects of the economy experiencing more pronounced headwinds, potentially leading to increased market volatility.
  • Externally Driven Re-inflation: Despite domestic disinflation, a continued 'Weakening (USD)' and potential associated rise in global commodity prices could lead to a re-acceleration of US inflation via imported channels. This would challenge the Federal Reserve's disinflationary objectives, potentially necessitating a prolonged restrictive stance.

Watchpoints

  • A sustained decline in the quits rate coupled with a corresponding rise in the layoffs rate, pushing the Labor Market Turnover Differential further into a 'Bearish' regime (US Labour Market).
  • St. Louis Fed Financial Stress Index (STLFSI4) remaining 'Bearish' for two consecutive weekly readings, or its Stress_z component exceeding 2.0 (US Credit Conditions).
  • A sustained shift in the Fed Inflation Signal from its current "COOL" regime to "NEUTRAL" or "HOT" (US Inflation & Price Dynamics).
  • U.S. Dollar Index value crossing above its 12-month moving average, signaling a shift from 'Weakening' to 'Strengthening' (Global Inflation & FX Dynamics).
  • A transition of the Federal Reserve Liquidity Composite back to a "Neutral" or "Contraction" regime (US Liquidity & Monetary Conditions).
  • Sustained 'Demand_Tailwind' regime in copper and an expansion of this trend to a broader basket of industrial metals (Global Growth).
  • University of Michigan Consumer Sentiment remaining deeply 'Bearish' or declining further, with an accelerating negative year-over-year change (US Labour Market).

Conclusion

The current US macro environment reflects a complex interplay between cyclical rebalancing and evolving structural dynamics. The gradual easing of labour market tightness and moderating wage growth represent cyclical adjustments from post-pandemic highs. Concurrently, the significant liquidity injection from ON RRP unwinding, despite ongoing quantitative tightening, points to a structural shift in liquidity provision that is currently supportive.

The overall degree of macro stability is moderate. While core domestic inflation and market-implied expectations appear stable, and credit availability remains contained, there are notable areas of fragility. These include deeply 'Bearish' consumer sentiment, persistent 'Bearish' signals in labour market turnover, and an elevated systemic stress reading from the STLFSI4, which diverges from broader, more benign credit indicators.

The 'Weakening (USD)' regime introduces an external inflationary impulse that could challenge domestic disinflationary trends. This configuration suggests a resilient but unpressured core economy with emerging demand-side and idiosyncratic financial stress points that warrant close monitoring for potential inflection.

Model and Prompt Improvements

  • **Missing Views, Indicators, or Regions:**
    • Explicit global growth indicators (e.g., Purchasing Managers' Index (PMI) data for key economies) to provide direct, real-time insights into manufacturing and services activity beyond commodity flows.
    • Comprehensive measures of real wage growth (e.g., Average Weekly Earnings adjusted for CPI/PCE) to assess purchasing power and its direct impact on consumer demand.
    • Granular services inflation data (e.g., ex-housing services) and detailed global supply chain pressure indices to provide more specific insights into inflation drivers.
    • Equity market implied volatility (VIX) directly integrated into credit and liquidity composites for a more comprehensive view of market-implied risk and stress.
    • Forward-looking private credit sentiment indicators (e.g., senior loan officer surveys) to gauge future lending conditions.
  • **Structural Improvements to How the Theme Reports Are Combined:**
    • Harmonisation of reporting frequencies across all financial stress indicators to improve temporal alignment and reduce lead/lag issues in cross-theme synthesis.
    • Development of a framework for explicit regime shading on price charts for all signals, allowing for visual back-testing and clearer interpretation of how positioning and flow regimes interact with price trends over time.
    • Implementation of dynamic weighting schemes for composite indicators that adapt sensitivity during periods of pronounced market volatility or regime shifts.
    • Integration of a causal inference framework or a probabilistic model to better quantify the impact of a 'Weakening (USD)' on domestic inflation and distinguish between cyclical and structural drivers of inflation.
