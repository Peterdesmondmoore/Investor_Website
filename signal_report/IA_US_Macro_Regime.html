US Macro Regime State, Momentum & Transition Risk
An integrated view of regime conditions, momentum shifts, structural tensions, and transition risks

Macro Regime Assessment:

  • The global macro environment is characterized by Late-Cycle Growth, driven by robust credit conditions, positive cross-asset sentiment, and expansionary global trade and growth dynamics.
  • This expansion is fragile, with decelerating momentum and increasing transition risk.
  • Key tensions exist from a defensive US labor market and persistent structural disinflationary pressures. US growth and liquidity conditions also remain in a transitional, fragile balance.
  • Primary policy surprise risks are two-sided, stemming from conflicting signals in the labor market and inflation versus crowded market positioning.
  • Equities and rates suggest a defensive posture, commodities are mixed, and the USD is in a risk-on environment with elevated reversal risk.
  • Regime confidence has weakened notably, though theme alignment is stable. Tensions have eased slightly.

Why This Regime:

  • The "Late-Cycle Growth" regime is primarily driven by high-weight, high-conviction signals. US Credit Conditions are Expansionary/Risk-Positive (1), reflecting easing corporate spreads and contained financial stress. Cross-Asset Positioning & Sentiment is Bullish (2), indicating low divergence between speculators and hedgers and normal market volatility. Global Trade & Demand is Risk-Positive (3), supported by sustained speculative long positioning and a weakening USD. Global Growth & Economic Cycles is also Expansionary/Risk-Positive (4), underpinned by strong copper-specific demand.
  • Dynamic weighting prioritized these signals due to their high aggregation weight hints and high conviction bands. Structural signals, such as US Inflation & Price Dynamics, while indicating a "COOL" regime, are somewhat offset by cyclical growth drivers.
  • Signals were downweighted where interpretation confidence was mixed or internal conflicts were present. The US Labour Market signal (5), despite a medium aggregation weight, was downweighted due to internal conflict and mixed interpretation confidence in its underlying components, such as Labor Market Turnover. Similarly, the US Growth & Business Cycle signal (7) was downweighted due to conflicting high-weight signals (e.g., contractionary capex intent versus stable industrial production).

Alignment & Tensions:

  • Signals reinforcing the regime include strong US Credit Conditions (1) and positive Cross-Asset Positioning & Sentiment (2). Global Trade & Demand (3) and Global Growth & Economic Cycles (4) also broadly align to an expansionary outlook.
  • Key tensions exist from the US Labour Market, which is Defensive/Risk-Negative (5) due to rising underemployment. US Inflation & Price Dynamics indicates persistent structural disinflationary pressures (6), contrasting with cyclical growth. The US Growth & Business Cycle is Transitional (7) with conflicting indicators. Global Inflation & FX Dynamics is Late-Cycle/Crowded (8) with widespread extreme currency positioning. US Liquidity & Monetary Conditions are in a fragile balance (9) with contrasting signals.
  • These tensions do not overturn the regime call as the dominant drivers carry higher aggregation weights and conviction, reflecting a stronger consensus on the cyclical expansionary elements, even amidst fragility.

Scenario Balance:

  • The dominant scenario is continued late-cycle growth, supported by resilient credit and global demand, but constrained by US labor market softening and persistent disinflationary pressures. This maintains a fragile balance.
  • Primary upside risk involves a broader, more robust expansion if US labor market signals improve and inflation pressures remain contained, leading to sustained positive momentum.
  • Primary downside risk is a shift towards a slowdown or stress regime if credit conditions tighten, global demand falters, or geopolitical events trigger a broad risk-off sentiment.

What Would Change the Regime:

  • A sustained rise in the Broad U.S. Dollar Index above its 12-month moving average or a reversal in the 'Global Risk-On / Risk-Off Positioning Tone' to Neutral or Risk-Off (3)(8).
  • A rise in the High Yield Option-Adjusted Spread exceeding 50 basis points over three months, or the St. Louis Fed Financial Stress Index (STLFSI4) rising above 0 (1).
  • The Underemployment Risk signal consistently reversing from HOT to Neutral or COOL, or the University of Michigan Consumer Sentiment signal shifting from Bearish to Neutral or Bullish (5).
