Supply and Demand Analyst

Supply & Demand Structural Assessment — Gold

Supply-Side Structure

  • Recycling flows have been structurally muted despite rising gold prices, influenced by increased use of gold as collateral for loans, particularly in India where over 200 tonnes of jewellery were pledged in the formal sector (Gold Outlook 2026, p7-8). This behavior implies a reduced availability of scrap supply, departing from typical price elasticity.
  • Refining capacity expansion faces inherent constraints from low returns on gold bar production and high costs, limiting the supply's responsiveness to robust demand for physical products like fractional gold bars in the US (US Gold Demand Trends | Q3 2025, p3).
  • Secondary market liquidity in the US has diminished as retail investors shifted from net sellers to net buyers, prompting dealers to rely more heavily on refineries for products (US Gold Demand Trends | Q3 2025, p3).

Demand-Side Structure

  • Central bank demand continues as a persistent, above-average driver, primarily from emerging markets whose gold reserves remain significantly below advanced economies. This accumulation is largely policy-driven rather than market conditions, indicating price-insensitivity (Gold Outlook 2026, p4, p8).
  • ETF and institutional investment demand shows strong structural inflows, with global physically backed gold ETFs recording six consecutive months of positive flows and reaching record AUM and holdings (Gold ETF Commentary | November 2025, p1). Asia, particularly China and India, has emerged as a dominant driver of this trend, influenced by local market dynamics and regulatory changes (Gold ETF Commentary | November 2025, p2).
  • US physical consumer demand (jewellery, bar & coin) has declined cyclically in Q3 2025 (US Gold Demand Trends | Q3 2025, p1). However, a segment of retail demand for fractional gold bars remains strong and price-insensitive, with consumers willing to pay higher premiums, suggesting a durable underlying physical interest (US Gold Demand Trends | Q3 2025, p3).
  • Heightened geopolitical and economic uncertainty globally have been a significant driver of investor and central bank demand for gold, leveraging its role as a diversifier and source of stability (Gold Outlook 2026, p3, p4).

Structural Asymmetries

  • Central bank gold accumulation represents a non-economic demand floor, providing persistent, policy-driven support largely independent of market price signals, especially from emerging market countries (Gold Outlook 2026, p8).
  • There is a mismatch between rigid demand for certain physical gold products (e.g., fractional bars in the US, evidenced by willingness to pay higher premiums) and the inelastic supply response from refineries, which are reluctant to expand capacity due to cost and return issues (US Gold Demand Trends | Q3 2025, p3).
  • Muted recycling rates, stemming from gold being increasingly used as collateral, create a structural tightening of secondary supply, reducing its typical price-elasticity despite rising gold prices (Gold Outlook 2026, p7-8).
  • A notable regional divergence in demand leadership is observed, with Asian inflows now dominating ETF and institutional investment, driven by distinct local factors such as equity market weakness, geopolitical tensions, and regulatory changes in China and India (Gold ETF Commentary | November 2025, p2).

Stability vs Change

Area Assessment
Supply Recycling behavior has structurally changed, becoming less price-sensitive due to gold's use as collateral, leading to muted supply despite rising prices. Refinery capacity expansion faces stable, enduring constraints related to costs and returns (Gold Outlook 2026, p7-8)(US Gold Demand Trends | Q3 2025, p3).
Demand Central bank demand remains a structurally stable, policy-driven force. However, the regional leadership of ETF and institutional demand has materially changed, with Asia (especially China and India) now dominating global inflows, driven by distinct local factors (Gold ETF Commentary | November 2025, p1-2). US physical consumer demand is cyclical, yet a price-insensitive segment for fractional bars indicates a stable underlying interest (US Gold Demand Trends | Q3 2025, p1, p3).
Balance The gold market balance is experiencing a structural tightening driven by persistent, non-economic central bank demand and sustained, Asia-led ETF inflows, alongside reduced secondary supply due to muted recycling. This points to a more durable demand-side bias than cyclical fluctuations (Gold ETF Commentary | November 2025, p1)(Gold Outlook 2026, p7-8).
