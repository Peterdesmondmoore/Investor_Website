Supply & Demand Structural Assessment — Gold

Supply-Side Structure

  • Gold recycling flows have been structurally muted despite rising gold prices, influenced by the increased use of gold as collateral for loans, particularly in India. This behavior reduces the price elasticity of secondary physical supply (1).
  • Refineries show hesitation in expanding capacity for gold bar production, citing low returns and higher operating costs, despite elevated gold prices. This creates a logistical constraint, limiting the fulfillment of strong demand for fractional gold products in markets like the US (1).
  • Secondary market liquidity has declined due to a shift in retail investor behavior from net sellers to net buyers. This forces dealers to rely more on refineries for product sourcing, indicating a structural shift in the flow of physical gold to market (1).

Demand-Side Structure

  • Central bank demand remains robust and primarily policy-driven, especially from emerging markets, with reserves still significantly below those of advanced economies. This accumulation acts as a persistent, largely price-insensitive demand source (1).
  • Institutional investment demand, particularly for physically-backed Gold ETFs, has demonstrated sustained strength, recording six consecutive months of inflows. This has led to historic highs in total assets under management (AUM) and holdings, notably driven by Asian investors seeking portfolio diversification amidst geopolitical and economic uncertainty (1).
  • In the US, consumer demand for jewellery, bar, and coin declined year-over-year in Q3 2025, suggesting a degree of price sensitivity, although strong two-way activity (buying met with profit-taking) was observed for bar and coin. Regulatory changes, such as China's VAT reform for jewellery, are observed to structurally shift investment motives towards ETFs (1).

Structural Asymmetries

  • Non-economic demand from central banks establishes a persistent and rigid demand floor, driven by national policy objectives rather than market conditions. This creates a structural bias for accumulation irrespective of price movements (1).
  • Recycling supply, typically expected to be price-elastic, exhibits rigidity due to the increasing use of gold as collateral. This phenomenon effectively sequesters physical gold from the market, creating a persistent mismatch with robust investment demand (1).
  • The reluctance of the refining sector to expand capacity, driven by economic disincentives, creates a structural bottleneck in supply. This limits the ability of the supply side to adequately respond to strong retail investment demand for certain gold products, leading to potential price premiums (1).

Stability vs Change

Area Assessment
Supply The **structural dampening of recycling supply** due to increased collateralization is a material change, reducing its historical price elasticity. **Refinery hesitation to expand capacity** introduces a new, durable constraint. The underlying physical availability is stable, but its market accessibility has structurally tightened.
Demand **Sustained, robust growth in institutional and ETF investment demand**, particularly from Asia, reaching historic highs, represents a material change. **Central bank accumulation remains a stable, persistent, policy-driven feature**. Consumer demand, conversely, appears more cyclical and price-sensitive.
Balance The gold market balance is experiencing a **structural tightening**. This is driven by rigid central bank and strong institutional demand, combined with rigidified recycling supply and refining bottlenecks. This indicates a **sustained structural bias** toward tighter market conditions.
