Morgan’s February target of $6,300 is more than $1,200 higher than its own October 2025 average forecast of $5,055. These aggressive updates indicate that prior estimates, which were largely concentrated in the $4,200 to $5,000 range, have been superseded by rapidly evolving macroeconomic and geopolitical conditions.

Outlook Dispersion: While the target distribution has shifted higher, the outlook remains widely dispersed, signaling some uncertainty regarding the magnitude of the rally. While UBS and J.P. Morgan advocate for targets exceeding $6,000—with UBS even citing a bull-case potential of $7,200—other institutions like BMO Capital Markets maintain more conservative 2026 annual average targets near $4,600. This spread highlights a divergence in opinion regarding how much of the "geopolitical risk premium" will persist throughout the year.

Comparability and Market Data: Interpretation of the dataset is complicated by varying methodologies and horizons. For example, Citi’s $5,000 target is a near-term (0–3 month) projection, whereas WisdomTree uses a model-based quarterly progression ending at $5,020. Key Drivers: The upward bias in current targets is primarily attributed to sustained central bank demand and a recovery in investor flows through ETFs. Analysts also frequently cite "fiscal dominance," concerns over Federal Reserve independence, and the anticipated tailwinds from a sustained interest rate-cutting cycle. These factors are viewed as structural shifts that provide a higher floor for the metal regardless of short-term volatility.

Synthesis: Overall, the institutional consensus has transitioned from a moderate growth outlook to a high-conviction bullish stance, though the breadth of the target range—spanning from $4,600 to $6,300—reflects a highly uncertain environment. The prevailing evidence suggests an upward-biased distribution where recent analyst revisions are consistently outpacing both historical targets and current futures market pricing.
