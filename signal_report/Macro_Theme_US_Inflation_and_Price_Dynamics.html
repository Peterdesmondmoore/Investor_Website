1. Realised Inflation: CPI–PPI Dynamics

As of November 2025, the CPI-PPI Inflation Signal stands at NEUTRAL [Source: cpi_ppi_divergence]. The year-on-year PPI (3.61%) exceeds CPI (2.50%), resulting in a divergence of 1.10% [Source: cpi_ppi_divergence]. The robust z-score for this divergence is 0.25, which remains below the "HOT" threshold of 0.75 [Source: cpi_ppi_divergence].

The PPI-CPI divergence has shown positive momentum, rising from -4.85% in September 2024 to 1.10% in November 2025 [Source: cpi_ppi_divergence]. This indicates some re-emergence of upstream cost-push pressures, but these have not yet transmitted sufficiently to consumer prices to shift the signal beyond a neutral stance [Source: cpi_ppi_divergence]. Historically, a "HOT" signal implies significant cost-push pressure transmitting to consumers, which is not currently observed [Source: cpi_ppi_divergence].

2. Policy-Relevant Inflation: PCE & Core PCE

The Policy-Relevant Inflation (PCE) signal, reflecting an equal-weight composite of headline and core PCE year-over-year inflation, is in a Neutral regime as of September 2025 [Source: policy_relevant_inflation_pce]. Headline PCE registered 2.79% and Core PCE was 2.83% [Source: policy_relevant_inflation_pce]. The composite robust z-score is -0.335789, indicating moderate and stable inflation levels, positioned below historical extremes [Source: policy_relevant_inflation_pce].

Both headline and core PCE z-scores are negative (-0.25 and -0.42 respectively) but have shown an upward trend since April 2025, suggesting a slight re-acceleration from recent lows within the neutral band [Source: policy_relevant_inflation_pce]. These readings suggest inflation is near the central bank’s target range (implied by "Neutral") but does not currently signal sustained above-trend pressure or significant disinflationary risk from a policy perspective [Source: policy_relevant_inflation_pce].

3. Market-Implied Inflation Expectations

Market-Implied Inflation Expectations (MIIE) are currently in a STABLE regime as of December 2025, with a composite z-score of 0.038 [Source: market_implied_inflation_signal]. The 5-Year Forward Inflation Expectation (T5YIE) is 2.29%, and the 10-Year Breakeven Inflation Rate (T10YIE) is 2.25% [Source: market_implied_inflation_signal]. These levels suggest moderate inflation expectations, generally anchored.

Despite the stable regime, recent momentum in expectations is negative [Source: market_implied_inflation_signal]. The 3-month annualised z-scores for both T5YIE (-0.628) and T10YIE (-0.674) are negative, indicating a deceleration in short-term expectations. This negative momentum, offsetting moderate level readings, maintains the STABLE regime [Source: market_implied_inflation_signal]. Market pricing appears broadly consistent with observed PCE trends, but the negative momentum in expectations could foreshadow future shifts.

4. Term-Structure of Inflation

The Inflation Term-Structure signal is in a Neutral regime as of December 2025, with a composite value of -0.322918 [Source: inflation_term_structure]. This indicates that the relative pricing of short-, medium-, and long-term inflation is stable, not signaling strong reflationary or disinflationary pressures [Source: inflation_term_structure].

All primary components contributed negatively to the composite, with the z-scores for 5-year breakeven (-0.366390), 10-year breakeven (-0.425994), and the inverted real yield (-0.161878) all showing mild disinflationary bias [Source: inflation_term_structure]. This profile is consistent with a market not anticipating significant inflationary shocks or sustained policy easing, leaning towards a soft landing or continued normalisation narrative rather than re-acceleration or deep stagnation, within the defined neutral thresholds [Source: inflation_term_structure].

5. Wage Growth, Labour Tightness & Cost-Push Pressures

The Wage Growth and Inflation Composite is currently in a NEUTRAL regime as of October 2025, with a composite z-score of -0.546746 [Source: wage_growth_and_inflation]. This signals moderate disinflationary pressure stemming from labour costs [Source: wage_growth_and_inflation]. Both underlying components, Wage Pressure (z-score -0.357684) and Labor Tightness (z-score -0.735808), are below their historical medians, indicating easing dynamics [Source: wage_growth_and_inflation].

While Wage Pressure showed an upward momentum from January 2025, it stabilized from July to October 2025. Labor Tightness, however, contributes more significantly to the current disinflationary lean, suggesting a normalising or loosening labour market [Source: wage_growth_and_inflation]. These labour dynamics point to a reduction in cost-push inflation pressures, which is consistent with an environment of ongoing disinflation rather than renewed acceleration.

6. Synthesis: US Inflation Regime Assessment

The prevailing US inflation regime, as indicated by the broad Fed Inflation Signal, is COOL [Source: fed_inflation_signal]. This composite reflects a decelerating inflation trend, supported by the negative momentum in market-implied expectations [Source: market_implied_inflation_signal] and easing labour-cost pressures [Source: wage_growth_and_inflation]. Although policy-relevant PCE and CPI-PPI dynamics are currently in a Neutral regime [Source: policy_relevant_inflation_pce] [Source: cpi_ppi_divergence], their z-scores also indicate levels below historical averages, consistent with a disinflationary bias within a broader neutral context. Market expectations for inflation, while stable in absolute terms, show negative short-term momentum across the term structure, further reinforcing the disinflationary narrative.

The overall inflation profile suggests a state of Stable Disinflation with Anchored Expectations. The system indicates that inflation pressures are waning, with labour market contributions decreasing, and forward-looking market measures remaining within a controlled range. 7. Forward Risks

Upside Risks to Inflation

  • Renewed energy or commodity price shocks, which can quickly feed into producer and then consumer prices [Source: cpi_ppi_divergence].
  • Unexpected acceleration in demand, leading to tighter labour markets and renewed wage pressures, particularly if the current stabilisation in wage pressure is temporary [Source: wage_growth_and_inflation].
  • A reversal in the negative momentum observed in market-implied inflation expectations, which could quickly signal a shift in investor sentiment [Source: market_implied_inflation_signal].

Downside Risks to Inflation

  • Faster-than-anticipated normalisation in shelter inflation components, which could further drive down headline and core inflation measures, given their weight in CPI and PCE [Source: fed_inflation_signal] [Source: policy_relevant_inflation_pce].
  • Persistent weakness in global demand or trade, leading to reduced pricing power for producers and a broad disinflationary impulse [Source: cpi_ppi_divergence].
  • Further cooling of the labour market, leading to a sustained deceleration in wage growth and a reduction in cost-push pressures [Source: wage_growth_and_inflation].

8. Investment Interpretation (Informational)

An inflation regime characterized by stable disinflation with anchored expectations generally implies a more benign environment for fixed income, as interest rate increases are less likely. Equities may benefit from stable or growing earnings, provided economic growth remains supportive without significant demand erosion. Real assets and inflation hedges may see reduced tailwinds compared to periods of high or accelerating inflation. This environment tends to favor duration and quality in portfolios, and may temper demand for pure inflation protection.
