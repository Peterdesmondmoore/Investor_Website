2. Why a Simple Taylor-Style Rule Is Insufficient

A rigid Taylor-style policy rule is insufficient in the current macroeconomic regime due to several critical limitations inherent in its mechanical application. Firstly, policy reaction coefficients are time-varying; the relative weights assigned to inflation versus employment mandates shift depending on prevailing economic conditions, historical context, and evolving structural factors. A simple rule fails to adapt to these dynamic trade-offs. Secondly, inflation versus labor market weights themselves are not static and are subject to regime shifts; for instance, the central bank's tolerance for inflation might increase if financial stability risks are elevated, or vice-versa. Thirdly, point estimates, which form the basis of Taylor rules, inherently discount the dominance of risk asymmetry, where tail risks (e.g., severe recession or persistent inflation) matter more than average outcomes. Finally, uncertainty around key inputs (such as the natural rate of unemployment or the neutral interest rate) and the persistence of current regimes render simple averages or historical relationships unreliable for forward guidance. Flexible reaction functions explicitly account for these factors, prioritizing risk management and adaptability over deterministic adherence to static parameters.

Projections Synthesiser

Fed Projections Synthesiser

Inference of the Federal Reserve’s internally prepared future economic state, based exclusively on Fed-authored projections and communications.

1. Fed-Implied Growth Outlook

The Federal Reserve appears to be preparing for a period of ACCELERATING growth in the medium-term, with a MEDIUM level of confidence, while acknowledging near-term moderation. The December 2025 Summary of Economic Projections (SEP) indicates a modest upward revision for real GDP growth in 2025 to 1.7% and a more significant increase to 2.3% for 2026, compared to September's projections (7). This expected pickup is attributed to resilient consumer spending, expanding business fixed investment—particularly in AI-related infrastructure—and supportive fiscal policy (3, p. 6). However, the Beige Book reports for October and November 2025 present a mixed near-term picture, with overall economic activity "little changed" or "declining slightly" across many districts (1, p. 1)(2, p. 1). The temporary impact of a government shutdown in late 2025 is expected to weigh on current quarter activity but be largely offset by higher growth in the subsequent quarter (3, p. 1). Despite the upward revisions in projections, ongoing economic uncertainty and policy-related headwinds temper the overall confidence in the near-term growth trajectory.

2. Fed-Implied Inflation Trajectory

The Fed-implied inflation trajectory is one of STICKY disinflation in the near-term, transitioning to DISINFLATION by the medium-term. The December 2025 SEP projects total PCE inflation at 2.9% for 2025 and 2.4% for 2026, with core PCE following a similar path (3.0% for 2025 and 2.5% for 2026), both gradually converging to the 2.0% longer-run goal by 2028 (7). This reflects a slight downward revision from the September SEP, but still above the 2% target. The persistence of inflation is primarily framed as "transitory" and driven by tariffs impacting goods prices (12, p. 3)(13, p. 2)(3, p. 2). Chair Powell emphasized that goods inflation from tariffs is expected to be a "one-time price increase" that should peak around Q1 2026 and then fade, with non-tariff inflation already in the "low 2s" (3, p. 15, 18). Disinflation in services is noted as continuing (3, p. 2). The Fed remains sensitive to UPSIDE risks to inflation, particularly the possibility of tariff effects becoming more persistent or inflation expectations becoming unanchored (4, p. 8)(5, p. 7)(14, p. 10)(3, p. 23).

3. Fed-Implied Labour Market Trajectory

The Federal Reserve appears to be preparing for a LOOSENING labour market, with a MEDIUM degree of concern expressed about the speed of adjustment. The SEP consistently projects the unemployment rate to remain low (4.5% in 2025, 4.4% in 2026, and 4.2% longer-run) (7). However, qualitative assessments from FOMC statements and press conferences describe the labor market as "gradually cooling," with job gains having "slowed significantly" and the unemployment rate having "edged up" (9)(10)(11). Chair Powell clarified that actual job creation might be near zero or even negative after revisions (3, p. 12, 19). The slowing is attributed to both reduced labor supply (lower immigration, lower participation) and softened demand (12, p. 1-2)(3, p. 2). There is also an acknowledgment that AI technologies may be contributing to reduced labor demand and replacing entry-level positions (2, p. 1)(13, p. 15)(3, p. 20). The Fed's concern is evident in its policy adjustments, aiming to support the labor market as downside risks to employment have risen (9)(10)(11).

4. Dominant Risk Narrative

The dominant risk narrative emphasizes an asymmetric preparation against DOWNSIDE GROWTH/LABOUR RISK, despite persistent upside inflation risks. The FOMC statements consistently highlight that "uncertainty about the economic outlook remains elevated" and that the Committee is "attentive to the risks to both sides of its dual mandate" (8)(9)(10)(11). However, a clear shift occurred, with the September, October, and December statements explicitly stating that "downside risks to employment have risen" (9)(10)(11). This shift in the "balance of risks" has been the primary justification for the three consecutive 25-basis point policy rate reductions since September (9)(10)(11). Chair Powell characterized these cuts as a "risk-management" exercise to move towards a more neutral policy stance, given the increasing concerns about the labor market (12, p. 6)(13, p. 5). The decision in December to initiate purchases of shorter-term Treasury securities to maintain an ample supply of reserves also underscores a focus on financial stability and supporting monetary policy implementation, implicitly addressing potential funding risks (11). While upside inflation risks, particularly from persistent tariff effects, are acknowledged, the immediate policy response indicates a greater emphasis on mitigating risks to employment and economic activity.

Beige Book Summary

Federal Reserve Beige Book Summary — November 2025

Descriptive synthesis of current US economic conditions based on district-level reporting.

National Backdrop

Economic activity was largely unchanged since the previous report, though two Districts observed a modest decline and one noted modest growth (1). Overall consumer spending continued to decline, although higher-end retail spending remained resilient (1). Manufacturing activity increased somewhat across most Districts, despite tariffs and related uncertainty acting as headwinds (1). Revenues in the nonfinancial services sector were mostly flat to down (1). Loan demand was mixed, residential construction declined in some areas while remaining unchanged in others, and home sales activity varied (1). Outlooks were largely unchanged, with some contacts noting an increased risk of slower activity in coming months (1).

Labour Market Conditions

Employment declined slightly, with approximately half of the Districts reporting weaker labor demand (1). Many employers controlled headcounts through hiring freezes, replacement-only hiring, and attrition rather than layoffs (1). Some firms adjusted hours worked in response to business volume, and a few noted that artificial intelligence (AI) reduced demand for entry-level positions or increased worker productivity (1). Worker availability generally improved, though certain skilled positions and a reduced supply of immigrant workers remained challenging to fill (1). Wage growth was modest overall, but sectors such as manufacturing, construction, and health care experienced more moderate wage pressure due to tighter labor supply and rising health insurance premiums (1).

Prices & Cost Pressures

Prices rose moderately during the reporting period (1). Input cost pressures were widespread in manufacturing and retail, frequently attributed to tariff-induced increases for equipment and materials (1). Costs for insurance, utilities, technology, and health care also continued to rise (1). The extent to which businesses passed on higher costs varied, depending on demand, competitive dynamics, and consumer price sensitivity (1). Many firms reported experiencing margin compression or financial strain from tariffs, with some only passing through a small fraction of cost increases (1). Prices declined for certain materials, linked to sluggish demand or reduced tariff rates (1). Contacts largely anticipate persistent upward cost pressures, but plans for near-term price increases were mixed (1).

Sector Themes

  • Consumer activity: Overall consumer spending declined further, with lower- and middle-income households showing increased financial stress and cautious discretionary spending (1). Higher-end retail spending remained resilient (1). Auto sales, especially electric vehicles, saw declines following the expiration of federal tax credits (1). Demand for food assistance increased due to disruptions in SNAP benefits following the government shutdown (1).
  • Manufacturing: Activity increased somewhat nationally, though some Districts like Richmond and Philadelphia reported contractions (1). Tariffs and tariff uncertainty continued to be a significant headwind for planning and competitiveness (1). Demand related to AI data centers provided a boost to some manufacturers in Cleveland and Boston (1). Capital spending plans showed some pick-up, particularly for automation (1).
  • Services: Nonfinancial services revenues were mostly flat to down (1). Demand for professional and business services increased moderately in Cleveland due to tax law changes and evolving data needs (1). Tourism and travel activity reflected little change nationally, with mixed regional reports, and the government shutdown impacting business travel for some (1).
  • Housing & construction: Residential construction declined in some Districts (Philadelphia, Atlanta), remained unchanged in others (Chicago), and home sales varied (1). High mortgage rates continued to hinder residential activity (1). Commercial real estate activity improved slightly, with some recovery in office markets (Boston, New York, Dallas) and sustained demand for data center construction (Cleveland, San Francisco) (1). High construction costs and labor shortages continued to deter development (1).
  • Banking & credit: Loan demand was mixed nationally, with declines observed by small-to-medium sized banks (New York) and in construction and auto segments (Atlanta) (1). Credit standards were mostly unchanged but some tightening occurred for business and commercial mortgages (New York, Philadelphia, St. Louis) (1). Delinquencies remained elevated or increased for certain loan categories and among households and small businesses (New York, Cleveland, Richmond, St. Louis, Dallas) (1).
  • Agriculture & energy: Conditions were largely stable nationally (1). Low oil prices and low crop prices presented challenges for energy and agriculture sectors (Dallas, Kansas City, Minneapolis) (1). Energy industry segments in Atlanta grew moderately, with strong demand for primary energy sources, including LNG, solar, and wind, attributed to data center activity (1).

Regional Dispersion

  • Stronger districts:
    • Boston: Saw slight economic expansion, with renewed strength in home sales and modest growth in manufacturing sales (1).
    • Chicago: Experienced slight increases across most sectors including employment, consumer spending, and manufacturing (1).
    • Cleveland: Noted slight business activity increases driven by professional and business services and a boost to some manufacturers from AI data centers (1).
  • Softer districts:
    • New York: Experienced a modest decline in economic activity, with slight employment falls and consumer spending reductions (1).
    • Philadelphia: Activity was already trending down, with the government shutdown causing further disruption and modest declines across most sectors (1).
    • St. Louis: Economic activity remained unchanged, with demand slowed further by the government shutdown, and a pessimistic outlook due to risks of higher prices and slower activity (1).
    • Dallas: Weakened slightly in nonfinancial services, retail, and banking sectors (1).
    • Kansas City: Saw slightly slowed economic growth, reflecting softer labor and cooling consumer activity (1).
    • Minneapolis: Activity was flat on balance, with slight employment decreases and lower consumer spending (1).
  • Common constraints: Tariffs caused widespread input cost pressures, uncertainty, and margin compression (1). The government shutdown disrupted various economic activities, from consumer spending and SNAP benefits to business operations (1). Higher interest rates and financing conditions continued to hinder housing and commercial investment (1). Labor availability improved overall, but shortages persisted for skilled positions (1).
  • Outliers: AI adoption impacted hiring and productivity across multiple districts (1). Strong demand for data centers spurred manufacturing and energy sector activity in regions like Cleveland, Atlanta, and San Francisco (1). High-end consumer spending and luxury housing markets showed resilience, contrasting with broader softness (New York, Atlanta, Chicago, Minneapolis, San Francisco) (1).

Signals to Watch

  • Growth risks: Contacts noted an increased risk of slower overall activity in the coming months, with outlooks worsening in some districts due to concerns over economic slowdown, tariffs, interest rates, and uncertainty (1). Consumer caution and price sensitivity persist (1).
  • Labour inflections: The shift towards hiring freezes, attrition, and replacement-only hiring over outright layoffs signals easing labor demand (1). The growing influence of AI in replacing entry-level roles and boosting worker productivity could further curb new hiring needs (1).
  • Inflation persistence: Expectations for upward cost pressures to persist, driven by tariffs, insurance, utilities, and healthcare costs, suggest ongoing inflation at the producer level (1). The mixed outlook on firms' plans to raise prices indicates varied pricing power and demand elasticity (1).
  • Capital investment: Business spending intentions are mixed; while some firms delay capital expenditures due to uncertainty, others are increasing investment, particularly in automation and AI infrastructure, or dusting off previously paused expansion plans (1).
  • Financial stress: Rising delinquencies among households and small businesses, coupled with difficulties for small businesses in obtaining credit amidst policy uncertainty and government shutdowns, point to potential financial stress (1). Increased reliance on debt for household expenses and elevated demand for social services further highlight these vulnerabilities (1).
