Macro Signal Synthesiser

Conditions-based macro assessment for Federal Reserve policy justification.

1. Macro Conditions Summary

The current US macroeconomic landscape presents a nuanced and increasingly disinflationary picture, underpinned by a labor market showing signs of cooling and monetary conditions shifting towards ease. Inflation dynamics are mixed: while the broader Fed Inflation Signal registers a COOL regime (1), policy-relevant PCE measures remain Neutral (2), and market-implied expectations are STABLE (3). A notable shift is observed in the CPI-PPI divergence, which has moved to NEUTRAL, indicating a potential re-emergence of pipeline cost pressures (4).

The labor market, while broadly Neutral, shows incremental softening. The Explicit Slack Gap is NEUTRAL and STABLE with MEDIUM confidence (5). However, underlying signals point to increasing slack, with the Employment Composition nearing a COOL regime (6), rising U-6 underemployment (7), and a Bearish Labor Market Turnover Differential (8). Wage growth pressures are Neutral, primarily due to easing labor tightness (9).

Credit conditions are predominantly Neutral, with corporate spreads in a NORMAL regime (10) and private credit impulse Stable (11). However, this benign outlook is contradicted by a Bearish standalone Financial Stress Index (12), signaling underlying systemic tension. Monetary conditions are easing, with the Federal Reserve Liquidity Composite in an Expansionary regime (13) driven by ON RRP unwinds and M2 money supply growth turning Bullish (14). The real-rate regime is Neutral (15), and the policy stance relative to the Neutral Rate (r*) is NEAR NEUTRAL, with r* drift STABLE (16).

Cross-asset positioning indicates a NORMAL VIX volatility regime (17) but with significant sector-specific crowding, particularly in Metals (speculative long crowded) and Rates (speculative short crowded). There is High Squeeze Risk across futures markets (18), and the dominant macro positioning theme is 'Supply Stress' (19), implying underlying physical market tightness. Despite this, overall speculative vs. hedger divergence is Normal, with the exception of extreme divergence in Fed Funds futures (20).

Forward-looking, the primary tension lies between persistent, albeit moderating, inflation (due to neutral core PCE and shifting CPI-PPI) and a visibly cooling labor market, all within an easing monetary policy framework. Downside growth risks are emerging from labor market slack and an elevated financial stress index, while global growth proxies show a "Demand_Tailwind" (21) and improving forward cycle momentum from Rates CoT flows (22).

2. Why a Simple Taylor-Style Rule Is Insufficient

A mechanical Taylor-style rule is insufficient in the current regime due to several critical limitations that flexible reaction functions aim to address. Firstly, policy reaction coefficients are inherently time-varying; the relative importance of inflation versus employment gaps shifts depending on the prevailing economic state, such as periods of high uncertainty or supply-side shocks. Secondly, the explicit weights policymakers assign to inflation and labor objectives are not static and often adjust by regime, reflecting dynamic assessments of economic stability and mandates. Thirdly, point estimates, as utilized in simple rules, fail to capture the crucial element of risk asymmetry, where the costs of undershooting or overshooting policy targets may be disproportionately high on one side (e.g., downside growth risks versus upside inflation risks). Finally, the current environment is marked by significant uncertainty and the importance of regime persistence, making simple average-based rules prone to frequent, potentially destabilizing, reversals. A flexible approach, conversely, integrates these qualitative and forward-looking dimensions, prioritizing signal persistence and tail risk management over rigid, backward-looking calculations.

Projections Synthesiser

Inference of the Federal Reserve’s internally prepared future economic state, based exclusively on Fed-authored projections and communications.

1. Fed-Implied Growth Outlook

The Federal Reserve appears to be preparing for an ACCELERATING growth trajectory in the NEAR-TERM, followed by a moderation to a stable long-run pace. The December 2025 Summary of Economic Projections (SEP) indicates that real GDP growth is projected to rise from a median of 1.7 percent in 2025 to 2.3 percent in 2026, before settling at 2.0 percent in 2027, 1.9 percent in 2028, and a longer-run rate of 1.8 percent (2). This marks an upward revision for 2025 and, notably, for 2026 growth compared to the September 2025 SEP (1)(2).

Chair Powell clarified that this anticipated pickup in growth is partly driven by resilient consumer spending and robust business fixed investment, particularly in AI infrastructure (Chair Powell's Press Conference Transcript — December 10, 2025). He noted that economic activity has been expanding at a moderate pace, with consumer spending remaining solid and business investment continuing to expand (Chair Powell's Press Conference Transcript — December 10, 2025). While housing sector activity remains weak, the temporary effects of a government shutdown in the current quarter are expected to be largely offset by higher growth in the subsequent quarter (Chair Powell's Press Conference Transcript — December 10, 2025). The outlook's confidence is assessed as MEDIUM to HIGH, given the upward revisions and identified drivers, but some districts still report elevated uncertainty (3)(4).

2. Fed-Implied Inflation Trajectory

The Fed projects a continued DISINFLATION path toward its 2 percent target over the MEDIUM-TERM. The December SEP shows median total PCE inflation declining from 2.9 percent in 2025 to 2.4 percent in 2026, reaching 2.1 percent in 2027 and 2.0 percent by 2028 (2). This trajectory is a slight downward revision for 2025 and 2026 compared to the September SEP, implying a somewhat faster disinflationary path (1)(2).

Inflation is currently described as "somewhat elevated," with total PCE at 2.8 percent and core PCE at 2.8 percent for the 12 months ending September (Chair Powell's Press Conference Transcript — December 10, 2025)(Chair Powell's Press Conference Transcript — October 29, 2025). The pickup in goods inflation is primarily attributed to tariff effects, which are expected to be a "one-time shift in the price level" that will peak around Q1 2026 and then dissipate (Chair Powell's Press Conference Transcript — December 10, 2025). Disinflation in services is noted as continuing (Chair Powell's Press Conference Transcript — December 10, 2025). Near-term inflation expectations have declined from their peaks earlier in the year, while longer-term expectations remain "well anchored" at the 2 percent goal (Chair Powell's Press Conference Transcript — December 10, 2025).

The Fed remains SENSITIVE TO UPSIDE RISKS for inflation, particularly if tariff-induced price increases prove more persistent or if longer-term inflation expectations become unanchored due to the prolonged period above target (Chair Powell's Press Conference Transcript — December 10, 2025)(FOMC Minutes of the Federal Open Market Committee, July 29–30, 2025)(9)(11).

3. Fed-Implied Labour Market Trajectory

The Fed is preparing for a LOOSENING labour market trajectory, characterized by a "gradual cooling." The median unemployment rate in the December SEP is projected to remain at 4.5 percent in 2025, then decline slightly to 4.4 percent in 2026 and 4.2 percent by 2027-2028 (2). This path is unchanged from the September SEP (1).

The Committee acknowledges that "job gains have slowed significantly" and that "downside risks to employment have risen" (6)(8). This slowdown is attributed to both a sharp decline in labor supply—due to lower immigration and reduced labor force participation—and softening labor demand (Chair Powell's Press Conference Transcript — December 10, 2025)(Chair Powell's Press Conference Transcript — October 29, 2025). Wage growth continues at a modest pace (Chair Powell's Press Conference Transcript — December 10, 2025). There is increasing discussion and concern regarding the impact of AI technologies potentially reducing labor demand, particularly for entry-level positions, and allowing higher productivity with fewer workers (Chair Powell's Press Conference Transcript — December 10, 2025)(Chair Powell's Press Conference Transcript — October 29, 2025).

The SPEED OF ADJUSTMENT is described as "gradual cooling," with current layoffs and hiring both remaining low (Chair Powell's Press Conference Transcript — December 10, 2025). The DEGREE OF CONCERN EXPRESSED is MEDIUM, with the Committee's shift in policy bias reflecting increased attention to downside employment risks (6).

4. Dominant Risk Narrative

The *DOMINANT RISK NARRATIVE* is one of a challenging and unusual policy tension, with significant and opposing risks to the Federal Reserve's dual mandate. The Fed explicitly states that "risks to inflation are tilted to the upside and risks to employment to the downside—a challenging situation" (Chair Powell's Press Conference Transcript — December 10, 2025)(Chair Powell's Press Conference Transcript — October 29, 2025)(Chair Powell's Press Conference Transcript — September 17, 2025).

There is EXPLICIT EMPHASIS on DOWNSIDE GROWTH/LABOUR RISK, driven by the observed cooling in the labor market, slowing job gains, and rising unemployment rate, which has prompted recent rate cuts as "risk management" (Chair Powell's Press Conference Transcript — September 17, 2025)(Chair Powell's Press Conference Transcript — October 29, 2025). The Committee has noted "downside risks to employment have risen" (6). Concurrently, UPSIDE INFLATION RISK remains a significant concern, stemming from the potential for tariff effects to be more persistent than anticipated and the possibility of longer-term inflation expectations becoming unanchored due to a prolonged period of above-target inflation (Chair Powell's Press Conference Transcript — September 17, 2025)(Chair Powell's Press Conference Transcript — December 10, 2025)(9)(10)(11).

The Fed recognizes a "no risk-free path" for policy (Chair Powell's Press Conference Transcript — December 10, 2025) and observes a "bifurcated economy" where lower-income households are struggling with affordability while higher-income consumers remain resilient, adding complexity to the risk assessment (Chair Powell's Press Conference Transcript — October 29, 2025)(Chair Powell's Press Conference Transcript — December 10, 2025). The recent government shutdown has also introduced near-term uncertainty regarding economic data availability and the overall outlook (Chair Powell's Press Conference Transcript — October 29, 2025). The policy approach emphasizes a careful balancing of these dual-mandate tensions (12).

Beige Book Summary

Publication Date — November 2025

Descriptive synthesis of current US economic conditions based on district-level reporting.

National Backdrop

Overall economic activity was little changed since the previous report, with most Federal Reserve Districts noting stable conditions, though two reported a modest decline and one observed modest growth (1). Consumer spending saw a further decline, although higher-end retail remained resilient (1). Manufacturing activity increased somewhat across most Districts, despite ongoing headwinds from tariffs and associated uncertainty (1). Revenues in the nonfinancial services sector were largely flat to down, and residential construction activity varied, with declines in some areas and stability in others (1). Conditions in the agriculture and energy sectors remained broadly stable, but concerns persisted regarding low prices for oil and certain crops (1). Community organizations reported increased demand for assistance, partly due to disruptions from a government shutdown (1).

Labour Market Conditions

Employment levels declined slightly across the nation, with approximately half of the Districts reporting weaker labor demand (1). While there was an uptick in layoff announcements, most contacts indicated a preference for limiting headcounts through hiring freezes, attrition, and replacement-only hiring rather than outright layoffs (1). Several firms, including some in Cleveland and San Francisco, noted that artificial intelligence was replacing entry-level positions or boosting worker productivity, thereby reducing the need for new hires (1). Employers generally found it easier to secure workers, though specific skilled positions and a reduced availability of immigrant labor continued to pose recruitment challenges across multiple Districts, such as Philadelphia and St. Louis (1). Wage growth proceeded at a modest pace overall, but sectors like manufacturing, construction, and health care in Districts like Boston and Cleveland experienced more pronounced wage pressures due to tighter labor supply and rising health insurance premiums (1). Many firms in Kansas City anticipate employment to hold steady in the near term, with hiring expected to pick up in 2026 (1).

Prices & Cost Pressures

Prices rose moderately during the reporting period, with a slight easing in the pace of increase in some Districts like New York, but remaining elevated (1). Input cost pressures were widespread, particularly in manufacturing and retail, largely attributed to tariff-induced increases (1). Several Districts, including Boston, New York, and Cleveland, also cited rising costs for insurance, utilities, technology, and health care (1). The ability to pass through these higher input costs to customers varied significantly, depending on demand strength, competitive pressures, and consumer price sensitivity, leading to multiple reports of margin compression or financial strain for firms, especially in Philadelphia and Dallas (1). Prices for certain materials, such as eggs in Boston and wallboard in San Francisco, declined due to sluggish demand, deferred tariff implementation, or reduced tariff rates (1). Contacts generally anticipated upward cost pressures to persist in the coming months, but plans to raise prices in the near term remained mixed (1).

Sector Themes

  • Consumer activity: Consumer spending declined further overall, although higher-end retail remained resilient, as noted in New York and Atlanta (1). Lower and middle-income consumers displayed increasing signs of financial stress and caution with discretionary spending, impacting sales in Cleveland, Minneapolis, and San Francisco (1). EV sales saw declines in some Districts like New York and St. Louis following the expiration of federal tax credits (1).
  • Manufacturing: Activity increased somewhat, with Districts like Chicago and Dallas reporting modest growth in production and orders, particularly in durable goods and transportation equipment (1). However, tariffs and tariff uncertainty continued to be a significant headwind for manufacturers in Districts like Philadelphia, Richmond, and Dallas (1). Demand related to AI infrastructure investments provided a boost to manufacturers in Boston and Cleveland (1).
  • Services: Revenues in the nonfinancial services sector were mostly flat to down, with some Districts like New York reporting moderate declines in business services and leisure/hospitality (1). Professional and business services saw moderate growth in Cleveland, partly due to tax law changes and AI adoption (1). Nonprofits across multiple Districts, including Atlanta and St. Louis, reported increased demand for food assistance due to financial distress and the government shutdown (1).
  • Housing & construction: Residential construction declined in some Districts, such as Philadelphia and Dallas, while remaining unchanged in others like Chicago (1). Home sales activity varied, with Boston reporting renewed strength due to lower mortgage rates, while St. Louis noted sales had not met expectations (1). Commercial real estate activity improved slightly overall, with office markets showing recovery in Boston and New York, driven by improved demand and declining vacancies (1). High construction costs and labor shortages continued to deter development in some areas (1).
  • Banking & credit: Loan demand was mixed, declining overall in Districts like New York and Dallas, but increasing moderately in Cleveland (1). Credit standards were largely unchanged but tightened for business loans and commercial mortgages in New York (1). Delinquencies improved in New York but remained elevated, and some Districts, including Philadelphia, St. Louis, Richmond, and Dallas, reported ongoing or increasing delinquencies, particularly among households and small businesses (1).
  • Agriculture & energy: Conditions were largely stable, but challenges from low prices impacted oil and some crops in Districts like Kansas City and Dallas (1). Agricultural conditions remained weak in Minneapolis despite strong harvests, while St. Louis reported strains from low Mississippi River water levels (1). Energy industry segments in Atlanta grew moderately, with strong demand for primary energy sources, particularly LNG, solar, and wind, attributed to data center activity (1).
