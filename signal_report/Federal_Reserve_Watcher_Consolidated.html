1. Macro Conditions Summary

The current US macroeconomic landscape presents a nuanced and increasingly disinflationary picture, with signals suggesting a gradual moderation in economic activity and an easing of inflationary pressures. The overall Fed Inflation Signal is in a COOL regime (1), driven by weak inflation momentum, although policy-relevant PCE measures remain Neutral (2) and market-implied expectations are STABLE (3). A key tension arises from the CPI-PPI divergence, which has normalized to NEUTRAL, hinting at potential re-emerging pipeline pressures (4).

The US labor market is characterized as Neutral with Underlying Cooling Tendencies, reflected by an Explicit Slack Gap that is NEUTRAL and STABLE with MEDIUM confidence (5). While aggregate tightness measures are neutral (6), underlying signals such as rising underemployment (U-6) (7) and a Bearish Labor Market Turnover Differential (8) indicate increasing slack and weakening worker confidence.

US Credit Conditions are largely Neutral/Normal (9) with historically low corporate spreads (10), but an important divergence is noted by the standalone Financial Stress Index (STLFSI4) which is Bearish, signaling elevated systemic tension (11). Private credit impulse is Stable (12).

Monetary conditions are predominantly easing, with the Federal Reserve Liquidity Composite in an Expansionary regime due to significant ON RRP unwinds (13)(14). M2 money supply growth is Bullish (15), and the real-rate regime is Neutral (16), having stabilized after a period of decline. The policy stance is NEAR NEUTRAL relative to the Explicit Neutral Rate (r*), with the r* drift assessed as STABLE (17).

Cross-asset positioning indicates a bifurcated market. While the VIX Volatility Signal is NORMAL (18), there is High Squeeze Risk across futures markets (19), and a dominant Macro Positioning Theme of Supply Stress is ACTIVE (20). Extreme speculative long positioning in Fed Funds futures against deep hedger shorts suggests an elevated risk profile for policy expectations (21).

The US Growth & Business Cycle signal was not explicitly provided in the current theme reports, presenting a data gap for a comprehensive domestic growth assessment. However, global growth context shows a "Resilient Mid-Cycle with Metals-Led Tailwinds and Emerging Rate Support" (22), driven by industrial demand for metals (23).

2. Why a Simple Taylor-Style Rule Is Insufficient

A simple Taylor-style rule, which prescribes a mechanical policy rate based on inflation and output gaps, is insufficient for guiding policy in the current complex macroeconomic regime. Such rules inherently assume stable economic relationships and policy coefficients, which are demonstrably time-varying. The relative weights assigned to inflation versus labor market mandates frequently shift depending on the prevailing economic regime, often in response to unforeseen shocks or structural changes. Furthermore, mechanical rules fail to adequately incorporate risk asymmetry, where the costs of one type of error (e.g., overtightening into a recession) may substantially outweigh the costs of another (e.g., allowing temporary inflation overshoots). In an environment characterized by heightened uncertainty and the importance of regime persistence, policy requires a flexible reaction function that prioritizes decisive evidence of regime shifts over point estimates, accommodates non-linearities, and considers the potential for tail risks to dominate average outcomes.

Projections Synthesiser

Inference of the Federal Reserve’s internally prepared future economic state, based exclusively on Fed-authored projections and communications.

1. Fed-Implied Growth Outlook

The Federal Reserve appears to be preparing for a future state where economic growth, while having moderated in the near-term, re-accelerates in the medium-term. The Summary of Economic Projections (SEP) from December 2025 (14) projects real GDP to rise from a median of 1.7% in 2025 to 2.3% in 2026, then settle at 2.0% in 2027 and 1.9% in 2028, consistent with its longer-run projection of 1.8% (14). This marks an upward revision for 2026 growth compared to the September 2025 SEP (13). Chair Powell clarified that, adjusting for the temporary impact of the government shutdown, the underlying growth outlook for 2025 and 2026 would be 1.9% and 2.1% respectively (12). This re-acceleration is partly attributed to resilient consumer spending and strong business investment, particularly in AI infrastructure (12). However, Beige Book reports (1)(2) indicate economic activity has been "little changed" or "modestly declined" in recent months, with diverse regional outlooks ranging from expected upticks in 6-12 months to continued elevated uncertainty and downside risks from policy events like the government shutdown (1). Confidence in this outlook is Medium, as Chair Powell acknowledges forecasting difficulties and the impact of data limitations due to the shutdown on granular understanding of the economy (11).

2. Fed-Implied Inflation Trajectory

The Fed appears to be preparing for a path of continued **disinflation** over the medium-term, but acknowledges **sticky** elevated levels in the near-term, primarily due to tariff effects. The December 2025 SEP (14) projects total PCE inflation to decline from a median of 2.9% in 2025 to 2.4% in 2026, reaching 2.1% in 2027 and the 2.0% longer-run target in 2028. Core PCE inflation follows a similar downward path (14). The framing emphasizes that tariff-induced price increases are likely a "one-time shift in the price level" rather than an "ongoing inflation problem" (10)(11), though Chair Powell stresses the importance of careful monitoring to prevent these effects from becoming entrenched (10). Tariffs are expected to cause further upward pressure into Q1 2026, with inflation from goods then declining in the latter half of 2026, and "non-tariff inflation" already near the 2% target (12). The Fed's overall sensitivity remains high, with risks to the inflation forecast consistently viewed as **skewed to the upside** (7)(8)(9), particularly concerning the persistence of tariff effects and the potential for longer-term inflation expectations to become unanchored if inflation remains above target for an extended period (9). Longer-term inflation expectations, however, are explicitly stated to be "well anchored" at 2% (15).

3. Fed-Implied Labour Market Trajectory

The Federal Reserve appears to be preparing for a **gradual loosening** of the labor market, moving from solid conditions earlier in the year to a "less dynamic and somewhat softer labor market" (10). Employment levels were largely stable to declining slightly in recent weeks (1)(2), with job gains slowing significantly (e.g., to 29,000 per month in the three months to August (10)). The unemployment rate has edged up (to 4.3% in August (10) and 4.4% in September (12)), though it remains low overall. The speed of adjustment is anticipated to be gradual, with staff projections in the October minutes (9) expecting the unemployment rate to decline gradually after this year. While there are anecdotal reports of AI-driven job cuts and hiring freezes from some large companies (11)(12), aggregate layoff numbers have not sharply increased (11). Reduced labor supply due to lower immigration and participation rates also contributes to slower job creation (10)(11). The Fed expresses a **high degree of concern** about the labor market's trajectory, particularly the increasing "downside risks to employment" (4)(5)(6). Chair Powell highlighted concerns for economically vulnerable groups, including younger Americans and minorities (10), and the potential for a "K-shaped economy" where lower-income households face increasing struggles (11)(12). This heightened concern for employment is a primary driver for the recent policy rate adjustments (10).

4. Dominant Risk Narrative

The dominant risk narrative the Federal Reserve appears to be preparing for is the **challenging tension between elevated upside inflation risks and increasing downside employment risks**, with recent policy decisions indicating a proactive stance to mitigate the latter. While the Fed continues to assess inflation risks as "skewed to the upside" (9) due to the potential persistence of tariff effects and the risk of unanchored long-term inflation expectations, the Committee has explicitly noted a "shift in the balance of risks" (4) towards employment concerns since September. This is evidenced by slower job gains, an edging up of the unemployment rate, and a "less dynamic and somewhat softer labor market" (10). The **asymmetric preparation** is evident in the three consecutive policy rate reductions (September, October, and December 2025) (4)(5)(6), which Chair Powell described as "risk-management cuts" to move policy towards a more neutral stance (10)(11). The goal is to prevent a "sharper downturn" in the labor market (12). Additionally, the decision to halt quantitative tightening (QT) and initiate purchases of shorter-term Treasury securities in December (6) reflects a proactive measure to maintain ample reserves and support smooth market functioning, thereby mitigating potential financial stability risks. The Committee emphasizes a "balanced approach" to its dual mandate and stresses that policy is "not on a preset course," with future decisions being meeting-by-meeting and data-dependent, especially given uncertainties like the government shutdown's impact on data availability (12).

Beige Book Summary

Publication Date — November 2025

Descriptive synthesis of current US economic conditions based on district-level reporting.(1)

National Backdrop

Overall economic activity remained largely unchanged since the previous report, with a majority of Federal Reserve Districts noting stable conditions, while two observed modest declines and one reported modest growth (1). Consumer spending saw a further decline, although higher-end retail remained resilient, with some retailers attributing negative impacts to the government shutdown and auto dealers noting decreased EV sales after federal tax credits expired (1). Manufacturing activity increased somewhat across most Districts, despite ongoing headwinds from tariffs and associated uncertainty (1). Revenues in the nonfinancial services sector were mostly flat to down, and residential construction activity varied by region, with some declines and some stable reports (1). Loan demand was mixed, while office real estate saw signs of recovery (1). The outlooks generally remained unchanged, though some contacts noted an increased risk of slower activity in the coming months, while manufacturers expressed some optimism (1).

Labour Market Conditions

Employment levels declined slightly overall, with roughly half of the Districts reporting weaker labor demand (1). While there was an uptick in layoff announcements, most contacts opted to limit headcounts through hiring freezes, replacement-only hiring, and attrition rather than widespread layoffs (1). Several employers adjusted hours worked to match business volume, and a few firms noted that artificial intelligence was replacing entry-level positions or boosting productivity, thus curbing new hiring (1). Worker availability improved across most Districts, making it easier to find employees, though challenges persisted in securing skilled workers and amidst fewer immigrant workers (1). Wage growth generally maintained a modest pace, but sectors like manufacturing, construction, and health care experienced more moderate wage pressures due to tighter labor supply and rising health insurance premiums contributing to overall labor costs (1).

Prices & Cost Pressures

Prices rose moderately during the reporting period, with input cost pressures noted as widespread in manufacturing and retail, often attributed to tariffs (1). Additionally, several Districts cited rising costs for insurance, utilities, technology, and health care (1). The ability to pass through these higher costs to customers varied significantly, depending on demand, competitive pressures, and consumer price sensitivity, leading to multiple reports of margin compression for firms facing financial strain from tariffs (1). Conversely, prices for certain materials declined, which contacts attributed to sluggish demand, deferred tariff implementation, or reduced tariff rates (1). Looking ahead, contacts largely anticipate that upward cost pressures will persist, though plans to raise selling prices in the near term remained mixed (1).

Sector Themes

  • Consumer activity: Overall consumer spending declined further, with lower- and middle-income consumers notably tightening their discretionary spending (1). Higher-end retail spending remained resilient, as observed in New York (1). EV sales saw declines following the expiration of federal tax credits across several Districts (1).
  • Manufacturing: Activity increased somewhat across most Districts, although tariffs and trade policy uncertainty continued to be a headwind (1). Manufacturers in Cleveland reported a boost from AI data centers (1). Production growth continued modestly in Dallas, while some firms in Philadelphia anticipated increasing future capital expenditures (1).
  • Services: Nonfinancial services revenues were mostly flat to down (1). Professional and business services saw increased demand in Cleveland, partly due to tax policy changes and evolving data needs (1). Leisure and hospitality activity was mixed, with some areas reporting cautious discretionary spending (1).
  • Housing & construction: Residential construction activity saw declines in some Districts and was unchanged in others, with home sales varying (1). Commercial real estate activity picked up slightly, with an ongoing recovery in the office market noted across Districts like Boston and New York (1). High construction costs, influenced by labor shortages and tariffs, continued to deter development (1).
  • Banking & credit: Loan demand was mixed overall, with declines reported in consumer and commercial & industrial lending in Dallas, but an uptick in residential mortgages in New York (1). Credit standards were largely unchanged but some tightening was reported for business loans and commercial mortgages in New York and by lenders in St. Louis and Dallas (1). Delinquencies improved for some loan types but remained elevated (1).
  • Agriculture & energy: Conditions in agriculture and energy sectors were largely stable, though both faced challenges from low-price environments for oil and some crops (1). Kansas City reported oil and gas activity declining slightly as oil prices fell below profitable levels, while Atlanta noted strong demand for primary energy sources, particularly LNG, solar, and wind, attributed to data center activity (1).

Regional Dispersion

  • Stronger districts:

    • Boston: Experienced slight expansion in economic activity, marked by renewed strength in home sales, with an overall cautiously optimistic outlook (1).

    • Chicago: Noted slight increases across employment, consumer spending, business spending, construction, real estate, and manufacturing, coupled with some loosening of financial conditions (1).

    • Cleveland: Reported a slight increase in business activity, moderate growth in professional and business services, and a boost to manufacturing from AI data centers (1).

  • Softer districts:

    • New York: Economic activity declined modestly, with slight employment decreases and a moderate decline in the service sector (1).

    • Philadelphia: Activity was trending down even before the government shutdown, which further spurred modest declines across most sectors, alongside falling employment levels (1).

    • St. Louis: Economic activity and employment remained unchanged, but contacts reported a slowdown in demand, amplified by the government shutdown, leading to a pessimistic outlook (1).

    • Dallas: Showed slight weakening in nonfinancial services, retail, and banking, with a general worsening of outlooks due to various economic concerns (1).

  • Common constraints:

    • Tariffs and Trade Policy Uncertainty: Frequently cited as a headwind for manufacturing and retail across Districts like New York, Philadelphia, Cleveland, and Dallas (1).

    • Government Shutdown Impacts: Disrupted consumer purchases, social services, and business activity in multiple regions, including Philadelphia, St. Louis, and Kansas City (1).

    • Consumer Price Sensitivity: Particularly among lower- and middle-income households, leading to cautious discretionary spending and a search for discounts in Cleveland, Philadelphia, and San Francisco (1).

  • Outliers:

    • New York: Despite overall modest declines, higher-end retail and housing markets demonstrated resilience (1).

    • Philadelphia: Noted a shift towards part-time work, with employees returning to supplement wages at restaurant jobs (1).

    • Kansas City: Firms reported only passing through approximately 20 percent of input cost increases to customers, indicating limited pricing power (1).

Signals to Watch

  • Growth risks: The bifurcation in consumer spending, with higher-income households remaining resilient while lower- and middle-income segments tighten their belts, suggests potential shifts in overall demand momentum (1). Ongoing uncertainties related to tariffs and trade policy continue to pose headwinds for manufacturing and retail activity (1).
  • Labour inflections: The increased reliance on hiring freezes, attrition, and AI to manage headcounts, coupled with easier worker availability for general positions but persistent difficulty for skilled roles, indicates a softening labor market with specific structural adjustments (1).
  • Inflation persistence: Widespread and robust input cost pressures, particularly from tariffs, insurance, and health care, suggest that while some selling price increases are being absorbed by firms, the underlying cost structure remains elevated and may lead to renewed price pass-through if demand conditions allow (1).
  • Capital investment: Mixed signals on business capital spending, with some firms delaying investments due to tight margins and economic uncertainty, while others, particularly in manufacturing, are planning future expenditures or dusting off expansion plans, suggesting a cautious but potentially shifting investment tone (1).
  • Financial stress: Rising delinquencies among households and small businesses, increasing reliance on debt to cover essential expenses, and tightening credit standards by lenders for certain loan categories, point to emerging signs of financial stress, particularly for vulnerable segments (1).
