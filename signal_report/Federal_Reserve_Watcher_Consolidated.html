This suggests that while a broad risk-off tone is absent, specific areas are vulnerable to sharp reversals or flow-driven unwinds.

2. Why a Simple Taylor-Style Rule Is Insufficient

A rigid, mechanical application of a simple Taylor-style rule is insufficient in the current macroeconomic regime due to several critical factors. Firstly, policy reaction coefficients are inherently time-varying; the sensitivity of policy to inflation or employment gaps changes with structural shifts in the economy, such as the persistence of supply-side inflation or evolving labor market dynamics. Secondly, the Federal Reserve's dual mandate often necessitates shifting weights between its inflation and labor objectives depending on which pillar faces greater deviation or risk. In a flexible reaction function, this adaptive weighting becomes paramount, contrasting with the fixed coefficients of a simple rule.

Thirdly, risk asymmetry significantly dominates point estimates. A Taylor rule, based on average forecasts, fails to account for the differential costs of upside versus downside risks. For instance, the downside risks to growth stemming from a rapidly softening labor market may warrant a more pre-emptive response than a modest overshoot in inflation, particularly when financial stability risks are also emerging. Lastly, high uncertainty and regime persistence matter more than precise average forecasts. Policymakers must assess the confidence in signals, potential for regime shifts, and the inertia of existing trends, rather than reacting to every data fluctuation. A simple rule struggles to incorporate these qualitative, forward-looking judgments, which are crucial for navigating complex and dynamic economic landscapes.

Projections Synthesiser

Inference of the Federal Reserve’s internally prepared future economic state, based exclusively on Fed-authored projections and communications.

1. Fed-Implied Growth Outlook

The Federal Reserve appears to be preparing for a **STABLE to slightly ACCELERATING** growth outlook in the **NEAR-TERM** to **MEDIUM-TERM**. The Summary of Economic Projections (SEP) from December 2025 shows a modest upward revision for 2026 real GDP growth (2.3%) compared to the September 2025 projection (1.8%) ( 3 , Table 1)( 8 , Table 1). Chair Powell, in his December 10, 2025, press conference, attributed this stronger outlook to resilient consumer spending and continued investment in AI-related business activities, noting that outside forecasts also anticipate a pickup in growth ( 3 , p.6). Vice Chair Jefferson, in remarks on January 16, 2026, expressed a "cautiously optimistic" view, highlighting a "sharp acceleration" in Q3 2025 GDP to an annual rate of 4.3% and expecting the economy to expand at a "solid pace" in the near term, excluding government shutdown effects ( 14 , p.2-3). While the October and November 2025 Beige Books indicated overall economic activity was "little changed" or "moderated" across districts ( 26 , p.1 for Oct and Nov), some regional contacts expressed optimism for an uptick in demand over the subsequent 6 to 12 months ( 26 , Oct p.1). The Federal Open Market Committee (FOMC) statements in late 2025 and early 2026 consistently noted that economic activity was expanding at a "moderate" or "solid pace" ( 5 , p.1)( 2 , p.1)( 17 , p.1). The confidence in this growth outlook is **MEDIUM to HIGH**.

2. Fed-Implied Inflation Trajectory

The Federal Reserve appears to be preparing for a **DISINFLATION** path, aiming to return inflation to its 2% target, despite it remaining "somewhat elevated" in the **NEAR-TERM** ( 17 , p.1). The December 2025 SEP projects median PCE inflation to gradually decline from 2.9% in 2025 to 2.0% by 2028 and over the longer run ( 3 , Table 1). Chair Powell consistently framed the elevated goods inflation, driven by tariffs, as a "one-time price increase" that is "short-lived" and expected to peak in Q1 2026 before declining in the latter half of the year ( 3 , p.15)( 5 , p.2)( 8 , p.3). He also highlighted continuing disinflation in services ( 3 , p.2). Vice Chair Jefferson echoed this view, expecting tariff effects to be "short-lived" and inflation to return to target ( 14 , p.5). Governor Miran contended that "underlying inflation" (when excluding shelter and imputed prices) is already significantly closer to target, and he anticipates a "faster fall in PCE shelter inflation" ( 15 , p.3, Figure 8). The overall framing underscores a **transitory framing** for current inflationary pressures, with **MEDIUM** sensitivity to upside risks related to the persistence of tariff effects ( 4 , p.14).

3. Fed-Implied Labour Market Trajectory

The Federal Reserve appears to be preparing for a **LOOSENING** labour market trajectory ( 5 , p.1). While the unemployment rate remains relatively low (median 4.5% for 2025, 4.4% for 2026 in the December SEP) ( 3 , Table 1), job gains have "slowed significantly" ( 3 , p.2) and are potentially negative after adjusting for overcounting ( 3 , p.12). The **speed of adjustment** is characterized as "gradually cooling" ( 3 , p.1) but persistent, with a "less dynamic and somewhat softer labor market" showing low hiring and low layoffs ( 14 , p.3). The **degree of concern expressed is HIGH**, with multiple officials highlighting "downside risks to employment" as having risen ( 5 , p.1). Vice Chair Bowman described the labor market as "increasingly more fragile" ( 13 , p.2), and Governor Waller emphasized a "substantial deterioration" in job growth for 2025, posing a "significant risk" ( 18 , p.1). Factors contributing to this softening include declining labor force growth (due to lower immigration and participation) and reduced demand, with AI potentially contributing to job reallocations ( 3 , p.18, 20)( 26 , Nov p.1). Wage growth is reported as modest and easing ( 26 , Nov p.1).

4. Dominant Risk Narrative

The dominant risk narrative indicates an **explicit asymmetric preparation** against **downside growth/labour risk**, while still acknowledging persistent **upside inflation risk**. FOMC statements consistently highlight "elevated uncertainty" and being "attentive to the risks to both sides of its dual mandate," but with a notable addition from September 2025 onwards emphasizing that "downside risks to employment have risen" ( 7 , p.1)( 5 , p.1)( 2 , p.1)( 17 , p.1). Chair Powell explicitly stated that "risks to inflation are tilted to the upside and risks to employment to the downside" ( 3 , p.3), describing this as a "challenging situation" with "no risk-free path" ( 3 , p.11). However, Vice Chair Bowman identified labor market fragility as posing the "greater risk" ( 13 , p.2), and Vice Chair Jefferson indicated the "balance of risks" has "shifted" toward employment concerns ( 14 , p.5). Governor Waller argued forcefully for policy easing to "strengthen the labor market and guard against a deterioration" ( 18 , p.2). This suggests the Fed is particularly preparing against a sharper-than-expected deterioration in the labor market, even as inflation remains above target due to factors perceived as transitory.

Beige Book Summary

Publication Date — November 2025

Descriptive synthesis of current US economic conditions based on district-level reporting.

National Backdrop

Overall economic activity was largely unchanged since the prior report, though two Districts observed a modest decline, while one reported modest growth (1). Consumer spending saw a further decline, but higher-end retail segments showed resilience (1). Manufacturing activity generally increased somewhat, although tariffs continued to pose a headwind (1). Revenues in the nonfinancial services sector were mostly flat to down, and loan demand was mixed across districts (1). Residential construction reports varied, with some Districts noting declines and others observing no change, while home sales activity also showed varied trends (1). The agriculture and energy sectors remained largely stable, though some contacts faced challenges from low prices for certain commodities and oil (1). Community organizations reported increased demand for food assistance, partly due to government shutdown disruptions to SNAP benefits (1).

Labour Market Conditions

Employment levels declined slightly across the nation, with roughly half of the Districts reporting weaker labor demand (1). While there was an uptick in layoff announcements, most Districts noted that employers opted to limit headcounts through hiring freezes, replacement-only hiring, and attrition rather than widespread layoffs (1). Several firms, including in Cleveland, adjusted hours worked to manage business volume, and some noted that artificial intelligence was replacing entry-level positions or boosting worker productivity, thereby reducing the need for new hires (1). Worker availability generally improved across most Districts, making it easier for employers to find staff, though specific skilled positions and a reduced number of immigrant workers continued to present hiring difficulties (1). Wage growth remained modest overall, with some sectors like manufacturing, construction, and health care experiencing more moderate wage pressures due to tighter labor supply and rising health insurance premiums (1). Minneapolis reported that compensation growth was appreciably slower than last year, especially for starting wages, but noted an uptick in benefit costs (1).

Prices & Cost Pressures

Prices rose moderately during the reporting period, with widespread input cost pressures impacting manufacturing and retail sectors, primarily due to tariffs (1). Districts such as Philadelphia and San Francisco also cited rising costs for insurance, utilities, technology, and health care (1). The ability to pass through these higher input costs to customers varied significantly, influenced by demand, competitive pressures, and consumer price sensitivity, leading to multiple reports of margin compression (1). Some firms, particularly manufacturers in the Dallas District, reported financial strain from tariffs and customer pushback on price increases (1). Conversely, prices for certain materials declined, attributed to sluggish demand, deferred tariff implementation, or reduced tariff rates (1). Looking forward, contacts generally anticipated persistent upward cost pressures, though plans to raise selling prices in the near term were mixed (1).

Sector Themes

  • Consumer activity: Overall consumer spending declined further, with resilience noted in higher-end retail, while lower- and middle-income consumers in Districts like Cleveland and Atlanta showed increasing caution and financial strain (1). Sales of electric vehicles (EVs) saw declines following the expiration of federal tax credits, particularly in New York and Atlanta (1). The government shutdown negatively impacted consumer purchases in some areas, with retailers in Dallas reporting decreased sales and a slowdown in foot traffic in Kansas City (1).
  • Manufacturing: Manufacturing activity generally increased somewhat, notably in Chicago with a slight rise in fabricated metals orders and machinery sales (1). Boston manufacturers reported modest growth and increased demand from AI infrastructure investments (1). Tariffs and related uncertainty continued to be a significant headwind, leading to modest declines in Philadelphia and some softening in orders in Richmond and Atlanta (1).
  • Services: The nonfinancial services sector experienced flat to declining revenues overall (1). New York reported moderate declines, particularly in business services and leisure/hospitality (1). Cleveland saw moderate growth in professional and business services, driven by tax law changes and AI adoption (1). Transportation activity was largely flat, with trucking volumes declining in Atlanta and Chicago (1).
  • Housing & construction: Residential construction activity varied, with declines in some Districts like Dallas, while others remained unchanged (1). Home sales activity also varied, with Boston reporting renewed strength due to lower mortgage rates, but Atlanta saw slight declines (1). Commercial real estate improved somewhat, notably in New York City's office market, and data center construction boosted demand in Cleveland (1). However, high construction costs and uncertain funding continued to deter development in Boston and Philadelphia (1).
  • Banking & credit: Loan demand was mixed nationally, with overall declines but slight increases in residential mortgages in New York (1). Credit standards were mostly unchanged but some Districts, like Philadelphia and St. Louis, reported tightening standards for business loans and commercial mortgages (1). Delinquencies generally improved but remained elevated in New York, while Philadelphia, Richmond, and St. Louis noted ongoing increases, particularly among households and small businesses (1).
  • Agriculture & energy: Conditions in these sectors were largely stable but presented challenges (1). In the Chicago District, farm income prospects rose slightly due to increased crop prices, despite dry conditions in some areas (1). Low oil prices and a global supply surplus led to a slight decline in oil and gas activity in Kansas City and concerns in Dallas about future production (1). Strong demand for primary energy sources, particularly LNG, solar, and wind, was noted in Atlanta, largely driven by data center activity (1).

Regional Dispersion

  • Stronger districts: The Chicago District experienced a slight increase in economic activity across most sectors, including employment, consumer spending, and manufacturing (1). Cleveland also saw a slight increase in business activity, with moderate growth in professional and business services (1). Boston reported slight economic expansion, driven by renewed strength in home sales, despite flat consumer spending (1).
  • Softer districts: New York experienced a modest decline in economic activity, with employment falling and services shrinking moderately (1). Philadelphia's activity was trending down, with the government shutdown exacerbating declines across most sectors (1). Dallas reported a slight weakening of economic activity, particularly in nonfinancial services, retail, and banking (1). St. Louis and Minneapolis reported flat activity overall, with St. Louis noting a demand slowdown amplified by the government shutdown and Minneapolis seeing a slight decrease in employment (1). Kansas City observed a slight slowdown in economic growth due to softer labor conditions and cooling consumer activity (1).
  • Common constraints: Tariffs and trade policy uncertainty were frequently cited headwinds impacting input costs, manufacturing outlooks, and consumer pricing (1). The federal government shutdown caused disruptions to consumer spending, social services, and business operations in multiple Districts including Philadelphia, St. Louis, Kansas City, and Dallas (1). Rising health insurance premiums were a widespread concern, contributing to higher labor costs (1). A cautious discretionary spending environment and price sensitivity among lower- and middle-income consumers was reported across several regions (1).
  • Outliers: In Boston, strong gains in home sales activity, driven by lower mortgage rates, helped offset flat consumer spending (1). The Cleveland District noted moderate loan demand growth partly due to declining interest rates, while some commercial clients remained cautious about investing (1). Despite an overall decline in services, New York's manufacturing sector showed a moderate pickup, and higher-end retail remained resilient (1).

Signals to Watch

  • Growth risks: Contacts noted an increased risk of slower economic activity in coming months (1). The federal government shutdown impacted demand and operations in multiple Districts (1). Ongoing uncertainty regarding tariffs and the broader economic outlook continued to restrain business investment and consumer spending (1).
  • Labour inflections: The shift from layoffs to attrition, hiring freezes, and replacement-only hiring as primary headcount management strategies (1). The role of artificial intelligence in reducing demand for entry-level positions and improving worker productivity (1). Pockets of tightness persist for skilled workers and in sectors with limited immigrant labor, despite general easing in worker availability (1).
  • Inflation persistence: Persistent upward pressure on input costs, particularly from tariffs, insurance, utilities, and health care premiums (1). Mixed intentions among firms regarding passing through these costs to consumers, with some anticipating increased price growth in the next six months while others reported margin compression (1).
  • Capital investment: Manufacturers in Philadelphia and Kansas City expressed optimism about growth and plans to increase capital expenditures, with some firms beginning to revisit paused expansion plans for 2026 (1). Conversely, some agriculture contacts in Chicago were delaying capital expenditures due to tight margins (1).
  • Financial stress: Increased demand for food and housing assistance from community organizations, with some struggling to meet demand due to funding constraints and the government shutdown (1). Rising reliance on debt by lower- and middle-income households for basic expenses (1). Continual increases in consumer loan delinquencies and defaults, particularly among households and small businesses, were noted in Districts like Philadelphia, St. Louis, and Richmond (1).
