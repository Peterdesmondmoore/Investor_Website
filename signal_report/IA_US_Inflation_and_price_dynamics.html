US Inflation and Price Dynamics
Systematically synthesises realised inflation, cost pressures, and market expectations to assess the prevailing US inflation regime.

Regime Assessment:

  • State the regime clearly using one label: Transitional / Fragile Balance
  • State the Regime Confidence Index: Medium Confidence
  • State whether the regime is stable or fragile: Fragile

Why This Regime:

  • Identify the dominant drivers (cite key signals): The Fed Inflation Signal (1), a high-weight and high-conviction cyclical indicator, points to a "COOL (disinflation/deflation risk)" regime, reflecting broad disinflationary forces. Conversely, the Policy-Relevant Inflation (PCE) Signal (2) and the structural Market Implied Inflation Signal (3), both also high-weight, indicate "Neutral" or "Stable" conditions. This conflicting evidence among dominant signals results in a fragile assessment.
  • Explain how dynamic weighting shaped the conclusion: The Fed Inflation Signal (1) receives a high weighting due to its high aggregation hint and conviction band, strongly suggesting disinflationary pressures. However, the influence of the structural Market Implied Inflation Signal (3) pointing to stable conditions, coupled with the "Neutral" PCE Signal (2), prevents a definitive "Defensive / Risk-Negative" classification. The composite view leads to a "Transitional / Fragile Balance."
  • List which signals were downweighted and why (cite where applicable): No signals were explicitly downweighted. All received high interpretation confidence and reported no internal conflict.

Alignment & Tensions:

  • Where signals reinforce each other (cite): The Policy-Relevant Inflation (PCE) Signal (2), Wage Growth & Inflation Signal (4), Inflation Term-Structure Signal (5), and CPI vs PPI Divergence Signal (6) largely reinforce a neutral or stable inflation outlook.
  • Where tensions exist (cite): The Fed Inflation Signal (1), with high weight and conviction, indicates a "COOL (disinflation/deflation risk)" regime. This stands in tension with the prevailing neutrality from other high and medium-weight signals.
  • Why tensions do not overturn the regime call: The persistent downward trend in the Fed Inflation Signal (1) indicates material disinflationary pressure. Yet, the stability observed in the structural Market Implied Inflation Signal (3) and the Fed's preferred PCE gauge (2) suggests outright deflation is not the singular dominant narrative, thus reflecting a fragile balance.

Scenario Balance:

  • Dominant scenario and its condition: Inflation remains in a fragile balance. Disinflationary risks from comprehensive measures are countered by stable core and market-implied expectations.
  • Primary upside risk and trigger: A sustained rebound in core inflation or market-implied breakevens could push the system towards a firmer Neutral or Reflationary regime.
  • Primary downside risk and trigger: Further deceleration in broad inflation measures, specifically if the Fed Inflation Signal composite z-score remains below -0.5, could deepen the "COOL" regime towards explicit deflationary risk (1).

What Would Change the Regime:

  • 2–3 concrete invalidation conditions drawn from signals (cite):
    • A sustained rise in the Fed Inflation Signal composite z-score above -0.5, shifting its regime back to NEUTRAL (1).
    • The Policy-Relevant Inflation (PCE) composite z-score falling below -0.75, which would indicate a "Cool" regime (2).
    • A significant decline in both 5-year and 10-year market-implied inflation expectations, driving the composite MIIE z-score below -0.5, indicative of a FALLING regime (3).
