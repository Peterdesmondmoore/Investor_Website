Fed Projections Synthesiser Summary

Inference of the Federal Reserve’s internally prepared future economic state, based exclusively on Fed-authored projections and communications.

1. Fed-Implied Growth Outlook

The Federal Reserve appears to be preparing for a near-term state of STABLE to mildly DECELERATING economic activity in late 2025, transitioning to ACCELERATING growth in 2026 before stabilizing in the medium-term. Real GDP growth moderated to around 1.5-1.6 percent in the first half of 2025 (1)(3)(Transcript of Chair Powell's Press Conference — September 17, 2025)(Transcript of Chair Powell's Press Conference — October 29, 2025), a slowdown from 2.4 percent in 2024 (Transcript of Chair Powell's Press Conference — October 29, 2025)(Economic Outlook and Monetary Policy Implementation (Remarks by Philip N. Jefferson) — January 16, 2026). The October 2025 Beige Book noted activity "changed little on balance," with consumer spending inching down (7), a trend that continued into November with further declines (8). The impact of the late 2025 government shutdown was also expected to be a drag on economic activity (Transcript of Chair Powell's Press Conference — October 29, 2025)(Economic Outlook and Monetary Policy Implementation (Remarks by Philip N. Jefferson) — January 16, 2026).

However, the December 2025 Summary of Economic Projections (SEP) indicates an expectation for stronger growth in 2026, with median real GDP rising to 2.3 percent, up from 1.7 percent in 2025 (2). This upward revision is partly attributed to resilient consumer spending, supportive fiscal policy, and significant business investment, particularly in AI-related technologies and data centers (Transcript of Chair Powell's Press Conference — December 10, 2025)(Outlook for the Economy and Monetary Policy (Remarks by Michelle W. Bowman) — January 16, 2026)(Economic Outlook and Monetary Policy Implementation (Remarks by Philip N. Jefferson) — January 16, 2026). The longer-run median GDP projection remains stable at 1.8 percent (2).

The confidence in this outlook is MEDIUM, as evidenced by ongoing mentions of "elevated uncertainty" surrounding economic policies and their effects (4)(FOMC Statement — October 29, 2025)(FOMC Statement — December 10, 2025)(FOMC Statement — January 28, 2026), and Chair Powell's acknowledgement that "forecasting is very difficult, even in placid times," especially during a "challenging time, even more uncertain than usual" (Transcript of Chair Powell's Press Conference — September 17, 2025)(Transcript of Chair Powell's Press Conference — October 29, 2025).

2. Fed-Implied Inflation Trajectory

The Fed appears to be preparing for an inflation path characterized by STICKY elevated readings in the near term, followed by a gradual DISINFLATION toward the 2 percent target in the medium term. Inflation, as measured by total and core PCE, remained "somewhat elevated" above the 2 percent longer-run goal through late 2025, with readings around 2.8-2.9 percent (4)(FOMC Statement — October 29, 2025)(FOMC Statement — December 10, 2025)(Transcript of Chair Powell's Press Conference — September 17, 2025)(Transcript of Chair Powell's Press Conference — October 29, 2025)(Transcript of Chair Powell's Press Conference — December 10, 2025). The December SEP medians project PCE inflation at 2.9 percent in 2025 and 2.4 percent in 2026, converging to 2.0 percent by 2028 (2).

The elevated near-term inflation is primarily attributed to "tariff effects" pushing up goods prices (3)(Transcript of Chair Powell's Press Conference — September 17, 2025)(Transcript of Chair Powell's Press Conference — October 29, 2025). The prevailing framing is that these tariff effects will likely be a "one-time shift in the price level" rather than a persistent inflationary process, with their impact expected to peak in early 2026 and then wane (Transcript of Chair Powell's Press Conference — September 17, 2025)(Transcript of Chair Powell's Press Conference — October 29, 2025)(Transcript of Chair Powell's Press Conference — December 10, 2025). Near-term inflation expectations increased due to tariffs, but longer-term expectations remain "well anchored" at the 2 percent objective (Transcript of Chair Powell's Press Conference — September 17, 2025)(Transcript of Chair Powell's Press Conference — December 10, 2025). Governor Miran, however, questioned whether tariffs are the sole or primary driver of core goods inflation, suggesting other factors could contribute to its current level (The Inflation Outlook (Remarks by Stephen I. Miran) — December 15, 2025). He expects significant disinflation from shelter to offset other pressures (The Inflation Outlook (Remarks by Stephen I. Miran) — December 15, 2025).

The Fed remains SENSITIVE TO UPSIDE RISKS to the inflation forecast, particularly if tariff effects prove more persistent or if longer-term inflation expectations become unanchored (3)(Minutes of the Federal Open Market Committee — September 16-17, 2025)(Minutes of the Federal Open Market Committee — October 28-29, 2025).

3. Fed-Implied Labour Market Trajectory

The Federal Reserve anticipates a continued, gradual LOOSENING of the labor market. Job gains have "slowed significantly" through 2025, and the unemployment rate has "edged up" to 4.3 percent in August and 4.4 percent in September (4)(FOMC Statement — October 29, 2025)(FOMC Statement — December 10, 2025). The median SEP projections for the unemployment rate show it peaking at 4.5 percent in 2025 before gradually declining to 4.2 percent by 2027-2028 (2). This softening is attributed to both a decline in labor force growth (due to lower immigration and participation) and a reduction in labor demand (Transcript of Chair Powell's Press Conference — September 17, 2025)(Transcript of Chair Powell's Press Conference — October 29, 2025).

The speed of adjustment is viewed as GRADUAL, characterized by low hiring and low layoffs, creating a "less dynamic and somewhat softer labor market" (Transcript of Chair Powell's Press Conference — September 17, 2025)(Economic Outlook and Monetary Policy Implementation (Remarks by Philip N. Jefferson) — January 16, 2026). However, there are signs of increasing fragility, with firms reporting hiring freezes, reliance on attrition, and the use of AI to reduce headcounts (7)(8). Wage growth remains "modest to moderate" (The Beige Book — October 2025)(The Beige Book — November 2025), and is no longer seen as a significant source of inflationary pressure (Outlook for the Economy and Monetary Policy (Remarks by Michelle W. Bowman) — January 16, 2026).

The degree of concern expressed by the Fed is HIGH. FOMC statements consistently emphasize that "downside risks to employment have risen in recent months" (4)(FOMC Statement — October 29, 2025)(FOMC Statement — December 10, 2025). Chair Powell explicitly stated that the labor market has become "increasingly more fragile" and could "deteriorate further if demand does not strengthen" (Outlook for the Economy and Monetary Policy (Remarks by Michelle W. Bowman) — January 16, 2026). Governor Waller adopted a more pessimistic view, describing 2025 job growth as "virtually no growth... Zero. Zip. Nada." and citing a "substantial deterioration" risk (Statement by Governor Christopher J. Waller — January 30, 2026).

4. Dominant Risk Narrative

The dominant risk narrative indicates the Federal Reserve appears to be preparing for and actively mitigating against downside growth and labor market risks, even as inflation remains somewhat elevated. This reflects a shift in the Committee's balance of risks.

The Fed's actions demonstrate an asymmetric preparation against employment risks. While acknowledging "risks to both sides of its dual mandate" and persistent "upside risks to inflation" (3)(Minutes of the Federal Open Market Committee — September 16-17, 2025)(Minutes of the Federal Open Market Committee — October 28-29, 2025), the Committee's policy adjustments in late 2025 explicitly prioritize addressing the "risen" downside risks to employment (4)(FOMC Statement — October 29, 2025)(FOMC Statement — December 10, 2025). Chair Powell characterized the cumulative 75 basis points of federal funds rate cuts in September, October, and December 2025 as a "risk-management cut" to "preemptively stabilize and support labor market conditions" (Transcript of Chair Powell's Press Conference — September 17, 2025)(Outlook for the Economy and Monetary Policy (Remarks by Michelle W. Bowman) — January 16, 2026).

Furthermore, the decision to conclude balance sheet runoff and initiate purchases of shorter-term Treasury securities in December 2025 was aimed at maintaining an ample supply of reserves and supporting effective interest rate control (6)(Economic Outlook and Monetary Policy Implementation (Remarks by Philip N. Jefferson) — January 16, 2026), implicitly providing further liquidity support. This proactive stance on employment, alongside the belief that tariff-driven inflation is a "one-time shift in the price level" (Transcript of Chair Powell's Press Conference — September 17, 2025)(Transcript of Chair Powell's Press Conference — October 29, 2025), highlights a preparation for a scenario where labor market weakness is the more immediate and severe threat to the dual mandate. The January 2026 FOMC statement, holding rates steady, still noted "job gains remained low, and the unemployment rate has shown some signs of stabilization," reinforcing the ongoing attention to labor market health (FOMC Statement — January 28, 2026).

5. Evidence Map (Concise)

  • Key SEP signals (1)(2):
    • Median real GDP growth for 2026 was revised up from 1.8% (September) to 2.3% (December), signaling a more robust medium-term growth outlook.
    • Median PCE and Core PCE inflation projections for 2025-2026 were slightly lowered in December, but still remain above the 2% target, converging to 2% only by 2028.
    • Median unemployment rate projections remained relatively stable at 4.5% (2025) and 4.4% (2026), but risks to employment were flagged as elevated.
    • Median Federal Funds Rate projections remained consistent across September and December SEPs, indicating a sustained easing path from 3.6% (2025) to 3.0% (Longer Run).
  • Key Beige Book themes (The Beige Book — October 2025)(The Beige Book — November 2025):
    • Overall economic activity "changed little" in October, then "declined further" in November, with softening consumer spending, particularly for lower and middle-income households.
    • Labor markets were "largely stable" in October but "declined slightly" in November, characterized by muted demand, increased layoffs/attrition, AI-driven reductions in entry-level positions, and modest wage growth.
    • Prices "rose further" or "moderately" in both periods, driven by tariff-induced input costs and rising service-sector expenses, leading to firm margin compression.
  • Key language or framing from statements or speeches:
    • FOMC Statements (3)(4)(5)(6)(FOMC Statement — January 28, 2026): Consistent acknowledgment of "elevated uncertainty" and that "downside risks to employment have risen in recent months," prompting federal funds rate cuts and the conclusion of balance sheet runoff.
    • Chair Powell Press Conferences (Transcript of Chair Powell's Press Conference — September 17, 2025)(Transcript of Chair Powell's Press Conference — October 29, 2025)(Transcript of Chair Powell's Press Conference — December 10, 2025): Described the labor market as "increasingly more fragile" and rate cuts as "risk-management" actions to prevent further deterioration. Maintained that tariff-induced inflation is a "one-time shift in the price level" and not a persistent problem.
    • Vice Chair Bowman's Speech (Outlook for the Economy and Monetary Policy (Remarks by Michelle W. Bowman) — January 16, 2026): Highlighted the "shift in my balance of risks" toward employment concerns and emphasized the "fragility" of the labor market, while noting inflation (ex-tariffs) is closer to target.
    • Vice Chair Jefferson's Speech (Economic Outlook and Monetary Policy Implementation (Remarks by Philip N. Jefferson) — January 16, 2026): Expressed "cautious optimism" for growth and inflation, but underscored "downside risks to employment" from low hiring and moderating labor demand.
    • Governor Waller's Statement (Statement by Governor Christopher J. Waller — January 30, 2026): Dissented from holding rates steady in January 2026, citing "weak labor market" with "virtually no growth in payroll employment in 2025" and a significant risk of "deterioration." Argued that policy should move closer to a neutral stance as inflation (excluding tariff effects) is near 2%.
