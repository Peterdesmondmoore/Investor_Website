Fed Projections Synthesiser Summary

Inference of the Federal Reserve’s internally prepared future economic state, based exclusively on Fed-authored projections and communications.

1. Fed-Implied Growth Outlook

The Federal Reserve appears to be preparing for a future state of STABLE to ACCELERATING growth in the near-to-medium term. The December 2025 Summary of Economic Projections (SEP) indicates a median real GDP growth of 1.7 percent for 2025, accelerating to 2.3 percent in 2026, and then moderating slightly to 2.0 percent in 2027 and 1.9 percent in 2028 (1). This represents an upward revision to the growth outlook for 2025 and 2026 compared to the September 2025 SEP (1)(5).

This improved outlook is attributed to stronger expected potential output growth and increased support from financial conditions (3). Key drivers highlighted include resilient consumer spending, expanding business fixed investment (especially in technology and AI-related data centers), and supportive fiscal policy (3). The drag from higher tariffs is also expected to wane after 2025 (3).

Despite these positive projections, confidence in the growth outlook is MEDIUM. The Beige Book for October and November 2025 noted economic activity as "little changed" or experiencing "modest declines" across many districts (2). Contacts frequently cited elevated uncertainty due to government shutdowns, trade policy, and consumer price sensitivity, particularly affecting lower- and middle-income households (2). Housing market activity remained weak across reports (2).

2. Fed-Implied Inflation Trajectory

The Federal Reserve's projections imply a path of STICKY inflation in the near-term, transitioning to a gradual DISINFLATION towards the 2 percent target in the medium term. The December 2025 SEP shows median total PCE inflation at 2.9 percent for 2025, falling to 2.4 percent in 2026, and reaching 2.0 percent by 2028 (1). Core PCE inflation follows a similar trajectory (1).

The recent pickup in goods inflation is primarily attributed to tariffs, which the Fed largely frames as a "one-time shift in the price level" rather than an ongoing inflationary process (3). This tariff-induced inflation is expected to peak in the first quarter of 2026 and then decline in the second half of the year (3). Meanwhile, disinflation is noted as continuing for services, particularly non-housing services (3). Longer-term inflation expectations are noted to remain "well anchored" at the 2 percent goal (7).

However, inflation remains sensitive to UPSIDE RISKS. FOMC participants have consistently viewed the risks around the inflation forecast as "skewed to the upside" (8)(4)(3). This is due to uncertainties about the timing, magnitude, and persistence of tariff effects, and the possibility that elevated inflation could prove more persistent than anticipated, potentially unanchoring longer-term inflation expectations (8)(4)(3).

3. Fed-Implied Labour Market Trajectory

The Federal Reserve is preparing for a LOOSENING labor market. The unemployment rate, while still "low," has edged up to 4.4 percent in September 2025 (11). Job gains have "slowed significantly" this year (6), and private payroll gains stepped down noticeably as early as July 2025 (8). The September 2025 SEP projected the median unemployment rate to reach 4.5 percent by the end of 2025, before gradually declining to 4.2 percent by 2028 (1)(5).

The speed of adjustment is characterized as GRADUAL. Beige Book reports indicate stable to slightly declining employment levels, with employers limiting headcounts through hiring freezes, attrition, and replacement-only hiring rather than widespread layoffs (2). Labor supply has notably declined due to lower immigration and labor force participation (4). While labor demand has also softened, initial claims data do not yet indicate an acceleration of weakness (10).

A HIGH degree of concern is expressed regarding downside risks to employment. FOMC statements and minutes consistently highlighted that "downside risks to employment have risen" in recent months (6)(10)(11). Factors contributing to this concern include a less dynamic labor market, concentrated job gains in a few sectors, and the potential impact of artificial intelligence on labor demand (4)(2). Wage growth, while modest overall, shows pockets of pressure due to skilled labor shortages and rising health insurance premiums (2).

4. Dominant Risk Narrative

The Federal Reserve's dominant risk narrative emphasizes ASYMMETRIC PREPARATION against both **upside inflation risk** and **downside growth/labour risk**, with a pronounced shift towards balancing these concerns.

The Committee explicitly states its attentiveness "to the risks to both sides of its dual mandate" (9)(6)(10)(11). While "risks around the inflation forecast [are] skewed to the upside" due to potential tariff persistence and unanchored expectations (8)(4)(3), the "downside risks to employment have risen" (6). This shift in the "balance of risks" led to policy rate reductions in September, October, and December 2025 (6)(10)(11).

Evidence of asymmetric preparation is seen in these policy rate adjustments, which represent an effort to move towards a "more neutral policy stance" (6). The decision in October and December to conclude the reduction of securities holdings and initiate purchases of shorter-term Treasury securities further indicates a focus on maintaining ample reserves and supporting effective policy control in this environment (15)(16). The acknowledgment of economic uncertainty, including from government policy changes and data limitations due to the shutdown, further underscores the cautious and data-dependent approach to navigating these dual risks (3).

5. Evidence Map (Concise)

  • Key SEP signals:
    • December 2025 SEP median GDP growth for 2025-2026 revised up (1.7% to 2.3%), signaling stronger activity (1).
    • December 2025 SEP median PCE inflation for 2025-2026 lowered from September projections (2.9% to 2.4%), with a return to 2.0% by 2028 (1).
    • December 2025 SEP median unemployment rate stabilizes at 4.5% for 2025, then edges down to 4.2% by 2027-2028 (1).
  • Key Beige Book themes:
    • Overall economic activity was "little changed" or showed "modest declines" across districts in October and November 2025, but with significant regional/sectoral variations and high uncertainty (2).
    • Persistent moderate price increases driven by tariffs and rising input costs, leading to margin compression for some firms, while others pass costs to consumers (2).
    • Labor market softening with muted demand, increased layoffs/attrition, and the impact of AI on entry-level positions, balanced by improved labor availability in some areas (2).
  • Key language or framing from statements or speeches:
    • FOMC statements consistently highlight "uncertainty about the economic outlook remains elevated" and that the Committee is "attentive to the risks to both sides of its dual mandate" (9)(6)(10)(11).
    • Explicit recognition that "downside risks to employment have risen" and inflation "moved up and remains somewhat elevated" has led to a "shift in the balance of risks" (6)(10)(11).
    • Decisions to lower the federal funds rate by 1/4 percentage point in September, October, and December 2025 reflect a move towards a more neutral policy stance (6)(10)(11).
    • The decision to conclude balance sheet runoff by December 1, 2025, and initiate purchases of shorter-term Treasury securities from December 11, 2025, is aimed at maintaining ample reserves and supporting policy implementation (15)(11).
