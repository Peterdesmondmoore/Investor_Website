Fed Projections Synthesiser Summary

Inference of the Federal Reserve’s internally prepared future economic state, based exclusively on Fed-authored projections and communications.

1. Fed-Implied Growth Outlook

The Federal Reserve appears to be preparing for a **STABLE** to slightly **DECELERATING** growth environment in the near-term, transitioning to an **ACCELERATING** then **STABLE** path in the medium-term. Economic activity has generally been described as "moderated" or "expanding at a moderate pace" in the first half of 2025, with recent Beige Book reports indicating that overall activity has "changed little" or was "little changed" across most Districts, featuring some areas of softening alongside modest growth (2)(3)(4)(5)(12)(13).

For the medium-term, the December 2025 Summary of Economic Projections (SEP) indicates a notably stronger outlook for real GDP growth, with the median projection for 2026 rising to 2.3% (from 1.8% in September) and 2.0% in 2027 (1). This revised acceleration is attributed by Chair Powell to resilient consumer spending, supportive fiscal policy, and continued robust investment in AI and data centers, which is expected to boost productivity (8). However, confidence in the growth outlook is assessed as **MEDIUM** due to persistent "elevated uncertainty" surrounding government policy changes (tariffs, immigration, fiscal spending) and the temporary "data drought" caused by the federal government shutdown, which may require careful assessment of incoming data (7)(11).

2. Fed-Implied Inflation Trajectory

The Fed appears to be preparing for a path of near-term **STICKY** inflation that gradually transitions to **DISINFLATION** in the medium-term, ultimately returning to the 2 percent target. Inflation is currently "somewhat elevated" and has "moved up since earlier in the year" (3)(4)(5). This elevation is largely attributed to the "effects of tariffs" on goods prices, which are framed as a "one-time price increase" rather than an ongoing inflationary process (6)(7)(8). These tariff-related price increases are expected to continue to pass through and "peak in the first quarter or so of next year" before coming down in the second half of 2026 (8). Concurrently, disinflation in services, particularly non-housing services, is expected to continue (7).

The median SEP projections for total PCE inflation show a decrease from 2.9% in 2025 to 2.4% in 2026, reaching 2.1% in 2027 and 2.0% in 2028 (1). This reflects the expectation that tariff effects will be transitory. While "inflation away from tariffs is actually not so far from our 2 percent goal" (7), the risks to the inflation forecast are consistently "skewed to the upside" (9)(10)(11), indicating sensitivity to the possibility of tariff effects being more persistent or long-term inflation expectations becoming unanchored. However, longer-term inflation expectations are seen as "well anchored" (6)(14).

3. Fed-Implied Labour Market Trajectory

The Federal Reserve is preparing for a **LOOSENING** labor market, albeit from a position that is still considered relatively low in unemployment. Job gains have "slowed significantly" (3)(4)(5), and the unemployment rate has "edged up but remains low," reaching a median projection of 4.5% for 2025 in the latest SEP (1). Labor demand has "softened" (6)(7), with reports of employers reducing headcounts through attrition and layoffs, and increasing reliance on temporary workers (12)(13). The adoption of artificial intelligence (AI) is identified as a factor contributing to reduced labor demand, especially for entry-level roles, and enhanced worker productivity (6)(8).

The speed of adjustment is viewed as "gradual cooling" (7)(8), rather than a sharp deterioration, partly due to a "sharp decline" in labor supply from lower immigration and participation (7). However, pockets of tightness persist in specific skilled positions and sectors affected by immigration policy changes (12)(13). The degree of concern expressed by the Fed is **MEDIUM to HIGH**, as "downside risks to employment have risen in recent months" (3)(4)(5). The Committee has characterized recent rate cuts as a "risk-management cut" to support the labor market (6), indicating a proactive stance against a more significant downturn.

4. Dominant Risk Narrative

The dominant risk narrative has significantly shifted from a primary focus on upside inflation risks to a more **BALANCED** view, with an increasing emphasis on **DOWNSIDE GROWTH/LABOUR RISK** (6)(7)(8). This evolution is explicitly articulated in FOMC statements, which state the Committee is "attentive to the risks to both sides of its dual mandate" and judges that "downside risks to employment have risen in recent months" (3)(4)(5).

Chair Powell clarified this shift, noting that while inflation risks are still "tilted to the upside," the overall "balance of risks has shifted" more towards employment concerns, warranting a move towards a "more neutral policy stance" (6)(8). The sustained period of rate cuts in Q3 and Q4 2025, alongside the decision to conclude balance sheet runoff in December and initiate purchases of shorter-term Treasury securities to maintain ample reserves (4)(5), provides evidence of asymmetric preparation. These actions suggest a proactive effort to buffer the labor market and maintain economic stability even as inflation remains "somewhat elevated," under the assumption that tariff-driven inflation will be transitory (8). The revised Statement on Longer-Run Goals, effective August 2025, also supports this framing by noting that "employment may at times run above real-time assessments of maximum employment without necessarily creating risks to price stability" (14), indicating increased tolerance for labor market strength relative to inflation control in certain circumstances.

5. Evidence Map (Concise)

  • Key SEP signals
    • December 2025 SEP shows median 2026 GDP growth revised up to 2.3% (from 1.8% in September) and 2025 GDP up to 1.7% (from 1.6% in September) (1).
    • December 2025 SEP lowered median PCE inflation for 2025 (to 2.9%) and 2026 (to 2.4%) compared to September (1).
    • Median federal funds rate path for 2025 was lowered to 3.6% in the December SEP compared to 3.9% in September (1).
    • Unemployment rate median for 2025 remains at 4.5% (1).
  • Key Beige Book themes
    • Economic activity is "changed little" or "little changed," with softening consumer spending, particularly among lower-income households, and manufacturing facing headwinds from tariffs and waning demand (12)(13).
    • Labor demand is "generally muted," leading to increased layoffs and attrition in some districts, with AI noted as a factor reducing entry-level hiring, despite overall stable employment levels (12)(13).
    • Prices "rose moderately," driven by higher input costs from tariffs and services (e.g., health insurance), with varied pass-through to consumers (12)(13).
    • "Elevated uncertainty" regarding the economic outlook is a pervasive theme (12)(13).
  • Key language or framing from statements or speeches
    • Consistent statements that "downside risks to employment have risen in recent months" (3)(4)(5), indicating a shift in the balance of risks.
    • Inflation is characterized as "somewhat elevated," primarily due to "one-time price increase" effects from tariffs that are expected to subside by H2 2026 (6)(8).
    • Chair Powell stated that policy is now "within a broad range of estimates of its neutral value," allowing the Committee to "wait and see how the economy evolves" (8).
    • The Committee decided to conclude the reduction of securities holdings on December 1, 2025, and initiate purchases of shorter-term Treasury securities to maintain an ample supply of reserves (4)(5).
    • The August 2025 update to the Statement on Longer-Run Goals indicates that employment may run above maximum without necessarily creating inflation risks (14).
