Supply & Demand Structural Assessment — Gold

Supply-Side Structure

  • Recycling/scrap supply is structurally muted due to a growing trend of using gold as collateral for loans, particularly in India, rather than traditional recycling channels (Doc B, page 8) (2).
  • Refinery capacity for gold bar production faces structural constraints, with refiners hesitant to expand due to low returns and high costs, limiting the fulfillment of strong demand for smaller physical formats (Doc C, page 3) (3).

Demand-Side Structure

  • Investment demand via gold ETFs has become a robust structural driver, marked by six consecutive months of global inflows, led notably by Asian investors, pushing total AUM and holdings to historical peaks (Doc A, page 1) (1).
  • Central bank demand remains a persistent and strategically driven, non-economic factor, with emerging markets continuing accumulation and possessing significant potential for further purchases, especially amid geopolitical tensions (Doc B, page 4, 8) (2).
  • Physical consumer demand for jewellery and bar & coin shows mixed regional trends, with US consumption declining in tonnage but retail interest in fractional gold bars remaining very strong, indicating demand for physical security (Doc C, page 1, 3) (3).

Structural Asymmetries

  • A persistent supply rigidity is evident in the reluctance of refiners to expand gold bar production capacity and the structural shift of recycled gold towards collateralization, limiting secondary supply (Doc B, page 8)(2)(3).
  • Non-economic demand from central banks, driven by policy and geopolitical considerations, creates a rigid floor for overall demand, particularly from emerging markets that have substantial room for further gold reserves (Doc B, page 8) (2).
  • The market balance indicates a structural tightening due to inelastic physical supply meeting persistent and often price-insensitive demand from official sectors and growing investment flows (Doc B, page 8)(2)(3).

Stability vs Change

Area Assessment
Supply The significant shift in recycling behavior towards collateralization is a material structural change, contributing to muted scrap supply. Underlying refinery capacity constraints remain largely stable (Doc B, page 8)(2)(3).
Demand The emergence of Asia as a dominant driver of persistent ETF investment inflows is a material structural change. Central bank demand for strategic asset accumulation remains a structurally stable feature (Doc A, page 1)(1)(Doc B, page 4) (2).
Balance The tightening of supply-demand balances driven by sticky demand and constrained physical supply is a growing structural trend, while short-term physical consumer demand can exhibit cyclical volatility (Doc B, page 8)(2)(3).
