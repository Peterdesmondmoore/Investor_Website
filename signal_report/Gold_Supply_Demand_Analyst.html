Supply & Demand Structural Assessment — Gold

Supply-Side Structure

  • Recycling flows have been muted despite higher gold prices, influenced by an increased use of gold as collateral for loans, particularly in India where over 200 tonnes of jewellery were pledged (1, p8). This suggests a new structural factor limiting secondary supply.
  • Refineries are hesitant to expand capacity for gold bar production, citing low returns and high operating costs, indicating a persistent structural constraint on increasing the physical supply of certain gold products (2, p3).
  • The shift of retail investors from net sellers to net buyers has led to a drying up of secondary market liquidity, compelling dealers to source more products from refineries and placing additional strain on existing, constrained capacity (2, p3).

Demand-Side Structure

  • Central bank demand remains a significant and consistent contributor to gold's performance, with official sector purchases continuing strongly in 2025. Emerging market countries, whose gold reserves are still well below those of advanced economies, represent a key source for potential future accumulation (1, p7, p8). This demand is largely price-insensitive.
  • Global physically backed gold ETFs recorded six consecutive months of inflows, setting a trajectory for their strongest year ever, with total assets under management reaching US$530 billion and holdings increasing to 3,932 tonnes in November 2025 (3, p1). These flows, notably dominated by Asia with strong contributions from North America and Europe, reflect a structural investment appetite driven by geopolitical uncertainty and a desire for diversification (3, p1, p2)(1, p3, p4).
  • US consumer demand for jewellery, bars, and coins fell significantly by 33% year-over-year in Q3 2025, reaching its weakest level since the pre-COVID 2017-19 trough, reflecting a period where robust buying was met with profit-taking (2, p1). Conversely, fractional gold bar demand in the US is very strong, leading dealers to pay higher premiums, but this is limited by production capacity (2, p3).

Structural Asymmetries

  • Persistent central bank accumulation, particularly from emerging markets which have significant room to increase gold as a share of total reserves, provides a non-economic demand floor that structurally tightens the market balance (1, p8).
  • The inelasticity of refining capacity for physical gold products, coupled with strong, specific retail demand for items like fractional bars, creates a structural mismatch that can lead to higher premiums and unfulfilled demand, rather than an easy supply response (2, p3).
  • The sustained, multi-month inflows into global gold ETFs, driven by a broad desire for diversification amid heightened geoeconomic uncertainty, represent a structural tightening of investment demand, providing a persistent upward bias that is less sensitive to short-term market fluctuations (3, p1, p2)(1, p3).

Stability vs Change

Area Assessment
Supply Recycling behavior has materially changed, with gold increasingly being used as collateral rather than being sold in response to higher prices (1, p8). Refinery capacity remains a stable, enduring constraint (2, p3).
Demand Central bank accumulation is a structurally stable, long-term trend (1, p4, p8). ETF and institutional investment demand has materially changed, exhibiting a sustained inflow trend particularly from Asia, establishing a new structural bias (3, p1, p2)(1, p3). US consumer demand for jewellery and bar & coin shows a structural pattern of weakness (2, p1).
Balance The gold market balance has structurally tightened due to consistent investment demand and ongoing central bank accumulation (3, p1, p4, p7). The potential for increased secondary supply from collateralized gold liquidation represents a cyclical factor that could impact this balance if economic conditions deteriorate (1, p8).
