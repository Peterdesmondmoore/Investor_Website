Supply & Demand Structural Assessment — Gold

Supply-Side Structure

  • Gold recycling flows have been relatively muted in 2025 despite rising gold prices, a phenomenon linked to an increase in the use of gold as collateral for loans, particularly noted in India where over 200 tonnes of jewellery have been pledged in the formal sector (2).
  • Refineries are hesitant to invest in capacity expansion for gold bar production, citing low returns despite high prices and higher operational costs, which creates a constraint on physical supply responsiveness (3).
  • Secondary market liquidity has dried up due to a shift from retail investors being net sellers to net buyers, compelling dealers to source products directly from refineries (3).

Demand-Side Structure

  • Central bank demand remains a significant and consistent contributor to gold's performance, with strong official sector purchases expected to continue, especially from emerging market countries whose gold reserves are still low relative to advanced economies (2).
  • Global physically-backed gold ETFs recorded six consecutive months of inflows in November 2025, achieving new month-end peaks in total Assets Under Management (US$530bn) and holdings (3,932 tonnes). Asia led these inflows, indicating a regional shift in investment interest (1).
  • US-listed ETFs demonstrated significant structural flow, adding 137 tonnes in Q3 2025 (a 160% year-on-year surge) and accounting for 62% of global inflows, positioning North American funds for a record annual performance (3).
  • US consumer demand for jewellery and bar & coin experienced a sharp decline in Q3 2025, falling 33% year-on-year, though retail demand for fractional gold bars has improved since Q3, indicating price sensitivity offset by opportunistic buying (3).

Structural Asymmetries

  • Official sector gold accumulation acts as a persistent floor for demand, driven by policy considerations rather than market conditions, with emerging market central banks showing long-term potential for increased holdings (2).
  • A mismatch exists between constrained secondary supply (due to gold collateralization and refinery hesitancy to expand capacity) and strong, persistent demand from central banks and increasing retail investment, creating a structural tightening pressure on the gold market (2)(3).
  • The high structural support from central bank buying and strong, geographically diversified ETF inflows contrasts with more cyclical, price-sensitive physical demand from jewellery and bar & coin, leading to an asymmetric market where certain demand segments can buffer price corrections (1)(2)(3).

Stability vs Change

Area Assessment
Supply **Materially Changed:** Gold recycling flows are muted despite higher prices, primarily due to increased use of gold as collateral, altering secondary supply dynamics (2). Refinery reluctance to expand capacity also represents a durable constraint on physical supply (3).
**Structurally Unchanged:** No explicit structural changes in mine production or producer hedging were identified in the provided documents.
Demand **Materially Changed:** Investment demand, particularly via gold ETFs, has become a dominant and increasingly global structural driver, led by Asia, significantly influencing overall gold demand trends (1)(2). Geopolitical and geoeconomic uncertainty is a heightened and persistent driver of gold performance (2).
**Structurally Unchanged:** Central bank gold accumulation remains a stable, policy-driven component of demand (2). Gold's role as a portfolio diversifier and source of stability in volatile markets persists (2).
**Cyclical vs Structural:** ETF flows exhibit cyclical momentum-driven aspects alongside structural shifts in investor base (1)(2). Physical demand for jewellery and bar & coin shows cyclical price-sensitive behavior (e.g., profit-taking offsetting buying) within a broader trend (3).
Balance **Materially Changed:** The gold market balance is structurally tightening due to strong, non-economic central bank demand, sustained institutional investment inflows, and constrained secondary supply from muted recycling and refinery limitations (2)(3).
**Structurally Unchanged:** The underlying drivers for gold as a safe-haven asset remain consistent, contributing to a baseline of demand in an uncertain global environment (2).
