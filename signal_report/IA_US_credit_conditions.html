US Credit Conditions
Integrates credit spreads, systemic-stress indicators, and private-credit growth signals to assess the prevailing US credit-conditions regime.

Regime Assessment:

  • Regime: Transitional / Fragile Balance
  • Regime Confidence Index: Medium Confidence
  • The regime is fragile.

Why This Regime:

  • The `Private Credit Impulse` signal (1) is the dominant driver. It is classified with a High Aggregation Weight and High Conviction. This signal projects a transition to decelerating credit conditions, indicating an imminent contraction in private-sector credit growth.
  • Other key signals, including `Credit Spreads` (2), `Credit Conditions` (3), and the `Financial Stress Index` (4), currently reflect NORMAL or Neutral financial conditions. These signals carry Medium Aggregation Weights and High Interpretation Confidence.
  • Dynamic weighting emphasizes the forward-looking `Private Credit Impulse` (1). Its projected deceleration, despite current stability, shifts the overall assessment to "Transitional" rather than remaining purely "Expansionary".
  • The `Financial Stress Composite` (5) was downweighted. This signal exhibits Low Conviction, Mixed Signals, and has an Internal Conflict Flag set to Yes.

Alignment & Tensions:

  • Signals currently reinforcing each other include `Credit Spreads` (2), `Credit Conditions` (3), and `Financial Stress Index` (4). All indicate a stable, neutral financial environment, with low stress and moderate volatility (6).
  • The primary tension exists with the `Private Credit Impulse` signal (1). This signal projects decelerating credit conditions, contrasting with the prevailing neutral sentiment from other indicators.
  • This tension does not overturn the regime call, but defines it as "Transitional". The `Private Credit Impulse` (1) is a leading indicator. Its high weighting suggests a foundational shift is underway despite the current stability of other cyclical signals.

Scenario Balance:

  • The dominant scenario is for credit conditions to decelerate in the near term. This is driven by projected weakness in consumer and business loan growth (1).
  • The primary upside risk is a stronger-than-expected economic rebound. This could stimulate loan demand and bank lending, preventing a sustained deceleration and potentially re-entering a stable regime (1).
  • The primary downside risk is intensifying economic headwinds or increased financial stress. This could cause a sharper, more prolonged contraction in credit growth or wider corporate spreads (1)(2).

What Would Change the Regime:

  • A sustained rise in corporate bond spreads. This would push the `Credit Spreads Composite` z-score to +0.5 or higher (2).
  • A significant rebound in the six-month annualised growth rates for all underlying credit components. This would push their average robust z-score above -0.75, reversing the projected deceleration (1).
  • A sustained rise in the St. Louis Fed Financial Stress Index (STLFSI4). This would push its robust z-score above +0.5, indicating a shift to Bearish conditions (4).
