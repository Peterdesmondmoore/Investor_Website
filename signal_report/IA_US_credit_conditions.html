US Credit Conditions
Integrates credit spreads, systemic-stress indicators, and private-credit growth signals to assess the prevailing US credit-conditions regime.

Regime Assessment:

  • Regime: Transitional / Fragile Balance
  • Regime Confidence Index: Medium Confidence
  • Stability: Fragile

Why This Regime:

  • The regime reflects a nuanced state in credit conditions. The Financial Stress Composite indicates neutral financial conditions (1). However, the Financial Stress Index reports elevated stress (2). Both are high-weight, high-confidence signals.
  • Dynamic weighting prioritised these two high-conviction, high-confidence cyclical signals. Their direct contradiction defines the transitional and fragile nature.
  • Medium-weight signals for Credit Spreads (3) and Credit Conditions (4) were downweighted. This was due to their mixed signals, lower conviction, and internal conflicts. The Private Credit Impulse (5) was also downweighted for similar reasons and its low aggregation hint.

Alignment & Tensions:

  • The Credit Spreads signal shows a "NORMAL" regime, with underlying tightness approaching "EASY" conditions (3). The Credit Conditions signal indicates persistent "Easing" (4). These generally align with the "Neutral" assessment of the Financial Stress Composite (1). The Private Credit Impulse suggests stable credit growth (5).
  • A significant tension exists between the Financial Stress Composite's "Neutral" reading (1) and the Financial Stress Index's "Bearish" classification (2). The latter suggests growing stress relative to its historical trend, despite a raw level that might appear loose.
  • These tensions do not overturn the regime call. The "Transitional / Fragile Balance" classification explicitly accounts for this divergence. The composite view, integrating multiple indicators, offsets the rising trend in a single stress index.

Scenario Balance:

  • The dominant scenario is a fragile balance in credit conditions. Underlying stress trends persist despite a broader neutral composite. This implies no significant easing or tightening.
  • Primary upside risk is a broad re-acceleration across all credit components. This would be reflected by the Private Credit Impulse composite rising above 0.75 (5).
  • Primary downside risk is a significant and sustained increase in the robust z-score of the STLFSI4 index. This would push the Financial Stress Composite above its 0.75 threshold (1).

What Would Change the Regime:

  • A sustained rise in High Yield Option-Adjusted Spreads above 3.5%, particularly if the Credit Spreads Composite Z-score increases to 0.5 or above (3).
  • The Financial Stress Composite consistently rising above the 0.75 threshold, indicating a shift to "High Stress" (1).
  • The robust z-score of the STLFSI4 index consistently dropping below +0.5, signalling easing financial stress (2).
