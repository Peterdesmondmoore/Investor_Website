2. Why a Simple Taylor-Style Rule Is Insufficient

A mechanical, Taylor-style rule provides an inadequate framework for current monetary policy assessment under a flexible reaction function. Such rules falter because policy reaction coefficients are inherently time-varying, reflecting shifts in the central bank's objectives and the economic regime itself. The relative weights assigned to inflation versus labor market mandates frequently adjust, particularly in an environment characterized by supply-side shocks, structural shifts, and evolving expectations. Moreover, point estimates from these rules often fail to capture the critical role of risk asymmetry; policymakers must weigh the potential costs of under-tightening against over-tightening, a decision heavily influenced by prevailing tail risks rather than mere averages. Finally, uncertainty around key variables (e.g., the neutral rate r*) and the persistence of regimes matter more than any single, static equilibrium. A flexible reaction function thus prioritizes dynamic assessment of multiple, often conflicting, signals and forward-looking risks over rigid, backward-looking prescriptions.
