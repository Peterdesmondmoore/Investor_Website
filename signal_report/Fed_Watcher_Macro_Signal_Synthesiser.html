2. Why a Simple Taylor-Style Rule Is Insufficient

A simple, mechanical Taylor-style rule is insufficient for current policy assessment due to inherent limitations that become pronounced in the prevailing macroeconomic regime. Firstly, policy reaction coefficients for inflation and the output/employment gap are not static; they are demonstrably time-varying, reflecting shifts in structural economic relationships and the evolving policy framework. Relying on fixed coefficients risks miscalibration in a dynamic environment. Secondly, the relative weights assigned to the inflation mandate versus the full employment mandate shift by regime and by the nature of the shocks affecting the economy. In an environment of disinflationary pressures and emerging labour market slack, the emphasis may naturally gravitate towards employment stability, rather than solely reacting to inflation deviations around target. Thirdly, risk asymmetry significantly dominates point estimates; policymakers must consider the potential for adverse tail outcomes (e.g., persistent disinflation leading to a liquidity trap or a sharp downturn in employment) more heavily than symmetrical deviations around a central forecast. Finally, deep uncertainty surrounding key unobservables, such as the natural rate of interest (r*) and the natural rate of unemployment, and the persistence of current regimes, renders mechanical rules less reliable. Focus must instead be on the direction and persistence of broad macro signals and their confidence, rather than on simple averages or precise deviations from assumed equilibrium points.
