Current View: The institutional outlook for copper in 2026 remains generally constructive, though recent revisions indicate a slight softening in the target distribution. The most recent point forecasts from March 2026, led by BMO Economics at $12,125 per tonne, suggest a market that expects sustained price strength compared to historical averages. However, the median of the most recent institutional predictions has drifted lower to approximately $11,662, down from a prior median of $12,000, reflecting a cooling of the more aggressive price targets seen toward the end of last year.

Recent Predictions: Data from the first quarter of 2026 shows a tightening of expectations between $11,000 and $12,511 per tonne. Goldman Sachs has positioned itself at the lower end of this recent cluster, forecasting prices to decline from peak highs toward $11,000 by the end of 2026 due to potential global surpluses. In contrast, the CME Group’s survey-based midpoint of $12,511 illustrates that a significant portion of the market still anticipates a higher price ceiling, driven by the structural demand associated with the energy transition and data center expansion.

Historical Context: When compared to forecasts issued in late 2025, the current distribution shows a reduction in extreme bullishness. During November and December 2025, institutions like Citi and UBS were projecting targets as high as $13,000 per tonne for mid-to-late 2026. While these targets remain in the historical record, the absence of similarly high new targets in early 2026 suggests a shift toward a more cautious, though still historically elevated, price regime. Stale data from the World Bank and other industry bodies from late 2025, which sat below $10,000, now appears increasingly disconnected from the current institutional consensus.

Market Conviction: The outlook is currently characterized by moderate dispersion, with a notable gap between floor-oriented guidance and aggressive point forecasts. ING Research has established a "floor" conviction near $10,000 per tonne, while several major banks maintain base-case expectations at least 15-20% above that level. This spread highlights a lack of absolute consensus on the magnitude of the projected supply deficit, even as most institutions agree on the directional pressure created by mine disruptions and grid infrastructure needs.

Comparability and Constraints: Direct comparability across the dataset is somewhat limited by the variety of target types and horizons. The data includes a mix of annual averages, specific quarter-end point forecasts, and survey-based ranges. For instance, the CME Group’s figures represent a survey average of high and low scenarios, which may not carry the same conviction as a single institution's base-case point forecast. Furthermore, several prominent research houses, including CRU and Wood Mackenzie, have recently provided qualitative outlooks without providing specific numeric targets, further complicating a purely statistical aggregation of the market view.

Key Divergences: A clear divergence exists between analysts focusing on macroeconomic headwinds and those prioritizing micro-supply constraints. Predictions that lean toward the $11,000 range frequently cite tariff uncertainty and a post-tariff global surplus as primary cooling factors. Conversely, the more bullish forecasts toward $12,500 and above are predicated on "severe supply disruptions" and "constrained concentrate availability," suggesting that the ultimate price path for 2026 will depend heavily on whether supply-side shocks or trade-related demand destruction becomes the dominant market force.
