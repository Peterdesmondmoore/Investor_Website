Core Narrative

The outlook for the Metals asset class is strongly bullish over the next 6-12 months, driven by robust demand, persistent supply constraints, and supportive macroeconomic factors. "Most metals will be better supported by tighter fundamentals" (4), particularly those critical for the energy transition and advanced technologies. Demand for copper is projected to "swell copper demand to 42 million metric tons by 2040, a 50% increase from current levels," propelled by "accelerating electrification, the race for artificial intelligence (AI), and surging defense spending" (5)(6). Silver is also highlighted for its "pivotal role as a 'next generation metal' across industries critical to green energy transition and digital transformation" (7), with strong demand from solar photovoltaics, electric vehicles, and data centers (7). Gold is expected to "sustain its strong trend-like rally into 2026" (2) and "reach yet new heights" (4), supported by safe-haven demand, central bank accumulation, and anticipated Fed rate cuts (2)(4). Uranium is also in a "bull market setup strengthens into 2026" (8).

On the supply side, "multiple disruptions" (3) and long development timelines for new mines (5)(6) are creating deficits. For example, "refined copper balance for 2026 is now showing a deficit of around 600kt" (4), contributing to a projected "supply deficit of 10 million metric tons by 2040—25% below projected demand" (5). Geopolitical factors and "deglobalization" (8) further exacerbate supply challenges, positioning hard assets as "strategic levers in a multi-polar world" (8). While some metals like Nickel and Iron Ore face oversupply or bearish outlooks (4), the overarching picture for critical industrial and precious metals remains strongly positive (3)(4)(5)(6)(7)(8).

Key Drivers

Energy Transition Demand

Directional implication: supportive

Accelerating global energy transition initiatives, including widespread electrification, drive significant demand for critical metals. "Electric vehicles (EVs) require 2.9 times more copper than a conventional car" (6), with global EV production forecast to grow at a 13% CAGR between 2025 and 2031 (7). Solar PV capacity has grown over tenfold, leading to a threefold increase in silver demand (7). Record global investment in clean energy, projected to reach "USD 2.2 trillion for 2025" (1), underpins this demand.

AI and Data Center Expansion

Directional implication: supportive

The "rapid growth of the Artificial Intelligence (AI) industry" is estimated to require "between 300,000 and 500,000 additional tons of copper each year until 2030" (3). "Data Centres and Artificial Intelligence represent another critical growth area for silver," with "total global IT power capacity grew by an estimated 53x, from 0.93 GW in 2000 to nearly 50 GW in 2025" (7). AI-driven capital expenditure also contributes to the "Long Hard Asset Trade" (8).

Geopolitical Tensions & Defense Spending

Directional implication: supportive

"Geopolitical and technological factors have consolidated and strengthened" demand for copper, including "increased defense spending and strategic mineral protection plans" (3). Surging defense spending further adds to accelerating demand for copper (5)(6). The accelerating "Deglobalization" trend (8) alters global commodity flows and reinforces hard assets as "strategic levers in a multi-polar world" (8).

Supply Constraints and Disruptions

Directional implication: supportive

"Multiple disruptions" (3) and "accidents and cuts to production plans at mines" have led to estimated cuts of "around 400,000 tons in global production this year" for copper, with effects expected to "continue throughout 2026 and 2027" (3). Copper faces "significant mine supply disruptions" (4). Overall, the supply response is constrained by factors such as "declining ore grades, rising costs, and long development timelines" (6), leading to "tighter fundamentals" (4) and "structural supply deficits" (8).

Monetary Policy & US Dollar Weakness

Directional implication: supportive

Prospects for "looser monetary policy in the United States" (3) contribute to higher commodity prices, especially for precious metals. "Further Fed easing expected to remain the dominant driver" for a bearish bias on the USD (2). "Anticipated Fed rate cuts, a weaker US dollar" (4) are expected to keep gold "strong and reach yet new heights" (4), supporting its "Role as a Core Store of Value Strengthens" (8).

Key Risks

Supply Chain Concentration

The concentration of supply chains, with "six countries responsible for two-thirds of mining production and China dominating processing capacity, making global supply vulnerable to disruptions" (5). Specifically, "China accounts for 12 of the 29 million metric tons of global smelting capacity" (6), posing a systemic risk.

Long Development Timelines for New Mines

Overcoming projected shortfalls, particularly for copper, "requires significant investment in new mines and expansion of existing assets," but the sector faces challenges like environmental opposition and "lengthy permitting processes, taking an average of 17 years for a new mine to go from discovery to production" (5)(6). This long lead time makes a rapid supply response challenging.

Technological Thrifting in Silver

While overall demand for silver is strong, "technological 'thrifting' reduces silver per cell" in solar PV, and "copper plating technologies show promise in further reducing silver content" (7). If widely adopted, such innovations could partially offset the growth in industrial demand.

Oversupply in Specific Metals

Not all metals face a bullish outlook; for example, "Nickel is anticipated to remain in surplus throughout 2026, primarily due to continued supply growth from Indonesia" (4). Similarly, "Iron ore faces a bearish outlook for 2026, influenced by weakening Chinese demand, particularly in the property sector, and increasing seaborne supply" (4). These specific market dynamics could weigh on the overall metals sector sentiment.

Global Economic Slowdown

Slowing growth in key demand regions, such as "slowing growth in China" (2), could reduce overall industrial metals consumption. A general weakening in global economic activity could dampen the strong demand projected from traditional industrial sectors (4).

Disagreements & Uncertainties

Silver Demand from Solar PV: Thrifting vs. New Cell Technology

There is an ongoing dynamic between technological advancements that aim to reduce silver content per solar cell and the emergence of new cell technologies that require more silver, creating uncertainty about net silver demand in the solar photovoltaic sector.

  • Claim A: Technological "thrifting" reduces silver per cell, potentially limiting overall demand growth for silver in solar PV (7).
  • Claim B: New next-generation solar cells (TOPCon, SHJ) require more silver, potentially offsetting reductions from thrifting, though "copper plating technologies show promise in further reducing silver content" (7) in the future.
