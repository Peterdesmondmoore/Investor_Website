Core Narrative

Precious metals are expected to extend their record-breaking rally through 2026, supported by a combination of structural shifts in global finance and tightening physical fundamentals (4)(5)(11). Gold is increasingly viewed as a core store of value amidst fiscal dominance, currency debasement concerns, and a multi-polar deglobalization trend (8). While markets experienced a sharp technical reversal in early 2026 (12), the underlying drivers—sustained central bank diversification, a Federal Reserve easing cycle, and geopolitical unease—remain intact (2)(11). Silver is projected to potentially outperform gold due to its dual role as a monetary hedge and a critical industrial metal, facing its fifth consecutive year of supply deficit (6)(9)(11).

Key Drivers

Monetary Policy and US Dollar Weakness

Directional implication: supportive

Anticipated Fed rate cuts and a softening US Dollar (DXY) reduce the opportunity cost of holding non-yielding assets (1)(4). Lower rates are expected to maintain investor enthusiasm even if real rates remain relatively static due to sticky inflation (5).

Structural Central Bank Demand

Directional implication: supportive

Sovereign diversification away from US dollar holdings is a relevant structural factor, with central bank purchases averaging nearly 260 tonnes per quarter (5)(11). A majority of surveyed central bankers expect to increase gold reserves over the next five years (11).

Industrial Demand and Supply Deficits (Silver)

Directional implication: supportive

Silver faces persistent structural deficits driven by record industrial demand in solar photovoltaics, electric vehicles, and AI infrastructure (7)(11). Demand from these sectors is expected to outperform global GDP as above-ground inventories are eroded (9).

Key Risks

Rising Real Yields

If 10-year US Real Yields break significant resistance levels, they could act as a major headwind for gold prices (1). A divergence in the historical inverse correlation occurred in 2025, but yields remain a critical monitoring point for opportunity costs (11).

Institutional Money Rotation

Large investors may gradually rebalance portfolios from precious metals into base metals like copper, which is emerging as a strategic asset for the energy transition and AI infrastructure (10).

Technological Thrifting

In the solar sector, innovation in photovoltaic technologies aims to reduce silver consumption per watt by 15-20% by 2025, potentially dampening industrial demand growth (6)(7).

Signposts to Watch

Technical Resistance Breakout

Would confirm: A sustained move above the US$4,245/oz resistance level for gold would signal a completion of the current triangle continuation pattern and reinforce the core uptrend (1).

Would invalidate: A failure to hold recent support levels following the sharp 9-11% reversal in early 2026 would suggest a deeper correction (12).

Fed Leadership and Guidance

Would confirm: The appointment of a more dovish successor to the Fed Chair would likely drive the US dollar lower and support precious metals (1).

Would invalidate: A hawkish shift in guidance or evidence of "sticky" inflation that prevents anticipated rate cuts (5).

Scenarios

Sustained High-Level Consolidation

Gold averages between $4,200 and $4,325 through 2026, supported by steady central bank buying and moderate Fed easing, while silver averages near $50-$55 as industrial demand offsets inventory stockpiles (3)(4).

Implied stance: neutral

Key drivers: Monetary Policy; Structural Central Bank Demand

Stratospheric Breakout

Gold reaches $4,500/oz by Q4 2026, and silver surges to $62-$65/oz, fueled by acute geopolitical tensions, G20 fiscal sustainability fears, and an exponential rise in AI-driven silver demand (5)(6).

Implied stance: bullish

Key drivers: Industrial Demand and Supply Deficits (Silver); Fiscal Dominance

Real Yield and Rotation Correction

Prices retreat or remain rangebound as real yields break higher and institutional capital rotates into base metals like copper to capture energy transition gains (1)(10).

Implied stance: bearish

Key drivers: Rising Real Yields; Institutional Money Rotation

Disagreements & Uncertainties

Gold and Real Yield Correlation

There is uncertainty regarding the reliability of gold's historical inverse correlation with real yields, which appeared to break down in 2025 (11).

  • Rising real yields continue to be cited as a significant headwind (1).
  • Geopolitical hedging and sovereign diversification are now seen as outweighing traditional opportunity costs (11).

Silver Industrial Substitution

The impact of technological "thrifting" versus new demand in the solar sector (7).

  • Thrifting could reduce silver consumption per watt by 15-20% (6).
  • Next-generation solar cells (TOPCon, SHJ) require significantly more silver, potentially offsetting thrifting reductions (7).
