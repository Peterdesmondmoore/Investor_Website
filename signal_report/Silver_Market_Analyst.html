Silver Research Analyst

6–12 Month Stance

Stance: bullish

Confidence: high

Core Narrative

Precious metals, particularly gold and silver, are anticipated to extend their significant gains into 2026, following a record-breaking performance in 2025 where gold rallied over 60% and silver surged over 120% (5). Gold prices exceeded $4,300/oz and silver surpassed $60/oz by mid-December 2025 (5). The outlook for 2026 remains constructive, with forecasts for gold averaging around $4,200-$4,325/oz and reaching up to $4,500-$4,530/oz by Q3/Q4 2026 (3)(4)(5)(6). Silver is expected to outperform gold, with average forecasts of $50-$55/oz and projections as high as $62-$65/oz by Q3/Q4 2026 (3)(4)(5)(6). This bullish sentiment is driven by a combination of macroeconomic factors such as anticipated Fed rate cuts and a weaker US dollar, structural demand shifts including robust central bank gold purchases and increasing industrial silver demand, and ongoing geopolitical uncertainties (2)(4)(5)(6)(7)(8).

Key Drivers

Monetary Policy Easing and US Dollar Weakness

Directional implication: supportive

Expectations of lower rates from the Federal Reserve, an anticipated Fed easing cycle, and a softer US dollar are key macro tailwinds for precious metals (2)(4)(5)(6)(7). A potential 88% chance of a 25bps Fed rate cut this week and the influence of dovish Fed guidance or a more dovish successor to Chair Powell could prove influential, impacting yields, the dollar, and gold (1). A depreciating dollar and falling bond yields are expected to support gold (7).

Sustained Central Bank Gold Demand

Directional implication: supportive

Central banks have been substantial and consistent buyers of gold, with purchases averaging almost 260 tonnes per quarter since Q1 2022 (5). This demand is a significant structural factor in the global gold market, driven by strategies to diversify foreign exchange reserves, as most central bankers surveyed expect a higher share of gold reserves and lower U.S. dollar holdings in the next five years (4)(5)(6)(8)(11). This geopolitical hedging and sovereign diversification are now outweighing opportunity costs for gold (11).

Persistent Silver Supply Deficits

Directional implication: supportive

The silver market is facing its fifth consecutive year of market deficit, with a significant deficit of 117.6Moz forecast for 2025, and persistent deficits anticipated for 2026 (4)(6)(8)(9)(11). Mine production is projected to peak in 2026 and then decline, while industrial consumption continues to outpace mine supply, contributing to a drawdown in stock levels and physical tightness in key markets like London and Mumbai (6)(9)(11).

Strong Industrial Demand for Silver

Directional implication: supportive

Silver's dual role as a precious and industrial metal, coupled with increasing applications across industries critical to green energy transition and digital transformation, drives robust demand (2)(4)(6)(7)(8). Key sectors include solar photovoltaics, automotive and electric vehicles (especially battery electric vehicles, which consume significantly more silver), and data centers and artificial intelligence hardware (4)(5)(6)(7)(8)(9)(11). Industrial demand is anticipated to outperform global GDP (9).

Geopolitical Uncertainties and Fiscal Concerns

Directional implication: supportive

Ongoing geopolitical uncertainties, regional tensions, and broader unease over the fiscal sustainability of G20 economies contribute to a flight to safety, strengthening gold’s role as a haven and core store of value (1)(2)(4)(5)(6)(8)(9)(11). Deglobalization, where hard assets become strategic levers, and fiscal dominance leading to currency debasement also support demand (8).

Key Risks

Rising Real Yields and Technical Resistance for Gold

While declining FX opportunity costs supported gold in the past, rising yields acted as a headwind (1). If 10-year US Real Yields continue to edge higher and break significant resistance levels, they could exert pressure on gold prices (1). Technically, gold is "struggling to complete a 'triangle' continuation pattern," requiring a sustained move above US$4,245/oz to confirm its core uptrend (1).

Industrial Demand Volatility and Thrifting in Silver

Despite strong industrial demand, technological innovations in sectors like solar photovoltaics could reduce silver consumption per watt by 15-20% in 2025, and copper plating technologies show promise in further reducing silver content (6)(7)(9). While new next-gen solar cells may require more silver, this "thrifting" could limit demand growth in certain applications and contribute to a flat-lining of industrial fabrication (7)(9). Additionally, US subsidy cuts and trade tariffs in solar, or mixed government incentives and rising tariffs impacting EV affordability, could dampen overall industrial demand for silver (7).

Impact of Existing Silver Stockpiles

Even with significant forecast deficits for 2025, the price impact might be limited by "still sizable stockpiles of silver" (9). This suggests that while underlying fundamentals are tight, these inventories could buffer against immediate price surges.

Institutional Portfolio Rebalancing

There is evidence of "Institutional Money Rotation" where "large investors are gradually rebalancing portfolios from gold and silver into base metals like copper" (10). A significant shift in institutional capital away from precious metals could exert downward pressure on gold and silver prices.

Sharp Short-Term Price Reversals

Gold and silver markets have experienced sharp short-term reversals in the past, with gold dropping close to 9% and silver more than 11% from their peaks (12). Such historical volatility indicates that rapid price corrections remain a risk even within an overall bullish environment (12).

Signposts to Watch

Federal Reserve Policy Actions

Would confirm: Clear guidance towards an easing cycle, actual rate cuts, or signals of a more dovish stance from the Fed (1)(2)(4)(5)(6)(9).

Would invalidate: Delayed or fewer rate cuts than anticipated, or a more hawkish stance from the Fed, leading to sustained higher interest rates (1).

US Dollar Index (DXY) Movements and Real Yields

Would confirm: A continued downward trend in the US Dollar (DXY) and stabilization or decline in 10-year US Real Yields (1)(4)(5)(6).

Would invalidate: The US Dollar turning higher again, or 10-year US Real Yields continuing to edge higher and breaking significant resistance levels (1).

Central Bank Gold Purchasing Trends

Would confirm: Continued robust purchases of gold by central banks, maintaining or increasing their diversification of foreign exchange reserves (2)(4)(5)(6)(8)(11).

Would invalidate: A significant slowdown or cessation of central bank gold purchases (5)(6)(11).

Silver Supply-Demand Balance and Industrial Activity

Would confirm: Further evidence of persistent supply deficits, increased physical tightness in key markets, and sustained or accelerating industrial demand from solar PV, EVs, and AI sectors (4)(6)(7)(8)(9)(11).

Would invalidate: A reversal of the supply deficit into a surplus, significant impact from thrifting technologies reducing silver consumption, or a slowdown in key industrial sectors (6)(7)(9).

Disagreements & Uncertainties

Gold Price Trajectory

While most analyses predict a bullish trajectory for gold into 2026, one perspective suggests it could remain rangebound under certain conditions, even while acknowledging potential for surprises.

  • The outlook for gold into 2026 remains constructive, with prices expected to remain strong and reach new heights, averaging $4,325/oz and potentially up to $4,500/oz by Q4 2026 (4)(5).
  • Should current macro conditions persist, gold could remain rangebound, though 2026 may still surprise (1).

Silver Industrial Demand vs. Thrifting

There is a nuanced disagreement regarding the net impact of technological advancements (thrifting) versus new demand sources on silver's industrial consumption.

  • Industrial demand for silver is expected to rise with easing monetary policy and greater economic activity, with new next-gen solar cells requiring more silver, potentially offsetting reductions from thrifting (6)(7). Consistent offtake from photovoltaics and electrification sectors is contributing to stock drawdown (11).
  • Innovation in photovoltaic (PV) technologies could reduce silver consumption per watt by 15-20% in 2025, and industrial fabrication is forecast to flat-line due to thrifting in PV and slower ethylene oxide capacity growth (6)(9).
