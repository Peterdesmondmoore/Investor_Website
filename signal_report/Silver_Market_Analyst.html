Silver Research Analyst

6-12 Month Stance

Stance: bullish

Confidence: high

Core Narrative

The outlook for precious metals through 2026 remains structurally bullish, with gold and silver expected to reach new historical heights (4)(5). Despite a sharp reversal in early 2026 that saw gold drop nearly 9% and silver over 11% from recent peaks (12), the sector is supported by a confluence of macroeconomic and fundamental drivers. Key support stems from an anticipated Federal Reserve easing cycle, sustained central bank diversification away from fiat currencies, and persistent fiscal sustainability concerns in G20 economies (2)(5)(8). Silver is projected to potentially outperform gold, driven by a fifth consecutive year of market deficit and surging industrial demand from the AI, solar, and electric vehicle sectors (7)(11). While rising real yields and technical resistance at US$4,245/oz present near-term headwinds, the "debasement trade" continues to favor hard assets as a core store of value in a multi-polar world (1)(8)(11).

Key Drivers

Central Bank Accumulation

Directional implication: supportive

Central banks have become relevant structural buyers, averaging nearly 260 tonnes of gold purchases per quarter (5). Surveys indicate a long-term strategy to increase gold reserves while reducing U.S. dollar holdings over the next five years (11).

Monetary Policy and Real Yields

Directional implication: supportive

Expectations of Federal Reserve rate cuts and a softening US dollar are expected to maintain investor enthusiasm and lower the opportunity cost for holding non-yielding precious metals (2)(5).

Structural Industrial Deficits (Silver)

Directional implication: supportive

Silver is facing its fifth consecutive year of market deficit as industrial consumption, particularly in photovoltaics and AI-related hardware, outpaces mine supply (11). Mine production is expected to peak in 2026 before entering a decline (9).

Key Risks

Rising Real Yields

The 10-year US Real Yield continues to edge higher; a breach of significant resistance levels could act as a material headwind for gold prices (1).

Institutional Asset Rotation

Large investors may gradually rebalance portfolios away from precious metals and into base metals like copper to capture growth in electric infrastructure and high-tech sectors (10).

Technological Thrifting

Innovation in solar technologies could reduce silver consumption per watt by 15–20% in the near term, potentially softening the impact of increased installation capacity (6).

Signposts to Watch

Gold Resistance Threshold

Would confirm: A sustained move above US$4,245/oz would complete a triangle continuation pattern, confirming the core uptrend for gold (1).

Would invalidate: Failure to break resistance, combined with the US Dollar turning higher, would suggest gold remains rangebound (1).

Silver Inventory Levels

Would confirm: Continued drawdowns in London and Mumbai vault stock levels would validate the physical market tightness and support a move toward $60-$65/oz (6)(11).

Would invalidate: A build-up in above-ground stockpiles or a slowdown in industrial fabrication would limit price appreciation despite the structural deficit (9).

Scenarios

Sustained Constructive Growth

Gold averages between $4,325/oz and $4,500/oz by Q4 2026, supported by steady Fed easing and consistent central bank demand (4)(5). Silver follows with an average price of $50-$55/oz as industrial demand remains firm (3)(4).

Implied stance: bullish

Key drivers: Structural Industrial Deficits (Silver); Silver Industrial Demand

Rangebound Macro Stability

Current macro conditions persist without further geopolitical escalation, keeping gold rangebound as rising real yields and slowing ETF inflows offset FX-related support (1).

Implied stance: neutral

Key drivers: Rising Real Yields; Monetary Policy and Real Yields

Disagreements & Uncertainties

Gold's Correlation with Real Yields

There is a documented divergence in how gold responds to interest rate movements compared to historical norms (11).

  • Rising real yields continue to act as a primary headwind and opportunity cost for gold (1).
  • Geopolitical hedging and sovereign diversification have begun to outweigh traditional yield-based opportunity costs (11).
