Core Narrative

Precious metals are expected to maintain their upward momentum through 2026, building on a record-breaking 2025 performance (1)(2)(3). Gold is forecasted to average between $4,200 and $4,500 per ounce (2)(4)(3), while silver is projected to outperform gold due to persistent supply deficits and increasing industrial demand, with price targets reaching $55 to $65 per ounce (4)(3)(5). The sector benefits from a structural shift toward hard assets as a hedge against currency debasement and fiscal instability in G20 economies (6). While the market experienced sharp short-term reversals in early 2026 (7), the long-term outlook remains constructive, supported by central bank diversification and the start of a global interest rate easing cycle (1)(8).

Key Drivers

Monetary Policy Easing

Directional implication: supportive

The Federal Reserve's easing cycle and the anticipation of lower interest rates are expected to maintain investor enthusiasm by reducing the opportunity cost of holding non-yielding assets (3)(8). Even with sticky inflation, lower nominal rates are seen as a supportive driver for gold and silver prices (1)(3).

Structural Industrial Demand (Silver)

Directional implication: supportive

Silver demand is being propelled by its critical role in the green energy transition and digital transformation. Solar photovoltaics, electric vehicle production (which consumes 67-79% more silver than internal combustion engines), and AI-related hardware such as data center servers are driving record industrial consumption (9)(3)(10).

Central Bank and Sovereign Diversification

Directional implication: supportive

Central banks have become relevant structural buyers, diversifying foreign exchange reserves away from the U.S. dollar (8). Geopolitical hedging and sovereign diversification are increasingly outweighing traditional opportunity costs like real yields (11).

Key Risks

Rising Real Yields and Dollar Strength

Continued upward movement in 10-year US real yields acts as a technical headwind for gold (11). A recovery in the US Dollar (DXY) could also pressure precious metals prices, as seen in historical inverse correlations, despite recent divergences (11)(1).

Institutional Asset Rotation

There is evidence of large investors rebalancing portfolios away from gold and silver into base metals like copper to capture gains from tightening supply-demand balances in industrial materials (12).

Technological Thrifting

Innovation in photovoltaic technologies could reduce silver consumption per watt by 15-20%, potentially mitigating the impact of increased solar installation capacity (5)(10).

Signposts to Watch

Technical Resistance Levels

Would confirm: A sustained move above resistance at $4,245/oz would confirm the completion of a continuation pattern for Gold's core uptrend (11).

Would invalidate: A failure to hold recent support levels following sharp reversals could indicate a deeper correction toward historical averages (7).

London Vault Inventory Levels

Would confirm: Further drawdowns in London and Mumbai silver stockpiles would confirm physical tightness and justify higher price premiums (5)(10).

Would invalidate: Stabilization or growth in silver inventories would suggest that above-ground stocks are sufficient to absorb current deficits (10).

Disagreements & Uncertainties

Yield-Price Correlation

Analysts disagree on whether gold's historical inverse correlation with real yields still holds (8)(11).

  • Rising real yields remain a primary headwind that could cap gold's gains (11).
  • Geopolitical hedging and sovereign diversification have decoupled gold from yields, making them less influential (8).

Silver Supply Sufficiency

There is uncertainty regarding the impact of multi-year silver deficits on market prices (10).

  • Persistent deficits will eventually erode above-ground inventories and force a price spike (10).
  • Sizable existing stockpiles of silver may limit the immediate price impact of these deficits (10).
