Recent window is mostly direct institution forecasts.

Current View: The institutional outlook for copper prices in 2026 remains broadly constructive, though recent forecasts suggest a slight softening of the most aggressive "record high" expectations seen late last year. While major banks and industry surveys continue to position the metal well above historical norms, the distribution of price targets has moved toward a more consolidated range as analysts weigh structural deficits against emerging macroeconomic headwinds such as tariff uncertainty and potential global surpluses.

Recent Predictions: Data from the first quarter of 2026 indicates a clustering of targets between USD 11,000 and USD 12,500 per tonne. BMO Economics recently established an annual average forecast of USD 12,125, while a February survey by the CME Group produced a midpoint target of USD 12,511. These figures suggest that while the conviction for double-digit pricing remains high, there is a distinct move away from the hyper-bullish sentiment that characterized the end of 2025.

Historical Context: Late 2025 forecasts from institutions like Citi and UBS were significantly more optimistic, with both firms projecting peaks of USD 13,000 per tonne by mid-to-late 2026. Goldman Sachs, however, has recently moderated this enthusiasm, lowering its end-2026 projection to USD 11,000. This downward shift in the distribution reflects a growing consensus that while supply disruptions and energy transition demand provide a solid floor, high prices may eventually trigger increased scrap supply and weigh on spot demand.

Comparability and Conviction: The current dataset presents challenges for direct comparison due to varying methodologies. Recent data includes point-in-time forecasts for specific quarters alongside annual averages and survey-based ranges. For instance, the CME Group’s wide survey range (USD 11,023 to USD 13,999) underscores a high degree of uncertainty in the market. Consequently, the outlook should be viewed as dispersed rather than unified, with institutional conviction split between those prioritizing severe mine-side deficits and those focused on broader industrial slowdowns.

Supply and Demand Drivers: Across all reporting institutions, the primary catalysts for upward price pressure remain mine-level supply disruptions and structural demand from grid infrastructure, AI data centers, and the energy transition. Conversely, the World Bank maintains a more conservative floor of USD 9,800, citing weak industrial activity as a potential drag. The prevailing sentiment suggests an upside bias persists, but it is increasingly tempered by the timing of tariff decisions and the realized delivery of new mining projects.

Synthesis: Overall, the institutional landscape for copper is characterized by high-plateau expectations. While the median outlook has softened slightly from late-2025 peaks, the floor for the metal appears to have structurally shifted higher. Investors face a market where the central tendency sits near USD 11,500 to USD 12,000, but one that remains highly sensitive to supply-side shocks and shifting trade policies that could easily push pricing toward either extreme of the current forecast range.
