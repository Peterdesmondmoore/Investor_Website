Federal Reserve Policy Outlook Report

Dominant Macro Regime

The prevailing macro regime is characterized by a gradual moderation in economic activity and persistent, albeit easing, inflationary pressures. The labor market is exhibiting signs of softening, with rising slack and reduced dynamism, though not yet a sharp deterioration. Financial conditions are net accommodative due to Federal Reserve liquidity operations, while credit conditions remain generally benign. Global growth dynamics are mixed, showing industrial tailwinds in some areas alongside a weakening U.S. dollar and defensive positioning in certain cross-asset markets, indicating a nuanced late-cycle environment with two-sided risks.

Policy Decision Matrix

The Federal Reserve faces a challenging environment with elevated, yet moderating, inflation alongside increasing downside risks to employment. The current policy stance reflects a shift towards a more neutral position, balancing the dual mandate amidst mixed economic signals and a desire to avoid an unnecessary slowdown.

Decision Probability Macro Justification
CUT 60% Continued moderation in inflation, further softening in the labor market with rising unemployment and persistent underemployment, and a weakening in overall economic activity would compel a further reduction in the policy rate. The balance of risks, increasingly tilted towards employment, supports additional easing to prevent a sharper downturn.
HOLD 35% If economic activity stabilizes at a moderate pace, inflation plateaus above the 2% target (especially due to tariffs), and labor market softening becomes less pronounced or shows signs of stabilization, the Committee may opt to pause to assess the cumulative effects of past adjustments and gather more complete data.
HIKE 5% A significant re-acceleration of core inflation beyond current levels, particularly if services inflation proves stickier or tariff-induced price increases become more persistent and broader, combined with unexpected resilience or re-tightening in the labor market, would necessitate a more restrictive stance. This scenario currently appears less probable given prevailing trends.

Upside & Downside Risks

The balance of risks remains two-sided, with persistent inflationary concerns coexisting with growing downside risks to employment and economic activity.

  • Upside risk: A more persistent pass-through of tariff-related costs into broader consumer prices and/or an unexpected rebound in labor demand, especially in cyclically sensitive sectors, could force a more hawkish stance.
  • Downside risk: A sharper-than-anticipated deterioration in the labor market, marked by increasing layoffs and a rapid rise in the unemployment rate, or a more significant global economic slowdown, would force a more dovish stance.

Data That Would Strengthen the Call

To increase confidence in the base case of further easing, clear signals of a continued rebalancing of the economy are crucial, particularly in the labor market and inflation metrics.

  • A sustained deceleration in core PCE inflation, moving closer to the 2% target, especially in non-housing services, would confirm disinflationary momentum.
  • A notable increase in the unemployment rate (e.g., above 4.5%) coupled with a significant rise in initial jobless claims and declining wage growth, signalling substantial labor market slack.
  • Further declines in business investment outside of the AI/tech sector and weaker consumer spending indicators, reinforcing the moderation in overall economic activity.

Inflation & Labour Assessment

The inflation regime is predominantly cooling, with the overall US Inflation Signal in a "COOL" state. CPI and PCE measures are moderating, with PCE showing moderate, sustained pressure below its long-term robust median. Market-implied inflation expectations are stable but exhibit a notable downside bias in short-term momentum. The labor market is characterized by increasing slack and moderating tightness, with the unemployment rate rising and underemployment stress near historical highs. Wage growth is modest to moderate and easing, reducing cost-push pressures. While headline employment figures are neutral, underlying trends indicate reduced dynamism and weakening momentum, suggesting a persistent unwinding of labor market tightness.

  • Inflation regime: Cooling, with moderating PCE and core PCE, stable long-term inflation expectations, but persistent tariff-related cost pressures in goods.
  • Labour market: Neutral with underlying softening, characterized by increasing slack, rising unemployment, elevated underemployment stress, and easing wage growth dynamics.
  • Policy bias: Conditions increasingly support easing, driven by the moderation in inflation and the growing downside risks to employment.

Growth & Liquidity Context

The US economy is in a late-cycle phase with signs of growth moderation. Business investment outside of core manufacturing, particularly durable goods excluding transportation, shows mixed signals. Industrial production remains neutral, with modest growth primarily supported by the mining sector. The yield curve against the federal funds rate is inverted or flat, indicating restrictive monetary policy, while the overall yield curve slope is flat/neutral, suggesting balanced long-term expectations. Monetary conditions are net accommodative due to a significant unwinding of the Overnight Reverse Repo facility, which provides substantial liquidity, partially offsetting the ongoing contraction of the Fed's balance sheet and a neutral stance in M2 money supply and real interest rates.

  • Business cycle: Late-cycle with moderating overall activity, pockets of strength in core manufacturing, but underlying caution in business investment and broad industrial activity.
  • Liquidity & financial conditions: Net accommodative due to strong ON RRP unwinding, but systemic reserves are still declining; real interest rates are neutral.
  • Yield curve implications: The inverted/flat curve relative to the policy rate suggests ongoing monetary policy restrictiveness, which may dampen future growth and credit demand, while the neutral composite slope reflects stable long-term expectations.

Credit & Financial Stability

Credit conditions in the US are generally benign and neutral across most key indicators. Corporate funding costs, as reflected by credit spreads, are historically tight, but high-yield spreads show a slight upward momentum from extreme lows. The financial stress composite is neutral, with systemic stress within typical historical ranges, although one underlying component (STLFSI4) indicates elevated tension. The overall credit conditions index has normalized from an easing phase to a more balanced state. Private credit impulse is stable, driven by total bank credit, but data incompleteness for specific components introduces some uncertainty regarding the full picture of credit growth dynamics.

  • Credit conditions: Generally benign and neutral, with historically tight spreads but some upward momentum in high-yield, and a normalization of overall funding conditions.
  • Financial stress: Neutral, with systemic stress contained within historical ranges, although one underlying component points to elevated tension.
  • Constraint on policy: Current credit conditions do not present an acute financial stability constraint necessitating immediate policy intervention. However, monitoring potential normalization or widening of spreads from tight levels, and rising consumer loan delinquencies, remains important.

Global & Sentiment Overlay

Global economic conditions present a mixed picture. While Q2 2025 data showed strong industrial demand (Metals Tailwind), more recent futures positioning (October 2025) reveals extreme speculative short crowding in gold and silver, signaling defensive sentiment and high tactical reversal risk in safe-haven assets. This suggests underlying caution despite earlier industrial resilience. The U.S. dollar is in a "Weakening" regime, easing global financial conditions, which tends to put upward pressure on global commodity prices and reduce FX-related stress for non-US entities. Cross-asset positioning indicates normalizing volatility but concentrated crowding in specific sectors (e.g., metals long, FX/agriculture short), with a "Supply Stress" macro theme emerging from commercial hedger activity in commodities. Overall investor risk appetite appears to be normalizing from recent stress but remains discerning and sensitive to sector-specific dynamics.

  • External inflation forces: Upward pressure from a weakening USD, which tends to boost commodity prices and increase import costs for non-USD economies, though tempered by a generally cooling domestic inflation environment.
  • FX and spillovers: A weakening USD is easing global financial conditions, reducing external debt burdens for dollar-denominated borrowers, and potentially encouraging capital flows towards non-US and emerging markets.
  • Risk appetite & positioning: Normalizing volatility from recent highs, but with concentrated speculative positioning in commodities (defensive in precious metals, supply-driven in energy/agriculture), indicating discerning risk-taking and sensitivity to sector-specific fundamentals rather than broad systemic fragility.

Interpretation of Loaded Fed Communications

The loaded Fed documents, specifically the September 16-17 and October 28-29, 2025 FOMC Minutes, the September 17 and October 29, 2025 Chair Powell's Press Conference Transcripts, and the October and November 2025 Beige Books, collectively paint a consistent picture of policy easing in response to a shifting balance of risks. The Committee has acted to lower the federal funds rate by 1/4 percentage point at both the September and October meetings, acknowledging a moderation in economic activity and a softening labor market. Notably, the decision to conclude the reduction of the aggregate securities holdings as of December 1, as announced in October, signals a definitive end to quantitative tightening, further contributing to an accommodative monetary stance and addressing tightening money market conditions.

  • Key communications: The latest FOMC minutes and Chair Powell's press conference transcripts from September and October 2025 are most relevant. They communicate a consensus for rate cuts due to rising employment risks and a conclusion to balance sheet runoff. The Beige Books provide qualitative district-level validation of moderating activity, labor market softening, and persistent, albeit easing, inflation pressures.
  • Continuity vs change: These communications represent a significant shift from a previously hawkish bias to a more dovish stance, emphasizing a "risk-management" approach to prevent an unnecessary economic slowdown. The decisions to cut rates in consecutive meetings and to halt balance sheet runoff reflect a proactive move towards a more neutral policy stance, responding to the increased downside risks to employment.
  • Alignment with macro data: The Fed's communications align well with the macro themes, which show a cooling inflation regime, a neutral but softening labor market with elevated slack, and moderating overall growth. The easing liquidity conditions driven by the ON RRP unwind also resonate with the Fed's decision to stop quantitative tightening, indicating an awareness of money market dynamics and reserve levels.
