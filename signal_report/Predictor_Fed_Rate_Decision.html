Fed Policy Advisor – Rate Decision Outlook (Cut / Hold / Hike Probabilities)

Executive Summary

The macroeconomic regime as of late 2025 is characterized by a resilient but decelerating US economy, marked by a cooling labor market, slowing consumer demand, and rising downside risks to employment. Inflation remains "somewhat elevated," largely driven by tariff-induced goods prices, which the Federal Reserve views as likely a "one-time shift" rather than persistent inflationary pressure. Financial conditions show a mix of easing liquidity, neutral real rates, and historically tight but slightly widening credit spreads, with rising consumer delinquencies. The Fed's communication tone is consistently dovish, having implemented 75 basis points of cuts across the last three meetings (September, October, December 2025) and initiating purchases of shorter-term Treasury securities to maintain ample reserves. While Chair Powell noted the Committee is "well positioned to wait and see" after the December cut, the Summary of Economic Projections (SEP) for 2026 continues to project lower federal funds rates. This, coupled with the Fed's proactive, forward-looking posture and explicit prioritization of downside employment risks, suggests a continued easing bias for the next policy decision.

Current Macro Regime & Policy Backdrop Assessment

US Inflation & Price Dynamics

The US inflation environment is in a "Neutral" regime with underlying disinflationary momentum, despite policy-relevant PCE inflation remaining above the 2% target. The Fed Inflation Signal indicates a "COOL" regime, suggesting disinflation/deflation risk, though its z-score is near neutral [Source: fed_inflation_signal]. CPI-PPI dynamics are "NEUTRAL," with a positive divergence suggesting mild cost-push pressure from producers but not at extreme levels [Source: CPI_PPI_Divergence.html]. Market-implied inflation expectations are "STABLE," with negative z-scores for short-term changes hinting at disinflationary momentum [Source: market_implied_inflation_signal]. The Inflation Term-Structure is also "Neutral," indicating balanced pricing across horizons with below-median inflation expectations [Source: inflation_term_structure]. Wage growth and labor market tightness are "NEUTRAL," suggesting easing cost-push pressures after cooling earlier in the year [Source: wage_growth_and_inflation.html].

Fed commentary consistently notes inflation "remains somewhat elevated" but emphasizes that goods inflation, driven by tariffs, is likely a "one-time shift" [Source: press_monetary_policy]. This perspective downweights current inflation readings as a barrier to further easing, particularly in light of more pressing employment concerns.

Underemployment risk is "NEUTRAL" but at historically high stress levels, and momentum is increasing towards slack [Source: underemployment_risk]. Labor market tightness (JOLTS) is "NEUTRAL" but easing, while turnover is "Bearish," signaling diminishing worker confidence and waning dynamism [Source: labor_market_tightness] [Source: Labor_Market_Turnover.html]. Chair Powell consistently highlights "downside risks to employment" as having "risen" [Source: press_monetary_policy].

Fed Communication / Reaction-Function Channel

Recent Fed communications, particularly from Chair Powell's press conferences and FOMC statements (September, October, December 2025), consistently emphasize a shift in the balance of risks towards employment downsides. Following three consecutive 25 basis point rate cuts (totaling 75 bps this year), the federal funds rate target range now stands at 3-1/2 to 3-3/4 percent [Source: press_monetary_policy]. The December 2025 FOMC also initiated purchases of shorter-term Treasury securities to maintain ample reserves, signaling further easing on the liquidity front [Source: press_monetary_policy].

Chair Powell stated in December that policy is now "within a broad range of estimates of its neutral value," and the Committee is "well positioned to wait and see how the economy evolves from here" before making "additional adjustments" [Source: press_monetary_policy]. Despite this "wait and see" language, the median SEP projections for 2026 and 2027 show further reductions in the federal funds rate (3.4% and 3.1% respectively) [Source: press_monetary_policy]. This suggests that while an immediate pause is a possibility, the overall policy trajectory remains geared towards easing to mitigate persistent downside risks to employment and support the economy's return to 2% inflation once tariff effects dissipate. The internal divergence within the Committee, particularly regarding the future path of rate cuts, remains notable [Source: press_monetary_policy].

Probabilistic Assessment of NEXT FOMC Policy Decision (January 25/26, 2026)

Given the sustained dovish bias, the explicit increase in downside risks to employment, and the forward-looking posture of the Federal Reserve in a late-cycle environment, the balance of evidence points towards a continued easing, although the "wait and see" language introduced in December injects some probability for a pause. The median SEP projection for 2026 explicitly implies further rate cuts within the year. The continued softening in the labor market and demand, as observed in the latest Beige Book, reinforces the need for further monetary support if the Fed is to remain proactive in addressing employment risks.
